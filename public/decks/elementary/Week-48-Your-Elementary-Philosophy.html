Residency · Week Forty-Eight · Elementary

You cannot teach in a way that is different from what you believe.
You can pretend for a while.
But eventually who you are comes through.

Montessori Makers Residency Week 48 · Elementary · Month 12 · Behaviour, Community, Capstone
Why this matters right now

You are at the end of the academic phase. In a few months, theory meets a real classroom, real families, real community.

If you have not gotten clear about what you believe, you will make decisions based on what feels comfortable, what you learned growing up, or what the system asks of you.

And you will teach in a way that is not aligned with what you actually care about.

02
What a philosophy actually is

A philosophy is not a statement you write for an interview. It is what you actually believe about how the world works and what your role is.

What you believe about children. About learning. About justice. About your role.

You will teach from what you actually believe. Not from what you say you believe.

03
Three questions that will drive every decision
01 · Children

Are they capable? Trustworthy? Good?

Do you believe wealthy children are naturally smarter? That poor children are damaged? That neurodivergent children are broken? That children of colour are more aggressive?

These beliefs are absorbed from the culture. If you do not interrogate them, they will drive your teaching.

02 · Learning

Through instruction, or discovery? One way, or many?

Is learning something that happens to children — or something children do?

Your answer will shape how you structure your classroom.

03 · Justice

Treat everyone the same — or meet each person where they are?

Do you believe systems are just? Or built on inequality?

Your answer will shape how you respond to inequality in your classroom.

04
Interrogating belief one · capacity

Do you believe all children are capable learners? Or that some are naturally smart and some are not?

If intelligence is fixed

You will give up on children who struggle. You will stop pushing children who are succeeding — they are “already smart.”

A sort happens, silently, all year.

If intelligence is developed

You will push all children. You will believe all can learn if they are supported.

Every child is in formation. None are finished.

05
Interrogating belief two · goodness

Is behaviour a child trying to communicate something, or a moral failing?

If behaviour is communication

You respond with curiosity and support.

Something is not working. Let’s find it.

If behaviour is a moral failing

You respond with punishment.

And children will know — immediately — which one you believe.

06
Interrogating belief three · difference

Are children from marginalised communities “just like” children from privileged communities?

Colourblind teaching

Pretends difference does not exist.

Refuses to see what the child is actually carrying.

Equity-centred teaching

Understands that different experiences, traumas, and needs are real.

You cannot teach in an equity-centred way if you do not believe the differences exist.

07
Integrating Montessori with equity · “follow the child”

Following the child means following her into her full humanity. Her context. Her culture. Her community. Her identity.

Not following her while pretending that her identity does not matter.

Her behaviour makes sense given her experiences. Her learning is shaped by her culture, her community, her people.

08
“The child is good and capable” — and the world tells her otherwise

The child is growing up in a world that tells her — through a thousand messages — that she is not good, not capable, that her people are not good, not capable.

If you believe the child is good and capable, you have to actively work against the messages that say she is not.

Centre her culture in the curriculum. Centre her history. Be intentional about the systems working against her.

09
Freedom is not enough

Freedom without justice is just a different kind of oppression.

If you emphasise freedom without addressing the systems that constrain some children’s freedom, you are teaching a lie.

You are teaching that all children have the same freedom when some children have been historically denied it.

10
Classroom reality · the child making errors in her math

The moment is the same. Three beliefs — three teachers — three different classrooms.

If you believe

Children learn by being shown the right way.

You intervene. You correct her. You tell her the way.

If you believe

Children learn by figuring things out.

You leave her alone.

If you believe

Children learn through guided exploration.

You sit with her. You ask a question that helps her notice.

11
Same child. Two teachers. Two classrooms.

A child is yelling. She might hit. What you do flows from what you believe about the child.

If the child is being bad

You punish. You remove. You wait for her to come back compliant.

The belief becomes the child’s experience of herself.

If her nervous system is overwhelmed

You help her regulate. You sit. You stay.

The belief becomes the child’s experience of herself.

12
What history will you teach?

What you do flows from what you believe about whose history matters — whose story gets to be told — what children need to know about the world and about themselves.

Dead white men. All cultures, flattened. Or centering the communities who have been marginalised.

This choice is also a philosophy. You are making it whether you know it or not.

13
Equity provocation · your philosophy is not neutral

Teaching Montessori without an explicit equity and justice framework is a choice to let the dominant culture shape your teaching.

You do not get to opt out. You only get to choose whether you are conscious about it or not.

14
Neurodivergence note · the belief shapes every decision

Do neurodivergent children need to become neurotypical? Or are their brains different, valuable, and in need of support?

This belief will shape every decision you make with a neurodivergent child.

15
What you should be able to articulate

Say it in plain language. To families. To other teachers. To yourself when you are doubting.

What you believe about children — and why. What you believe about learning — and why. What you believe your role is. How Montessori shapes you. How equity and justice shape you.

Your philosophy will change. You need to have something to start with.

17
Closing question

For your own formation.

What do you believe about children that is most different from how you were treated as a child?

What would it take for you to actually teach that belief — especially when it is hard?
