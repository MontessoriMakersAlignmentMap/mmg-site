Montessori Makers Residency Week 5 · Elementary
Why this matters right now

You are about to tell
children stories about
the formation of the
earth, the evolution
of life, the coming of
human beings.

Tell them as dry facts and you will activate the parts of children’s minds that memorize and file away. You will not activate the parts that ask deep questions, make connections, generate curiosity.

The Great Lessons are designed to use imagination. If you do not understand imagination as a cognitive tool, you will teach these lessons in a way that contradicts their purpose.

02
What the second-plane mind can and cannot do

The reasoning mind of an
elementary child cannot yet
work with pure abstraction. It thinks in pictures first.

Without imagination

“Billions of years.”

A phrase on a page. Three words. A child repeats them but cannot hold them.

With imagination

A river a child can walk along.

Picture the earth forming. Picture life appearing, gradually complicating. Now the child has something to think with.

What comes after

Real abstraction.

Later, the child can work with the number line, the timeline, the taxonomy. But the picture has to come first.

This is not a developmental weakness. It is how the mind of a six-to-twelve-year-old actually does its work.

03
The crucial distinction · imagination is not fantasy

Imagination in the sense Montessori means is imagination grounded in reality.

Fantasy

Imagination untethered from reality.

A child fantasizes about being a unicorn.

Fine for play

Not a cognitive tool for learning about the real world.

Imagination

Imagination grounded in reality.

The ability to hold a mental picture of something real you cannot currently see.

The ability to walk through a sequence of events in your mind and see how one thing leads to another.

The ability to take what you know about one situation and apply it to understand another.

The cognitive tool

This is how a six-year-old thinks about billions of years. There is no other way.

04
The claim, in one sentence

Imagination is not
decoration. It is not
something the child
does after they
understand. It is
the tool that lets
them understand.

05
What imagination actually does · four functions, one mind

Imagination is not separate from intellectual work. It is what makes the work possible.

i.
Imagination holds the unseeable. Billions of years, pre-human earth, the interior of a cell. Without imagination, these are words on a page. With it, they become pictures a child thinks with.
ii.
Imagination drives investigation. A child goes to books with a question she is already imagining the answer to. She reads to confirm or refine her mental picture. She conducts experiments to test what she imagines.
iii.
Imagination becomes moral. The child walks in someone else’s shoes. A historical figure facing injustice, an indigenous community facing colonization — imagination is the tool through which moral understanding develops.
iv.
Imagination drives creation. Drawings, models, stories from the perspective of a person in an ancient civilization. These are not decoration. They are thinking made visible.
06
How the Great Lessons are built to invite imagination

A Great Lesson is not a lecture. It is a shaped invitation to picture.

i · frame

Gather. Lower the voice. Slow the pace.

Signal that this is different from daily work.

ii · invite

“Picture the earth as it forms.”

Explicit verbal cues that imagination is the work.

iii · anchor

Globes. Timelines. Pictures. Demonstrations.

Sensorial objects as anchors for the unseeable.

iv · hold

Pause. Let the silence work.

Do not interrupt the child’s picturing with questions.

v · release

Let investigation begin from the child.

Books, experiments, drawings follow — they do not precede.

07
A guide at the front of a gathered group of elementary children. She is telling the story of the coming of animals, speaking slowly, her voice carrying scale and wonder. A timeline unfurls on the floor. A child’s eyes are far away, picturing. The room is quiet. She has just paused. She does not fill the silence.
Scene A · imagination activated

She gives children
time to picture.

“Imagine the oceans filling the earth. Imagine the simplest forms of life appearing. Imagine them gradually becoming more complex.” She does not rush. She does not interrupt herself to ask questions. After she finishes — she is quiet.

A week later, a child is still thinking about this lesson. Still reading. Still drawing. Still imagining.

08
A guide at a whiteboard listing the main groups of animals. Bullet points. A stack of worksheets in her hand. Children sitting at tables. One is answering “did you understand?” and nodding. Another child’s eyes are on the window. The timeline is tidy and contained. There is no time given for silence or picturing.
Scene B · imagination bypassed

Same lesson.
No imagining.

“Animals first appeared in the oceans. Then they came to land. Here are the main groups.” She lists. Shows pictures. Asks comprehension questions. Did you understand? Can you tell me back what I said?

A week later — a worksheet. Children fill it in. They have learned facts. They have not thought.

09
A week after the Great Lesson. A child at a low table surrounded by books — dinosaurs, deep-sea creatures, a field guide to rocks. She is drawing picture after picture of what she imagines early life looked like. Papers spread in the sequence she is thinking in. The guide has supplied the materials and sat nearby. She does not correct the inaccuracies. She asks: what are you wondering about? They talk about extinctions. The guide suggests a book. The child reads it, still imagining, still creating.
Scene C · imagination as engine

The picture
drives the
reading.

She reads with a question she is already imagining the answer to. She is not receiving information. She is refining a mental picture.

The book becomes a tool the imagination uses — not a substitute for it.

10
Imagination becomes moral at the second plane

The child begins to
understand herself
as part of a story.
Imagination becomes
the tool of empathy.

Reading about a historical figure who faced an injustice — imagination is how the child feels what was at stake, why that person made the choices they did.

Learning how colonization affected indigenous peoples — imagination is how the child understands what was lost, what was violated, what resilience looked like.

Moral understanding does not arrive through a lecture on ethics. It arrives because a child has imagined her way into another life.

11
How well-meaning guides shut imagination down

Being too literal. Too focused on facts. Too quick to correct.

  • × Worksheets that ask children to memorize facts about ancient Egypt rather than inviting them to imagine living there.
  • × Treating imagination as frivolous. Something children do when they should be working.
  • × Stopping the Great Lesson for comprehension questions before the child has had time to imagine it.
  • × Redirecting a child’s drawing toward accuracy — when the work of imagination matters far more than accuracy here.
  • × Insisting children know the facts before they pursue their questions — when in fact the questions drive the learning.
  • × Saying “That’s nice. Now you need to read this book and answer these questions.”
12
The opposite moves · what opens imagination

Create space. Ask questions
that invite picturing. Honor
the deep work even when the facts are still fuzzy.

Ask picturing questions.

What do you see? What does it smell like? Who else is there?

Value the representations.

A child’s drawing of the early earth is thinking. Treat it that way.

Trust the fuzzy middle.

A child who has imagined the scale of geological time has learned something real — even if the eras are still in the wrong order.

13
A child on the floor with a long sheet of butcher paper. He is building a model of a settlement adapting to geography — rivers drawn in blue, terraced fields in brown, huts in clusters. Pebbles for stones. A small paper figure labeled with a name. He has been at this for two mornings. The guide has not asked for a title page or a paragraph explanation. The model is the thinking.
Creation as cognition

The drawing.
The model.
The story.
The thinking.

When a child draws the early earth, she is thinking about geology. When a child builds a settlement, he is thinking about how humans adapt to environment.

These are not extras. They are the way thinking happens.

14
The equity provocation · imagination is not universal

Some children have had
rich imaginative lives.
Others come from homes
that prize the practical
and the literal. This
is not a deficit.

Some children embrace imaginative work immediately. Some need time and practice to trust that imagination is legitimate work. Some need explicit instruction in how to use imagination as a thinking tool.

The goal is not to make all children imagine the same way. It is to honor that imagination can look different for different children and still be real thinking.

15
The neurodivergence note

Imagination exists across
different neurodivergent minds. It just shows up differently.

Pictures.

A child who imagines in vivid visual scenes.

Movement.

A child who imagines by acting it out in her body.

Sensation.

A child who imagines through texture, weight, sound.

Logic.

A child who imagines by tracing cause to consequence.

Your job is not to make all children imagine in the same way. Your job is to honor and develop the imaginative capacity of each child’s unique mind.

16
A test you can run this week

A week after your next Great Lesson, look around the room.

If you see worksheets completed and shelved —

the lesson activated memory. It did not activate imagination.

If you see drawings, books open, arguments, models being built —

the lesson activated the engine. Children are still thinking a week later.

The evidence of imagination is visible. It is not hidden inside children’s heads. It is on the shelves, on the carpet, on the walls.
