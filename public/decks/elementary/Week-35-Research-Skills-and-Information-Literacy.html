Montessori Makers Residency Week 35 · Elementary · Month 9 · Language Deep Dive
Why this matters right now

Information literacy is not a future skill. It is a now skill.

Children are forming ideas about how the world works based on what they read and hear.

Most have no framework for evaluating it. They do not know how to ask whether a source is trustworthy. Not a skill for high school — perhaps the most important literacy we teach.

02
Not rules · questions they can ask themselves

Not cynicism. Healthy skepticism.

01 · The author

Who wrote this? What is their stake?

Are they trying to sell us something? Do they have expertise?

02 · The source

Library · vetted publisher · official site · personal blog?

None mean right or wrong. They tell you how much vetting the source has undergone.

03 · The information itself

Does this claim have evidence? What do three other sources say?

If they disagree — what are they disagreeing about?

03
Model this thinking out loud

You are reading aloud. You pause. You say: “Let me notice something here.”

“This article is published by a company that makes cell phones. It is arguing that cell phones are good for children. That seems like they might have a bias. They would benefit from people believing this.”

“Does that mean the article is wrong? Not necessarily. But I need to check what other sources say before I decide what I think.”

04
Misinformation · created by people who are very smart at their job

Emotional language — because emotions are more memorable than facts.

The manipulation

They exploit existing beliefs. They use images that are designed to move us. Repetition.

At scale. With resources. With intention.

The first defence · awareness

They should not feel ashamed if they encounter something false and believe it.

They should feel empowered to ask questions.

05
The habit of checking

When you encounter something surprising — something that confirms what you already believe — something that makes you emotional — pause.

Where did you read this? Who wrote it? What do other sources say? Not a rule. A habit of mind.

They can learn that saying “I am not sure if that is true” is smarter than believing or disbelieving without thinking.

06
Algorithms and personalisation · even elementary can understand

The information they see online is chosen — not neutral.

Different people see different information. Their phone shows them more of what they have already looked at. Not sinister, necessarily — but important to understand.

The information they see is not a complete picture of the world. It is a picture filtered through the interests of companies — and an algorithm’s understanding of what will keep them engaged.

07
The sharpest part · whose knowledge is in the “official” sources

For many children, the knowledge of their own community has been systematically excluded from “official” sources.

The harm to name

Teaching children to trust mainstream sources without context teaches them their community does not have valuable knowledge.

It sets them up to devalue their own people.

The correction

Grandmother’s traditional knowledge might not be in a textbook.

That does not make it less true. It makes it less published — a different thing.

08
A child asks: “Why do some people get sicker from the same illness than other people?” A real question — from actual wondering. You help her find sources. You ask her what different ones say. She discovers — some sources are about the science of illness. Some are about the history of racism in medicine. Some are about how poor communities have less access to healthcare. She is learning the question has multiple answers. That truth is complex. That she needs multiple sources to understand it. That is information literacy.

09
The equity provocation · said plainly

When we teach children to evaluate sources while privileging mainstream ones, we are teaching them to believe the powerful and question the marginalised.

That is not information literacy. That is cultural indoctrination.

An equity-centred approach teaches children to evaluate all sources — including the official ones. To recognise the power dynamics that shape what gets published.

Knowledge comes from many places. A complete understanding requires multiple perspectives.

10
Neurodivergence note · access to the ideas, in many forms

Some children need visual formats — charts, diagrams, colour-coding — to understand bias and credibility.

Some benefit from simpler language for complex concepts — but they still have access to the ideas.

Some will do research better with a partner, where they can talk through their thinking.

11
The library visit · teach the building

A library is a classroom. Teach her to use it.

Once a term, at least, bring the class to a real library. Not for a performance. For the skill. Show her how to ask a librarian a question — honestly, specifically.

Show her the shelves. The catalogue. The quiet. The person at the desk who is a professional in finding things out.

A child who is at home in a library has a second home for life.

12
Lateral reading · the fact-checker’s habit

Leave the page. Check from outside.

Professional fact-checkers do not stay on a website to judge it. They open other tabs. They ask — what do other people say about this source? Who runs it? Who funds it?

Teach the motion at ten — open a second tab. It becomes automatic by fifteen.

Staying inside one page is how children — and adults — get fooled.

13
The interview · a primary source she can create

An interview is research too.

Teach her to call a neighbour. A grandparent. A local business owner. Prepare five questions. Record, with permission. Listen more than speak.

What she hears will not match what the book says. That mismatch is the real curriculum.

She has, at ten, produced knowledge — not just consumed it. Change what that does to a child.

14
The source log · a practice, not a punishment

Every claim carries its source home.

From nine onward, every research note carries — author, title, date, page. A simple notebook, kept well, becomes a bibliography by the end of the project.

Don’t teach citation styles. Teach the habit of tracking. The style comes later. The habit does not.

This is how scholarship begins — quietly, in the margin, at nine.

15
Digital consent · the part no curriculum names

What she shares leaves a trail.

Teach her — before the project goes online — what a screenshot is. What a link is. What happens to a photograph when it is uploaded. Who can see it. Forever.

Teach her to ask — before posting about a friend — may I share this?

Information literacy is two-sided — how to find, and how to release. Both, at ten.
