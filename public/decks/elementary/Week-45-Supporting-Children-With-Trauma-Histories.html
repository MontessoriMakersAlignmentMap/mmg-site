Residency · Week Forty-Five · Elementary

For a child whose nervous system learned that the world is dangerous,
the freedom of Elementary can be exactly what heals her.
It can also be exactly what destabilises her.

Montessori Makers Residency Week 45 · Elementary · Month 11 · Behaviour, Community, Capstone
Why this matters right now

Elementary is less controlled than Primary. More autonomy. More responsibility. For most children, this is development. For a child with a trauma history, it can feel like chaos.

If her dysregulation shows up as aggression, withdrawal, or avoidance, we might call it a behaviour problem.

We might not understand that a nervous system is overwhelmed.

02
Trauma presents differently at the second plane

In Primary, trauma is loud. In Elementary, trauma has learned more sophisticated defences. Often, it is invisible.

Primary

Difficulty separating. Clingy or hard to comfort. Tantrums disproportionate to the trigger.

The body’s alarm is on the surface.

Elementary

Looks fine until she does not.

More sophisticated defences. More internal. Often invisible until something cracks.

03
What to actually watch for

Her nervous system has been trained to see danger. You will see it in four places.

Trust

Assumes people will hurt her or abandon her.

Reads threat into ambiguity. A quiet teacher is an angry teacher. A peer who doesn’t respond does not like her.

Regulation

Fine one minute. Fight-or-flight the next.

The trigger may not be obvious: a smell, a tone, a situation that echoes the original. From her perspective, the danger is real.

Body awareness

She has lost interoception.

Does not notice hunger, bathroom, tension. Cannot name emotions because she cannot feel them in her body.

Memory · attention

The brain is monitoring for threat.

Working memory is depleted by the work of staying safe. She is not “not paying attention” — she is paying attention to the wrong thing.

04
The paradox of freedom and responsibility

The same freedom that heals her is the same freedom that can destabilise her. Both are true.

Healing

Restores agency. Teaches that she has some control over her life — that she is not helpless.

Choice matters. She is capable. She can solve problems.

Destabilising

Chaos survivors feel neglected by freedom. Unpredictability survivors cannot hold their own choices.

Abuse survivors find agency itself dangerous.

05
The guide’s task

Hold both truths. She needs the freedom — it is part of what heals her. She needs support — or it is what destabilises her.

A guide who can read her nervous system. Who knows when to back off. Who knows when to push.

Whose own nervous system is regulated.

06
What your regulated nervous system contributes

This is not metaphorical. Your regulated nervous system is an essential part of the environment.

When a child enters your classroom, she is reading your nervous system. If you are calm, she can become calm. If you are dysregulated, she will become dysregulated.

Your steadiness teaches something words cannot. It teaches her nervous system that regulation is possible.

07
A commitment, not a stance

A guide working with trauma is committed to her own nervous system regulation.

She manages her own stress. She has her own support. She does the things that help her feel grounded and present.

She knows her own triggers. She does not dump her emotions onto children. She holds steady.

08
Scene · a child who learned to rely on herself

You create a routine that is predictable.
You greet her the same way every morning.
You do the things you say you will do.
When you say you will read aloud at three o’clock — you read aloud at three o’clock.

You are not performing safety. You are teaching her nervous system that the world can be predictable.

09
When freedom overwhelms — make the container smaller

She has difficulty choosing her work. The freedom of ten options is overwhelming.

So you offer three, not ten. You sit with her while she chooses. You make the choice feel manageable.

Over time, you expand. The container grows as she grows.

10
Scene · something triggered her — and she yelled

You do not respond with anger or judgement. You recognise what is happening.
You get small and calm.
You say: I see this is hard. I am here.

You do not demand she regulate right now. You give her nervous system what it needs in order to regulate. You are steady. You are present.

11
The child whose trauma is invisible

He looks fine. He does his work. He seems well-adjusted. And he cannot believe anything good about himself.

When you praise his work, he assumes you are lying or being condescending. When a peer invites him to play, he assumes it is a trick.

Trust takes time to build when it has been broken. Be consistent. Follow through. Do what you say. Celebrate the moments he takes the risk of believing someone.

12
Community practices that hold trauma

Talk about trauma without centring it as a child’s identity. Different nervous systems need different things — and it is the community’s job to provide them.

Do not tell her story. Do not make her a cautionary tale. Teach the community how nervous systems work — and how patience and kindness help people heal.

13
Equity provocation · traumas are not interchangeable

Housing instability is not abuse is not racism is not displacement. Trauma-informed practice means understanding this — not treating all traumatised children the same way.

How it shows up depends on what happened. When it happened. Who was with the child. And how the child made sense of it.

14
Neurodivergence note

Many children with trauma histories also present as neurodivergent. It can be hard to know what is rooted where. You do not have to figure it out.

You can support the child regardless. Create an environment that is safe and responsive to her needs.

15
Your own regulation · the first intervention

A dysregulated child cannot be calmed by a dysregulated adult.

Co-regulation is the mechanism. Your nervous system, settled, gives hers a model. Your breathing slows. She borrows it. No words needed.

Which means — you have to be able to regulate yourself first. In the middle of a hard moment. Without pretending. Without performing calm.

Your own therapy, your own sleep, your own support — is not self-care. It is the first line of intervention for every child in your room.

17
Closing question

Honestly.

If you have experienced trauma, what helped you heal?

How can you offer that to children in your classroom?
