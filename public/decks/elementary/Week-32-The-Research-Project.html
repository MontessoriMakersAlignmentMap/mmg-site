Montessori Makers Residency Week 32 · Elementary · Month 8
Why this matters right now

A culminating experience that draws together everything she has learned about thinking, reasoning, investigating, and communicating.

Not busy work. Mathematics as thinking. Science as thinking.

The research project becomes the centrepiece of the portfolio — documentation of intellectual development. And it shows your role: creating conditions, asking questions, supporting thinking.

02
The arc of a research project

From a genuine question to convincing herself of what is true.

01 · Question

Not assigned — hers.

What exactly do you want to know? Why does it matter to you?

02 · Design

How will she collect data?

Measurement. Sampling. Data collection as a mathematical problem.

03 · Evidence

Observation is not proof.

Control variables. Repeat. Is the evidence strong enough?

04 · Analysis

Organise. Graph. Find the pattern.

Statistics in service of a real question.

05 · Teach

She presents — she argues.

This is what I found. This is why I think I am right.

03
Questions emerge from conditions — not assignments

A child will not persevere for weeks on a question that does not interest her.

Your work first

Create conditions where children develop questions.

Read together. Notice things. Wonder out loud. Visit places. Talk about what confuses you.

Your work then

Listen. Help her articulate what she is wondering about.

What do you already know? What would it take to find out?

04
A boy fascinated by seeds. One day: “Does temperature affect how fast seeds sprout?” Four identical containers — warm, room, cold, ice. Data sheet. Weeks of observation. He discovers warmer water leads to faster sprouting — but not in a straight line. The warmest water is too hot. Sprouting slows at extremes. He graphs it. A curve, not a line. You ask: “Why does the line go up and then come down?” — “Maybe the seeds get cooked if it is too hot.” — “What experiment would test that?” He did not follow a procedure. He let the evidence lead him.

05
Documentation · the messy, real story of how she thought

Not a final neat product. A portfolio of thinking over time.

Photographs at different stages. Notes on what she wondered, changed, was surprised by. Data sheets. Drafts. You write down what she says when she talks about her work.

When she looks back at notes from six weeks ago — she sees her own growth. She sees how her thinking has deepened.

06
A child notices: sometimes the prices at the corner store near her house seem high compared to the supermarket. She wants to know whether it is true. Ten items. Two stores. Comparison chart. Some items more expensive at the corner store — some less. She calculates how much more her family would spend per month if they bought everything at the corner. The number surprises her. She learns about markup, about profit, about why small stores charge more. She considers whether it is fair that her neighbourhood has more expensive stores. This is a research question that emerges from lived experience. You honour it. You help her find what she needs. You show her that her questions matter.

07
The equity provocation · whose questions get honoured

Research projects reveal and amplify inequities if we are not careful about how we structure them.

A child with family resources has support — rides to the library, help gathering materials, someone to talk through findings. A child without these resources may struggle — not because she is less capable, but because the structural support is not there.

You provide what families cannot. Books, articles, materials in school. Time to discuss her thinking.

And be intentional about whose questions get honoured. Whether grandmother’s remedies work for colds — is a legitimate research question.

08
Neurodivergence note · where different minds shine

Some children are more powerful researchers than traditional students.

ADHD: struggles with routine — hyperfocuses on a question she cares about. Autism: pursues a special interest with extraordinary depth. Dyslexia: communicates findings more clearly through visual representation or oral presentation.

Support each child in designing research that matches her strengths and the way her mind works.

09
Stage one · finding a question worth a year

Not a topic. A question.

“Volcanoes” is a topic. “Why do some volcanoes explode while others ooze?” is a question. The first exhausts itself in a week. The second can carry a child through the year.

Your work at this stage — refuse the first topic. Refuse the second. Keep asking “but what do you actually want to find out?”

A good question has heat in it — something the child cannot walk away from.

10
Sources · primary and secondary, named and known

Teach the difference. At nine.

Primary

A letter from the time. A fossil. A photograph. An interview. A seed planted and measured.

Secondary

A book written later. An encyclopaedia. A documentary. Someone else’s synthesis.

Why it matters

Primary sources give them the thrill of direct contact. Secondary teaches them how others have thought.

Do both

Insist on at least one primary source. Insist on more than one secondary.

This is what research literacy looks like at ten.

11
Note-taking · the practice they will carry forever

Not copying. Converting.

Read a page. Close the book. Say it in your own words. Only then write. That sequence — read, close, speak, write — is the habit you are teaching.

Every note also carries a source. Page number. Author. Date. No exceptions. At nine.

The adult who cannot research responsibly is the child who was never taught to cite.

12
The first draft · will probably be bad. That is the plan.

Revision is the research. Not the clean-up.

The first draft reveals what they do not yet understand. The second forces them to find out. The third is the work they will show.

If you skip the second, you have not taught research. You have supervised transcription.

Protect the time to revise. More than any other stage.

13
Mid-stage · show it to the room

Other children are better editors than you.

At the halfway point, the child stands and speaks for ten minutes about what they have found. Other children ask questions. Real ones.

“How do you know?” “What does that word mean?” “Why does that part matter?”

Every hole in the research shows. Cheap, merciful, exact. The child walks back to work with a list.

14
The final stage · make it public

A research project ends with an audience beyond the teacher.

Parents invited. Younger children invited. The school cook invited. A ten-minute talk. A small booklet. A display with real artefacts.

Authentic audience changes the work. The stakes stop being grades. The stakes become “can I be understood by a stranger?”

That question is scholarship, at nine.

15
Your role · shifts through the weeks

Four roles. In order.

Week 1 · Midwife

Help the question be born. Ask. Refuse easy topics. Listen for heat.

Week 2 · 3 · Librarian

Point to sources. Model note-taking. Show one interview technique. Step back.

Week 4 · 5 · Editor

Read drafts with rigour. Ask where the evidence is. Refuse laziness. Kindly.

Week 6 · Host

Set the room. Invite the audience. Let them stand. Do not speak for them.

You are in each role fully — and then you move on.
