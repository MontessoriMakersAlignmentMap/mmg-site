Montessori Makers Residency Week 6 · Elementary
Why this matters right now

Geography seems
straightforward. Where
things are. Climate.
Terrain. How humans
organize themselves
on the earth.

But geography is not neutral. It is one of the most political subjects in the curriculum. How you teach it tells children either that the world is an objective fact that just is — or that the world has been organized in particular ways that reflect power.

You need to teach it with your eyes open to what you are teaching beyond the maps.

02
Cartography · every projection distorts

The earth is a sphere. Maps are flat. Every mapmaker chooses which distortions to accept.

Mercator · the classroom map

Greenland the size of Africa.

Enlarges lands near the poles. Shrinks lands near the equator. Africa appears smaller than it is.

Gall-Peters · equal area

Africa at its actual size.

Preserves relative land area. Shows the southern hemisphere at its actual scale.

Neither map is “true.” Every map is a choice. The question is: whose choice, for whose purposes?

03
Five political choices hidden inside a map

A map is never just a map. It is a set of decisions, made by someone, for some reason.

i.
Projection. Which distortions to accept. Which hemispheres look big. Which look small. Greenland is not the size of Africa.
ii.
Labels. European cities named in detail. Large African nations left as blank space inside colonial borders.
iii.
Routes. The arrows of European explorers drawn boldly. African, Arab and Asian trade routes — which predated them by centuries — not drawn at all.
iv.
Borders. The nation-state treated as the natural unit of the world. Indigenous territories, kinship lands, trade networks — treated as historical footnote.
v.
Resources. The earth mapped by what can be extracted from it. Oil, minerals, timber, cash crops. No map of what has been stripped. No map of what was there before.
04
Political geography encodes colonialism

Look at the borders
of Africa. Count the
straight lines.

Lines drawn by people who had never been there.

Those borders were drawn by European colonial powers. With no regard for the ethnic, linguistic, cultural, or ecological boundaries that organized the land. They were drawn to benefit extraction.

They divided communities. Divided ecosystems. Organized territory to prioritize external profit over human flourishing.

Teach those borders without teaching where they came from, and you teach children that the world we see now is natural and inevitable.

05
The language trap · “developed” · “developing” · “underdeveloped”

These are not neutral descriptions. They are a ranking.

What it says

All countries are moving toward the same model.

Western industrialized nations are the apex. Everyone else is behind.

What it erases

Alternative models of living well.

Communities that have deliberately chosen different paths. Traditional ways that are different, not less.

What it teaches

More stuff equals more progress.

Infrastructure, urbanization, consumption as the measure of a people.

Watch the words you use. Watch the rankings hidden inside them.

06
A guide stands at a world map pinned to the wall. She points to countries and tells children facts. Capital cities. Landmarks. Children are asked to label blank maps with country names. They learn where things are. No one asks why they are there. No one asks who drew the lines. No one asks what the map leaves out.
Scene A · geography as facts

Children learn
where.
Not why.

The map is presented as a thing that is. The borders as lines that have always existed. The names as the names. Children memorize. They label. They pass the test.

They have learned a world that was never questioned. A world that just is.

07
A guide unrolls three different maps of the same region side by side. A Mercator projection. A Gall-Peters equal-area projection. An indigenous map organized by language, kinship and trade. She asks the children: what does each mapmaker want you to see? What is bigger? What is smaller? What is named? What is blank? A child traces a trade route that predates European ships by eight hundred years.
Scene B · geography as justice

Children learn
to read
the map.

They see that multiple geographies exist at once. That the world can be organized in different ways. That the Western way is one way among many.

They have learned to ask: whose geography is this? Who benefits? What is being left out?

08
The equity provocation · hold both

The work is not to
reject Western
geography. It is to
teach it alongside
other geographies.

Children need to read political maps to function in this world. They also need to see indigenous maps, trade-route maps, ecological maps — and to understand that each one is a partial truth.

Teach them both to navigate the world as it is, and to imagine how it could be otherwise.

09
Names · what a place is called, and by whom

Denali. Mount McKinley. Uluru. Ayers Rock. The same mountain, two names.

The older name was usually given by people who lived alongside the place for thousands of years. The replacement name was usually given by people who arrived and decided the place was theirs to name. Teach children to notice which name a map uses — and why.

To rename a place is to claim it. To restore a name is to return it.

10
Climate geography · the map that was not drawn for tourists

The climate map is a justice map. The children who have contributed least to warming will inherit most of it.

Elementary children can hold this. They are ready for it. When you teach them biomes, teach them which biomes are shrinking. When you teach them coastlines, teach them which coastlines are disappearing. When you teach them rivers, teach them who lives downstream.

This is not doom. This is orientation.

11
Practical moves · how to do this on Monday

Small. Repeatable. Not a separate unit.

Put two maps on the wall, not one.

Mercator and Gall-Peters. Let children notice the difference without you naming it.

Keep a globe on the shelf.

A child who has held a globe is harder to fool with a flat map.

Use both names when both exist.

Denali / Mount McKinley. Uluru / Ayers Rock. Model the both-ness.

Ask the questions aloud.

“Who drew this line? When? Why?” The question is the lesson.

12
What children do after · projects that follow naturally

Second-plane children love this work. It rewards their moral seriousness.

Rename the neighborhood

Research what this land was called before, by whom, and what happened to them.

Draw a trade-route map

One that predates European ships. Sahel. Silk Road. Indian Ocean.

Map what came from where

Potatoes. Chocolate. Coffee. The plate is already a geography lesson.

Map your own day

Where did your shirt come from? Your phone? The water in your glass?

Language map

Plot languages, not nations. The world looks different.

Watershed map

Organize the region by its rivers instead of its states.

13
Pitfalls · ways this work goes wrong

Three failure modes. Watch for them in yourself.

Guilt as content.

Teaching colonial harm as emotional weight with no geography attached. Children tune out.

Tokenizing.

One indigenous map displayed once in February. The alternative becomes a mascot.

Abandoning rigor.

Children still need to know capitals, continents, rivers, cities. Critical literacy is not an excuse for vagueness.

14
A common worry · is this too heavy for seven-year-olds?

The second-plane child is explicitly hungry for moral complexity. Withholding it is the heavier choice.

Children who know unfairness exists and are allowed to talk about it do better than children who sense unfairness exists and are told it does not. The hiding does the harm. The naming does the work.

Tell the truth at a scale she can hold. She can hold more than you think.

15
The week, in one line

Teach geography as a verb —
something people have done to the world,
not something the world simply is.

16
Exercise · your own geography, examined

Before Monday, draw the world from memory.

No reference. Five minutes. Then notice: which continent did you draw first? Which did you forget? Whose countries did you know by shape — and whose did you lump together? Which oceans did you name?

This is the geography you carry. This is the geography, uncorrected, that would reach your children.
