Montessori Makers Residency Week 15 · Elementary
Why this matters right now

You are teaching ecology in a moment of environmental catastrophe.

Children in your classroom are already experiencing the consequences. Already living with environmental racism — the siting of polluting industries in their communities, the absence of green space, the scarcity of clean water.

And children from wealthy communities are being insulated from the consequences of their consumption.

You cannot teach ecology only as wonder and interconnection. You have to teach it with honesty about injustice and power.

02
What ecology is

The study of relationships.
Of flows. Of interdependence.

Nothing exists in isolation. A plant needs soil, water, sunlight, and the organisms that help it use nitrogen. Those organisms need the plant. An animal needs plants or other animals, water, air.

An ecosystem is a network of relationships. An organism cannot be understood apart from its relationships. This is beautiful. This is also foundational.

03
The wonder curriculum · beautiful, but incomplete

An ecology curriculum that focuses only on wonder in a local ecosystem is missing the larger context.

Many children live in ecosystems that have been deliberately destroyed or poisoned. Not a pristine forest. A parking lot next to a highway. An industrial site with contaminated soil.

The ecology they are studying is in a condition created by specific human decisions about power and profit and whose lives matter.

04
What an equitable ecology curriculum includes

Wonder. And what is happening, and why, and who has power over it.

Where our water comes from.

Whether it is clean. Whose is, whose is not — and why.

Where our food comes from.

How it is grown. What happens to our waste. The full system, not the sanitized piece.

Why certain communities carry certain burdens.

The history of siting. The history of zoning. The history of whose neighborhoods get the freeways and the refineries.

Movements of environmental justice.

The people fighting to protect their communities and their land. Named. Studied. Honored.

Indigenous knowledge.

Peoples who have sustained ecosystems for thousands of years. Western science is only now beginning to understand.

Agency.

Children can learn about environmental issues in their own community — and they can do something.

05
Interdependence · the honest version

Interdependence is also how harm travels through systems.

Pollution upstream affects fish downstream. Carbon burned in wealthy countries affects rainfall in poor countries. Corporations deciding where to dump waste affects the health of specific communities.

A child who understands this can understand that pollution in their neighborhood is not random. It happened because someone decided it was acceptable to poison the neighborhood where Black, brown, and poor people live.

Then the child can ask: Who made that decision? What can we do about it?

The water cycle. Children excited. Then one child asks — “Is our water clean?” The guide pauses. She knows this school has had lead contamination. She decides to answer honestly. “That is a really important question. The water in our pipes is supposed to be safe. Sometimes it is not. Some communities have had problems. 08
Solutions, not only problems

Honest about problems. And about the people solving them.

Indigenous peoples who have sustained ecosystems for thousands of years. Communities fighting for clean water, for green space, for the right to breathe clean air. Scientists remediating contaminated soil. Young people creating movements for climate justice.

Include these stories. Give children models of resistance and resilience.

Tell them: people care. Change is possible.

09
The personal dimension · without guilt

The child is not separate from nature — observing it.
She is embedded in it.

What not to say

“You are destroying the planet by using plastic.”

Develops anxiety. Or defensiveness. Neither produces care.

What to say

“You are part of ecosystems. You depend on them. They depend on you. And there are choices you can make that help them thrive.”

Develops genuine care and agency.

Not guilt for existing. Accurate information. And trust that the child will develop an ethical response.

10
The equity provocation · environmental racism is not an abstraction

Children of color are more likely to live near pollution. Less likely to have access to green space. More likely to have health problems from environmental hazards.

To children from marginalized communities

“Your environment is beautiful and worthy of protection.”

And right now it is under attack — and you have the right to fight for it.

To wealthy children

“Your access to nature is a privilege not all children have.”

Many communities have been denied green space and clean air. You can use your privilege to help change that.

11
What the wonder-only curriculum teaches

If you do not acknowledge the difference, you do a disservice to all children.

To children of color: their environment is normal; beauty and nature are elsewhere; their neighborhood is not worth caring about.

To wealthy children: access to nature is their right; pollution happens to other places; they do not have to think about the consequences of their consumption.

12
Neurodivergence note · outdoor study and climate anxiety

Concrete actions. Not catastrophic thinking.

A child with sensory sensitivities may find outdoor exploration overwhelming — and may need shorter periods. Quieter spaces. Fewer variables at once.

A child with anxiety about climate change may need explicit support in focusing on concrete actions they can take rather than spiralling into catastrophe.

13
Where the curriculum often begins — and where it often stops

A pond, a forest, a meadow. Observations. Cycles. Energy flowing through systems. Nothing wasted — everything recycled back in.

Children document what lives there. They see that when organisms die, they decompose and feed new life. They develop an intuitive sense of cycles.

This is profound. It is also incomplete if we stop there.

14
Indigenous knowledge as central content — not a footnote

Peoples who have sustained their ecosystems for thousands of years.

They have knowledge about how to live in relationship with the land that Western science is only now beginning to understand.

Treat this as primary content. Not as an interesting add-on when there is time.

15
The move — from observer to participant

Ecosystems are shaped by human decisions. Which means children can influence them.

The guide asks: what would we want to change? Who would we need to talk to? What would we say?

The child moves from standing outside the ecosystem, documenting it, to standing inside it as someone with a voice.
