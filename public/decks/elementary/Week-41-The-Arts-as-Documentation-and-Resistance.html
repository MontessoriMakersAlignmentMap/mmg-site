Montessori Makers Residency Week 41 · Elementary · Month 11 · Behaviour, Community, Capstone
Why this matters right now

We teach art and music as if they are separate from history and power. Painting because it is a nice skill. Singing because it is pleasurable.

We do not tell her the art she is making is dangerous, that it is powerful, that it is the way human beings have always documented what they know when documentation has been forbidden.

In a just elementary curriculum, art and music are not decoration. They are tools for knowing — and for power.

02
The thesis, said plainly

The arts have never been decoration. They have been the way people who were silenced found their voice.

Documentation. Resistance. Communication. Power.

03
There are many histories that were never written down

Not because they did not happen. Because the people who lived them were not allowed to read or write.

Not allowed to own documents. Not allowed access to the technologies of power. And yet they documented their knowledge. Through fibre arts. Through quilts and cloth. Through music and oral tradition.

The art was the archive. Because the archive was closed.

04
Three traditions · three ways of writing without writing

Beautiful to an outsider. Readable — by the people it was made for.

Quilts

Quilts made by enslaved African Americans sometimes contained maps — routes to freedom sewn into the pattern.

Decoration to the overseer. Directions to those who knew.

Spirituals

Documented theology, resistance, hope. Some lyrics encoded information about escape routes.

Sung, remembered, taught to children. The music carried what writing could not.

Indigenous textiles

Across the Americas. Documented knowledge of place, materials, relationship to land — readable by the community.

Confiscated. Put in museums. Still stolen property. Still documentation.

06
Why it was banned · what it was doing

When you see a government try to ban a music, you are watching that government admit the music has power.

Art and music allowed people to assert their existence, their humanity, their right to take up space. To create beauty in contexts where beauty was meant to be denied them.

To build community — across people facing the same oppression.

07
Three movements · three acts of refusal

Not genres. Responses to specific histories — by specific people — under specific conditions.

The Blues

Created by formerly enslaved African Americans in the American South. Survival and resistance.

Many white Americans tried to ban it — because they understood it was dangerous.

Mexican Muralism

Rivera and others, during and after the Revolution. Enormous public murals, impossible to ignore.

Not for the wealthy’s walls. Part of the built environment. Part of what you met as you walked.

Hip-Hop

South Bronx, 1970s–80s. Young Black and Latinx people whose communities were being destroyed.

Sampling, breaking, lyricism — making something new from the materials at hand.

09
A communication system — across borders, distances, danger

The griots were not only documenting history. They were the communication system of their communities.

Civil Rights protest songs carried information — where to gather, how to stay safe, what the movement was. They made it possible for people with different backgrounds and languages to be together.

Today: Palestinian visual art. Afghan music under the ban. Ukrainian song during the invasion. We exist. We have culture. We have humanity.

10
When we teach art, teach it as power

Not decoration or leisure. Tools for knowing, for resistance, for documenting, for communication.

Teach artists from cultures that have been historically oppressed. Teach the history of what those artists did with their art.

Ask every child: what story do you want to tell — and what do you want your art to do?

11
Not “make something pretty.” Real problems to solve through art.

Four kinds of prompt — four kinds of power. All available to an elementary child.

Document

Preserve something important in your community that might otherwise be lost.

Recipe. Language. Instrument. Story told by one grandmother.

Resist

Create art that names something unfair — at school, in your city, in the world.

A mural. A song. An installation that makes the ignored impossible to ignore.

Build community

Make something that can only exist because many hands made it together.

Collective portrait. Patchwork. Ensemble piece.

Communicate

Say something that cannot be said in words — to a specific audience.

To your younger brother. To the school board. To children in a different country.

12
What knowledge does your family have that is important to preserve? One group documents recipes from a grandmother’s kitchen and the stories that go with them. Another documents music traditions from their community. Another, the languages they speak at home and the stories in those languages. Written recipes with illustrations. Audio of grandparents telling stories. Video of cooking. Children learning to play instruments from their culture. The documentation is the art. The stories are valuable. It is their job to preserve them.

14
The equity provocation · who gets to be an artist

Teach art as decoration and leisure — and children from poor communities learn that art is a luxury. Something other people get to do.

Teach art as power, documentation, and resistance — and every child becomes an artist.

Every child has a story worth documenting. A voice worth amplifying. A form of resistance worth honouring.

15
Neurodivergence note · where the voice actually lives

Art and music are often where neurodivergent children express themselves and find power.

A child with autism may be unable to speak in traditional ways — and create profound visual art.

A child with ADHD may struggle with sitting still — and find flow in movement and music.
