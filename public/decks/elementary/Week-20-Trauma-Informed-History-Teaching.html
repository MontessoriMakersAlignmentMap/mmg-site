Residency · Week Twenty · Elementary

When you teach history
to children, you are often
teaching them
about harm.

And if that harm was done to their people, their family, their ancestors, then teaching history is not an academic exercise. It is something that touches their nervous system.

Montessori Makers Residency Week 20 · Elementary
Why this matters right now

Elementary children are now ready to encounter historical content that might activate trauma history or intergenerational grief.

A child whose family was displaced by forced removal hears the Indian Removal Act differently than a child whose family benefited from it.

A child whose ancestors were enslaved hears the economic reasons for the slave trade differently than a child whose ancestors were enslavers.

A child whose family survived genocide carries that knowledge in their body, whether they have words for it yet or not.

02
What this week is for

Your job is not to avoid teaching hard history. That would be dishonest.

It would rob children of the chance to understand their own experience as part of a larger story of human resilience.

Your job is to teach hard history in a way that honours children’s complexity and protects their nervous systems from retraumatization — while building their understanding of what their people have endured and overcome.

03
Intergenerational trauma · not metaphorical

Collective trauma shapes family systems and community patterns across generations.

Explicit transmission.

Parents telling children what happened. Or not telling them. Or telling them in ways that carry fear.

Implicit transmission.

Nervous systems of survivors staying in hypervigilance — creating homes where children absorb alertness they cannot name.

Social and material.

Families in poverty or precarity because of historical dispossession. Inheriting circumstances their ancestors’ trauma shaped.

04
What you are touching when you teach

A child may have an ancestor who was enslaved — and that ancestor’s fear of violence may live in the child’s nervous system.

The child may have no conscious memory of family conversations. But they may inherit a kind of grief or rage they do not have language for.

When you teach that history explicitly, you are giving the child a framework for understanding something they have already felt. That can be healing. Or it can be retraumatizing — if done without care.

05
The difference lies in how you teach it

“Your people were helpless” vs. “your people resisted.”

Retraumatizing

History as a fact. Violence unnamed. Victims passive.

The child whose ancestors were enslaved learns their people were helpless. The narrative of victimhood without the counter-narrative of agency.

·
Honest and healing

Humanity centered. Resistance named. Violence acknowledged without glorification.

What happened to your ancestors was real and wrong. And they survived. And their survival is part of your strength.

06
The same principle · for any history of collective harm

Not what was done to peoples. What peoples did.

Colonization — honour what Indigenous peoples did to resist, adapt, survive, maintain their cultures despite everything.

Genocide — not as endpoint. The communities that survived. The ways culture persisted. The ongoing fight to restore what was stolen. The descendants reclaiming what was nearly lost.

07
Preparation that goes beyond the content

Know your children. Not to label. To know what they carry.

Who has which heritage.

Indigenous. Enslaved ancestors. Displacement. Persecution. This is not surveillance. It is knowing.

Prep individual children or families.

Sometimes a note home: “Next week we’re studying this. I wanted you to know.”

Listen to what families tell you.

Sometimes nothing. That is fine. Sometimes something that gives you context for how their child might respond.

08
Name the violence · directly, not gratuitously

Children understand the weight of wrongness. You do not need graphic description.

“Many Indigenous peoples were forced off their land against their will. This was violence. It was wrong.”

Then move to resilience and agency. Peoples who resisted removal by hiding and maintaining communities. Peoples who adapted to new lands while maintaining their cultures. Contemporary Indigenous peoples reclaiming sovereignty and rebuilding.

Harm happened. Your ancestors were harmed. Look at what they did anyway. Look at who you are because of them.

09
When a child has a big response

You do not need to do therapy. You need to hold the space with steady presence.

“I can see this is hitting you. That makes sense. You can sit for a bit. You can move to a quieter space if you need to. You can talk about it later if you want to. I’m here.”

That is enough.

10
After difficult history · decompression

Do not teach about genocide and then move directly to math.

Let there be time for integration.

Art. Discussion. Quiet work. A deliberate exhale.

Check in individually.

With the children who were most activated. “I know that was heavy. Do you want to talk?”

Co-regulate.

Or just offer a regulated nervous system in proximity. Sit with them. Let them know you’re steady even if they’re not.

11
When the curriculum activates the child’s personal history

A parent in immigration detention. A family member recently evicted. The lesson becomes immediate and personal.

The child might not make the connection explicitly. But their body knows. They might become withdrawn, or act out, or have a panic response.

Remain present. Create structure. Do not minimize with “it was a long time ago.” Do not dive deep into their situation if they do not want to talk.

Offer choice and agency. “Do you need to step out for a bit? Do you want to sit somewhere quieter? Do you want to talk about it now or later?”

12
The guide teaches that there was a slave trade. Mentions the conditions on ships briefly. Moves on to the economic reasons for slavery — supply and demand, labor, profit. A Black child sits quietly, feeling something she cannot name. She knows her family history. She has heard her grandmother talk about her people. What she learns is that her people were economic units. Their transportation was a supply chain. She learns this as a fact, without context for what her people were beyond that.
Scene A · retraumatized through omission

She is being
retraumatized
through omission.

The harm is not in the words said. It is in the words that were not.

13
Millions of African peoples were forcibly taken from their homes and transported across an ocean in conditions designed to kill many of them. This was violence. This was evil. And then the guide teaches about the Africans who resisted. Who rebelled on slave ships. Who created communities in the places they were taken. Who maintained their cultures, their music, their spirituality, their languages — despite everything. Who organized rebellions. Who became architects of their own freedom.

14
The equity provocation

If you teach history without addressing harm, you teach that harm does not matter.

If you teach history in a way that centers harm without centering agency and resilience — you reinforce narratives of victimhood that harm the children whose ancestors experienced that violence.

The work is to name what happened honestly while making space for what your students’ ancestors did in response.

That is harder than either erasure or victimization. But that is what honesty requires.

15
Neurodivergence note · the nervous system is not misbehaving

Stronger nervous-system responses to violence or displacement content are not a behavior problem.

It is a nervous system responding appropriately to content that touches unresolved trauma.

Grounding techniques. Movement breaks. Co-regulation — your steady presence. And explicit permission to step out if they are dysregulated.
