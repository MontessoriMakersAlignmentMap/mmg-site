You are not studying Primary anymore.
You are studying what Primary built.

Montessori Makers Residency Week 49 · Elementary · Month 12 · Behaviour, Community, Capstone
Why this matters right now

Everything elementary children bring with them is rooted in what happened in Primary — or in the absence of it.

When you see an elementary child who is independent and capable, that came from Primary. You did not create it. You are building on it.

When you see a child who struggles with community, what looks like a behaviour problem might be a child who did not get the formation she needed.

02
The Primary foundation — carried in the body

Her hand knows how to hold a pencil. Her body knows how to sit and focus. Her nervous system was shaped by predictability, beauty, and order.

Hand

Years of pouring, cutting, using tools.

Her hand is educated.

Body

Years of concentrative work.

Her body knows how to stay.

Nervous system

Shaped by predictability and order.

Her baseline is calm.

Habits

How school works. How the day moves. How people care for one another.

It is already in her.

03
What Primary built — and how Elementary reshapes it

The skills are still there. Some of them need to be stretched into a new shape.

Primary built

Concentration on a single task. A small community. Independence for self-care.

All real. All useful.

Elementary asks

Moving between different kinds of work. A larger community. Responsibility for something that matters to the group.

The shape of the work has grown.

04
What children normalised in Primary — and you get to build on

You are not starting from zero with classroom norms. You are building on a foundation that is already there.

Environment

Sweep up. Put it back. The room is ours.

Concentration

When someone is working, you do not interrupt.

Choice

Within real limits. Choice matters.

Problem-solving

You try. You think. You figure things out.

Care

For each other. In small, constant ways.

05
What the Primary child understands

She has learned something essential about learning, and something essential about agency.

About learning

I can figure things out. Mistakes are part of it. My job is not the right answer — my job is to engage with the work.

When she hits difficulty, she does not immediately ask for help or give up. She tries.

About agency

I have choices. My choice matters. My voice has value in the community.

When asked what she wants to learn, she can answer. She has spent years answering that question.

06
Your responsibility — honour what was built

Respect what has been built. Do not assume the children do not have skills or understanding. Trust the formation that has happened.

You are continuing the work of guides who came before you. The children you receive are not blank slates. They have been formed.

Honour it.

07
Scene · independence, capability, concentration

She manages her own work. She solves her own problems. She concentrates deeply.
You notice this. You think: this child came through a good Primary program.
You trust her. You do not hover. You give her responsibility.

She rises to meet it. She is thriving — because the guide honoured the foundation that was built.

08
Scene · the child whose Primary was healing

He looks to you constantly for approval. He cannot manage his choices. He dysregulates easily.
You ask his former Primary guide. You learn:
his Primary was mostly spent healing from dysregulation. He did not get the full formation.

You give him more structure. More check-ins. You build the capacities Primary did not build — because Primary was focused on getting him safe. You honour that work. Then you build on it.

09
Equity provocation · Primary is not evenly distributed

Wealthier families are more likely to have access to good Montessori Primary. Families experiencing poverty are more likely to be in traditional or under-resourced programs.

If you are intentional about it, you can be the place where the child develops the independence and capacity that should have been built in Primary.

If you assume all children have it, you will blame them for not having what you did not give them.

10
Neurodivergence note · consistency across the handoff

Primary for a neurodivergent child may have looked different. Her guides made choices about how to support her.

Understand what was built and how. Continue the support in a way that is consistent.

11
Inherited pedagogy · what your body remembers before your mind does

When you tense, under stress, whose voice comes out of your mouth?

A teacher who never examined her own schooling will, at the hard moment, reach for the tools used on her. Shaming. Shaming with a softer voice. Correcting instead of observing. The body remembers.

Name what was done to you. Specifically. Out loud, with a trusted colleague.

This work is not indulgent. It is prerequisite. It protects the children from a script you never chose.

12
The teacher who hurt you · still teaching, in you

Name her. Name what she did.

The tenth-grade maths teacher who made you believe you were bad at the subject. The piano teacher who hit your hands. The professor who praised only the loud boys. They are not dead to you. They are still in the room.

Write a letter you will never send. Read it aloud. Burn it if you wish. This is not therapy for its own sake.

A teacher who has not grieved the teachers who failed her — will unknowingly be one.

13
The teacher who saw you · the one to name as well

Somebody saw you. What exactly did she do?

Be specific. She waited. She asked a second question. She kept the book you wrote. She remembered your sister’s name. She said “I expect more of you.” She said nothing at all, but sat beside you.

That specific move — study it. Practise it. It is already yours, because she gave it to you.

You are allowed to be in her lineage. Say her name aloud in your own classroom, once this year.

14
Your own schooling · was a class position, a racial position, a body

The school that formed you was not neutral.

Were you the one the teachers expected to succeed? The one they expected to disrupt? The one they could not see? Each position taught you something about authority, about belonging, about what knowledge is for.

You carry that position into every classroom. You cannot take it off. You can only know it, and watch it operate.

The child in front of you is probably in a different position. You will need to ask, and to listen — often.

15
Write your teaching lineage · a named document

Who taught you? Who taught them?

Trace it back, if you can. A teacher’s teacher. A mentor’s mentor. The author of the book that shifted everything. Whose child-care tradition? Whose language of observation?

A single page. Pinned above your desk. Seen daily.

On hard days — you are not alone. On good days — you are carrying someone forward. Both are true.
