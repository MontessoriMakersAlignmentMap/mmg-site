Montessori Makers Residency Week 40 · Elementary · Month 10 · Art and Language Integration
Why this matters right now

Music is abstract and concrete at the same time. She feels it in her body. She sees it on the staff. She understands it intellectually — all three at once.

Elementary mathematics is becoming abstract and symbolic — fractions and equations on paper. And we are still teaching most children to understand fractions by moving blocks or drawing pictures.

The connection between music theory and mathematics is not decorative. It is essential.

02
The thesis, said plainly

Music theory and mathematics are not separate languages. They are the same language — and music might be the most embodied way she learns it.

The notation is just a way to represent what she already understands.

04
Meter is multiplication · different groupings, different feels

Four groups of four is different from three groups of four. The relationship matters.

4/4

Four beats to the bar. March. The steady grid most of our music sits on.

Groups of four.

3/4

Three beats to the bar. Waltz. A different body weight on the first beat.

Groups of three.

6/8

Six eighths, felt as two groups of three. Compound meter — two levels of grouping at once.

Nested multiplication.

05
The beauty of the Montessori approach

She does not translate what she learned about fractions into music. The music is where she learned fractions.

Music theory is not “a way to make math fun.” It is a concrete context for abstract mathematical reasoning — one that happens through ears, body, and voice before it ever lands on paper.

06
Scales and intervals teach proportional thinking

Move a major scale up by one step. It is still a major scale — because the relationships between intervals stay the same.

The actual pitches matter less than the relationships between them. This is the same understanding that underlies congruent shapes — or equivalent fractions.

The values change. The proportional relationships do not.

07
Intervals are number relationships — heard, not calculated

She does not do the math to understand the relationships. She understands them through her ear and her voice.

Octave

A doubling of frequency. The same note, one size up.

Ratio 2 : 1.

Perfect fifth

The interval between the first and fifth of a scale. The strong consonance.

Ratio 3 : 2 — or 1.5.

Perfect fourth

Between the first and fourth. The settled answer to the fifth.

Ratio 4 : 3.

08
The material does the concrete work

When she uses the bells to explore intervals, she is manipulating the proportional relationships with her hands.

She can feel the relationship between a fifth and a third. She can rearrange the bells and hear how the relationships change.

The same concrete, manipulative-based learning we use for fractions or geometry. Happening through music.

09
Harmonic progression is algebraic thinking

A chord is a combination of pitches that can be symbolised. A I–IV–V–I progression is a pattern that can be recognised and repeated.

When she names that pattern in one key and finds it again in another — she is thinking algebraically. Variables stand for pitches. The structure stays.

Notation is downstream of the thinking. The thinking is the point.

10
Composition · where every layer comes together

She organises pitches into patterns over time. Rhythm. Melody. Harmony — brought together to make something new.

She is not learning music theory as isolated facts. She is learning it as a way of thinking about relationships, patterns, proportions.

That it happens through music is not incidental. It is the entire point.

11
Not “music in math class.” One curriculum, two modalities.

The same concepts through different modalities — each deepening the others.

Fractions

Come back to rhythm. Write the note values alongside the fractions on the board.

Same equation, two modalities.

Measurement & time

Return to tempo and duration. BPM is a rate. A bar is a unit.

Music as the stopwatch.

Geometry

The proportions of instruments. The geometry of acoustic spaces. Pitch arranged on a staff.

Space and sound, mapped.

The task: show how many quarter notes fit into a whole note. Some children draw the notes on a staff. Some move the bells to show the relationships. Some write the equation. Four quarters equals one whole. One-quarter plus one-quarter plus one-quarter plus one-quarter equals one. 4/4 equals 1. They are all answering the same question. All of the answers are valid.

Waltz meter

Now draw it.

The guide beats a rhythm on the drum. Children listen and move. They feel the pulse. She changes the meter; they feel the shift. Then the guide asks them to draw what they feel. Some children draw stick figures in different positions. Some draw circles and lines for the pattern. Some write 3/4 versus 2/4 and work out the proportions. The same mathematics — through the embodied experience of rhythm.
14
The equity provocation · who gets to be a mathematician

Mathematical reasoning is not the province of children who are naturally inclined toward abstraction. Some children understand proportion through their bodies before they understand it in symbols.

Teach mathematics only through symbols and worksheets, and you teach those children they are not mathematicians.

Teach it through music, through movement, through concrete materials — and they suddenly have access. The understanding is as real, as deep, as valuable as any achieved through notation.

15
Neurodivergence note · not outliers — central

Many neurodivergent children have profound musical and mathematical abilities. In a music-and-maths integrated curriculum, they are not outliers.

ADHD children often respond brilliantly to rhythm and pattern. Autistic children often have perfect pitch, or extraordinary mathematical reasoning.

They are seeing their gifts celebrated as central to the curriculum.
