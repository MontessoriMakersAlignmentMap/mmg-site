Montessori Makers Residency Week 30 · Elementary · Month 8
Why this matters right now

At month eight, resistance is not about never having engaged before. Something has shifted.

The work has become too hard and she has internalised that she cannot do it. Or the work feels too easy. Or something more complex — identity, anxiety, how she perceives her own mathematical capability.

The intervention is not more worksheets. It is not lowering expectations.

02
Resistance is rarely about mathematics itself

It is about what she has come to believe about herself as a mathematical thinker.

The messages accumulate

A parent: “I was never good at math.”

Getting told she is smart — or not — based on a test. Noticing who gets called on. Noticing who gets praised for speed.

What she absorbs

Mathematics is something you either get or you do not.

No growing into it. The messages accumulate in the body. They show up as resistance.

03
What resistance looks like

Not one face. Many disguises.

Avoidance

Choosing other work — deliberately.

Sitting with materials without intention.

Rushing

Racing through golden bead work to be done.

Not to understand.

Asking for easier

A cry for a place where she feels competent.

Not a limitation of capability.

Constant help-seeking

Not ignorance — distrust of her own thinking.

She cannot verify herself anymore.

Perfectionism

Refusing to try unless she knows she is right.

Devastating — because mathematics is built on trying, revising.

Body signals

Stress. Anxiety. Stomach aches at math time.

The nervous system is speaking.

04
Stereotype threat is real and operates in elementary classrooms

She does not have to consciously believe the stereotype for it to affect her performance.

When threat is activated, even very capable students show decreased performance and increased anxiety. Disengaging from the domain protects her from the threat.

In an equity-centred classroom, you understand this mechanism so you can actively work against it.

05
Dyscalculia and mathematics anxiety · different, both real

Neither is solved by trying harder. Both need different support.

Dyscalculia

A specific neurological difference in processing numerical information.

Often paired with strong conceptual reasoning. What is hard is speed, automaticity, symbolic representation. She does not grow out of it.

Mathematics anxiety

Actual physical stress response — even to the anticipation of mathematics.

The anxiety itself interferes with working memory. She may be capable — and yet unable to access her capability in the moment.

06
She used to love the golden beads. Three weeks ago something shifted. Picks up beads. Puts them down. “I noticed you have not been choosing the golden beads lately.” — “They are boring.” — “What was interesting before?” — silence. — “I am not as good at it as Ravi. 07
Rebuilding confidence · through the materials

The child needs to be in a place of success. Work alongside her initially — do not leave her alone with the anxiety.

Go back to materials she already understands deeply. Extend them. Find new questions inside the old. Make them beautiful and inviting again.

Name effort, not intelligence. “You stayed with that problem even though it was tricky. That is mathematical thinking.” Not “you are so smart.”

Help her see mistakes are information — not failure.

08
A boy who has always seemed to dislike mathematics. Chooses anything but math. You watch him one day during free choice. He is in the building area — examining how pieces fit, trying configurations, solving problems, being precise. His brain is working mathematically — he is just not using math materials. You say: “The way you are thinking about making that structure stable — mathematicians think that way too. We call it geometric thinking.” Next day you leave three-dimensional geometry materials near the blocks. Do not assign them. A few days later — he picks them up. He has no idea he is doing mathematics. That is the point.

09
The equity provocation · who you call on teaches the room

Your classroom practices either reinforce or interrupt the stereotypes children have already absorbed.

When you tell a girl of color she is a mathematical thinker, you are not just giving information. You are working against everything she may have been told about who is good at mathematics.

Not empty praise. Seeing her actual thinking — and naming it clearly, repeatedly — so she can internalise it against the messages.

Who do you call on? Who do you sit with? Who do you give harder work to? You are teaching it — even if you never say a word.

10
Neurodivergence note · do not assume motivation or character

A child with ADHD may appear not to care — when they are hyperfocusing on something else and cannot shift.

A child with anxiety may appear lazy — when they are in a physiological stress response.

Assume there is a reason. Your job is to figure out what it is.

11
The body in resistance · it is not laziness

Resistance is a physiology before it is a behaviour.

Heart rate up. Breath shallow. Shoulders tight. Stomach knotted. The child you are looking at may not be choosing to refuse the work. Their body is refusing it first.

Nothing you teach will land until that settles.

A glass of water. A walk to the garden. A different seat. Regulation before instruction. Every time.

12
The family story · is often in the room

Ask — gently — what mathematics has meant in this family.

A grandmother who was told she was stupid. A parent who still jokes about failing algebra. An older sibling held up as “the smart one.”

The child has inherited this before they met you. They are not blank.

Knowing this does not fix it. But it stops you from taking their refusal personally. And that changes what you do next.

13
Small re-entries · how the door opens again

Not a triumph. A doorway.

Through the body

Cooking. Building. Weighing. Measuring for a project they chose. Mathematics with a purpose they own.

Through a story

A historical problem. An unsolved puzzle. The number the hero needed to know.

Through a peer

An older child showing them. A partner who does not judge. Work done sitting close to someone safe.

Through smallness

One problem. Not a worksheet. One. Done. Put away. Tomorrow, one more.

Scale down until yes becomes possible.

14
What not to say · words that close the door

Some sentences feel kind and do harm.

“It’s okay, you don’t have to be good at math.” — confirms what they fear.

“I was bad at math too.” — makes it a family trait.

“Just try harder.” — assumes the problem is effort, not access.

“We have not found your door yet. We will.” — is the sentence that keeps the work alive.

15
A return plan · written, specific, patient

Resistance needs a plan, not a hope.

Name the child. Name the last moment it worked. Name a small next step. Name when you will try. Name who else will know.

Write it down. Share it with a colleague. Revisit in two weeks.

Improvisation is generous. But a child in a hard season needs the evidence that an adult has a plan.
