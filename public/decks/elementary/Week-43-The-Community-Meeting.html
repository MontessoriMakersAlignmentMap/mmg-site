Residency · Week Forty-Three · Elementary

The community meeting
is not a teacher explaining why children were wrong.
It is what happens when you actually believe
children are capable of justice
and should practice it.

Montessori Makers Residency Week 43 · Elementary · Month 11 · Behaviour, Community, Capstone
Why this matters right now

In most schools, justice happens top-down. An adult sees. An adult judges. An adult decides. Children learn their role is compliance, not participation.

In a Montessori elementary classroom, justice should happen in the community. Children talking to each other. Understanding each other. Repairing damage. Moving forward together.

This is not a behaviour strategy. This is practice for democratic citizenship.

02
What a community meeting actually is

Not a punishment. Not a shaming. A town hall. A community process. The child equivalent of collective problem-solving.

It is not

Theatre. Performance. A meeting where children talk but nothing changes.

Not an adult explaining what was wrong. Not a manipulated path to a predetermined conclusion.

It is

Naming what happened. Understanding the impact. Deciding how to repair it and move forward.

Called for harm. Called for broken norms. Sometimes called for something beautiful the community wants to celebrate.

03
The condition that makes it real

It only works if children believe they will actually be heard. That their voices will actually matter. That adults will not steer toward a predetermined conclusion.

If it is performance, it teaches children that their participation is meaningless.

If it is real — if what they say actually shapes what happens next — it is powerful.

04
The guide’s role

The guide facilitates. The guide does not decide what happens next. The community does.

Guide does

Name the issue. Ask questions. Keep focus on understanding, not blame.

Make sure the harmed are heard. Protect anyone from disproportionate shaming.

Guide does not

Pronounce verdicts. Impose outcomes. Steer toward “the right answer.”

Does not hold the power. The community holds the power.

05
The structure · four movements
01
Name

What happened — without judgement.

Not she did something wrong. This is what happened; this is affecting our community. Invite perspectives. Goal is understanding, not agreement.

02
Impact

Who is harmed. How.

The community develops empathy. Hearing it from the person harmed lands differently than being told by an adult.

03
Repair

What would actually help.

Not an imposed consequence — a conversation. What does the harmed need? What can the person who caused harm offer?

04
Return

Integrate back into the community.

The person who caused harm is still part of this. The person harmed knows the community still cares about her safety.

06
Scene · the meeting opens
Guide
This is what has been happening. A group of you has been excluding one of us. She has asked for this meeting.
Child A
We thought she wasn’t fun. We didn’t think she could play our game.
Child B
We were being funny.
Child C
She was unkind to me once. So I decided to be unkind back.
07
Scene · impact
Guide
I want to ask her what her experience has been.
Excluded child
I feel like I’m invisible. I try to play. Nobody lets me. I don’t understand what I did that was so bad. I feel like nobody likes me.
Guide
I want you to imagine what that feels like. Really imagine it.
08
Scene · repair
Child
We could try playing together at recess.
Child
We could invite her to our game tomorrow.
Child
I’m sorry.
Excluded child
I accept your apology. I would like to play with you.
09
After the meeting

The guide does not know if this will stick. She probably knows it will not completely stick.

There will be moments when the exclusion happens again.

But something has shifted. The children have named it. Thought about impact. Decided to try something different. And practiced being a community that repairs harm.

10
When the problem is not the individual — it is the community

A child has been excluded — and the exclusion is rooted in race, class, gender, or disability. The out-group has a demographic pattern. You cannot pretend it is not there.

Name it. The community has been excluding based on identity.

If you don’t, you have just taught children that bias is fine as long as everyone agrees to it.

11
When children defend the bias

They will give you reasons. The reasons will make sense to them. They will be wrong. And they will feel right.

Hold them in that discomfort. Having reasons for bias does not make it not bias.

Help them see the person they have excluded as a full human, not a category.

12
What is actually being taught

Every community meeting is a rehearsal for how democracy works — and how it does not.

·
Listening

What genuine listening sounds like.

Not just waiting to speak.

·
Disagreeing

How to disagree without dismissing.

The other person still belongs.

·
Solving together

How to hold a shared problem.

No one solves alone.

·
Responsibility

Voice matters. So does the cost of using it well.

The community is yours.

13
The equity provocation

Community meetings are a tool of power. A way to protect children from marginalised communities from the bias baked into top-down discipline.

But only if they are actually democratic. If adults manipulate them toward predetermined conclusions, they are just a different kind of exclusion.

You have to actually be willing to be surprised. You have to actually let children have power.

14
Neurodivergence note · participation, not performance

Community meetings can be challenging for neurodivergent children. Selective mutism. Sensory load. Time demands.

Offer alternative ways to participate. A written note read aloud. A one-on-one beforehand. A designated moment of quiet. Permission to leave and return.

The goal is not to exclude them from the process. It is to find ways for them to genuinely participate.

15
The circle itself · geometry as pedagogy

Sit in a circle. Not a grid.

A grid teaches hierarchy. One face at the front. Many faces looking the same way. A circle teaches something else — every face is seen, every face is equidistant from the middle, no one is at the back.

Move the furniture. Every time. That small labour of setting up is part of the practice.

The shape of the room is already teaching, before anyone speaks.
