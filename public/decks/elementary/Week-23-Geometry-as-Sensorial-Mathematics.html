Montessori Makers Residency Week 23 · Elementary
Why this matters right now

In Primary, geometry is sensorial. Feeling shape and proportion and size.

In Elementary, that sensorial knowledge becomes the foundation for geometric thinking.

Children move from sensing a cylinder — to understanding cylindrical properties — to eventually proving theorems about cylinders.

But the proof starts with the sensing.

02
Definition is not knowledge

Abstract geometric knowledge without sensorial grounding is empty.

Only the definition

“A three-dimensional shape with two circular bases and a curved lateral surface.”

The child has a definition. That is all they have.

Sensed first

They have felt a cylinder roll. Traced its edges. Stacked one on another.

The child has real knowledge. The words arrive onto something already understood.

03
The sensorial work is not frivolous

It is building spatial reasoning — what Montessori called the child’s sensibility to form.

Neural pathways around shape and proportion. Cognitive infrastructure that supports all later mathematical learning.

You cannot develop strong spatial reasoning by only looking at symbols. You have to move in space. Manipulate objects. Feel how they fit together.

04
The arc · sensation to proof

Each step depends on the previous one.

01 · Sense

Touch the form.

Primary · no instruction

Trace the shape. Place one inside another. Notice how they fit. Difference without words.

02 · Name

The word arrives onto the sensation.

Early Elementary

Cylinder. Vocabulary for what they have been sensing all along.

03 · Properties

Why does it roll? Why can it stack?

Elementary

Two circular bases. A curved surface. If these properties — then these behaviours.

04 · Prove

A formal way of describing what is already known.

Eventually · high school

Not abstract symbol manipulation. A proof you can trust, because the thing being proved is one you’ve lived with.

05
Two ways to learn geometry · the bridge between them

Elementary is the bridge. From inductive sensing to deductive reasoning.

Inductive · observation → principle

Every cylinder I have touched rolls this way. Every square has four equal sides.

Many experiences. Then a general principle begins to form.

Deductive · axiom → theorem

If you have these properties, then these behaviours follow.

The formal world of proofs. Euclid. High school geometry.

06
Why so many children decide they are bad at geometry

They missed the first plane. They arrived at proof without ever having touched the form.

So they experience formal geometry as meaningless symbol-pushing. They memorize proofs without understanding why they matter.

And they conclude — geometry is boring. I am not good at it.

The real problem is not their mind. It is what was missing before the mind was asked.

First-graders in elementary, working with the geometric cabinet for the first time this year. The shapes are laid out. The guide asks — “which shapes can roll?” Cylinder, sphere, cone. “Why?” “They’re round.” “The sphere is round all over. The cylinder is round but flat on top and bottom.” They are beginning to notice properties. Later: trace a circle, trace a square. “How do your fingers move?” “The circle is smooth.” “The square has corners.” They are translating tactile experience into understanding.

08
What a proof becomes, with this foundation

A proof is not abstract symbol manipulation. It is a formal way of describing something they already know deeply.

That is what allows children to actually understand geometry — instead of just memorizing proofs.

09
For the child whose mind does not work in symbols

Some children will get geometry taught purely deductively — because their minds work that way. Many will get lost.

Those children need the sensorial bridge.

Once the sensorial foundation is there — the deductive work becomes much more accessible to them.

10
The equity provocation · who gets to be “good at geometry”

Geometry is often treated as a subject for the mathematically talented. Spatial reasoning is something every child can develop.

A child who struggles with arithmetic may be brilliant at spatial reasoning. A slow reader may see geometric relationships instantly.

If you only teach geometry in the abstract, you will only reach the children whose minds already work in abstract symbols.

Ground it in the sensorial. Open it to more children.

11
Neurodivergence note · geometry can be the surprise

Some children you thought struggled with math are geometric thinkers waiting for you to notice.

Visual-spatial thinkers for whom geometry is more accessible than arithmetic. Some autistic children with exceptional geometric thinking.

Children with attention challenges who flourish with hands-on geometric exploration more than with abstract math.

Some will need more time with sensorial materials before the abstract makes sense. Give them the time.

12
Naming · the hand knows before the word arrives

The word follows the sensation. Never leads it.

Let them handle the scalene triangle for an hour before they hear the word. Let the shape be known by the fingertips first.

Then, when you say “scalene” — it does not feel like a test. It feels like a name for something they already know.

Vocabulary lands on a body that recognises it.

13
Small case study · the triangle, all the way through

One shape. Five years of deepening.

Age 3 · 4

They trace the triangle insets. The hand learns the angle before the mouth names it.

Age 6 · 7

They sort triangles by sides, then by angles. They meet “equilateral” as a word for what they already grouped.

Age 8 · 9

They cut off the corners of a paper triangle and place them in a line. The angles sum to a straight line. They have just proved something.

Age 10 · 11

They write the proof formally. It feels like naming something known — not learning something new.

That is the arc you are holding.

14
What to watch for · in the child with the material

Before you decide what to offer next, read the body.

Does the hand hesitate at a vertex, or move through it? Do they turn the shape, or accept one orientation?

Do they put two triangles together to see what they make — without being asked?

That unasked experiment is the readiness for abstraction. Name it. Give them the next material.

15
Your own relationship with geometry · is in the room

Most of you were taught geometry as a set of theorems to memorise.

You may have been quick, or you may have been lost. Either way, you probably did not touch the shapes. The shapes arrived on paper, already abstract.

That experience is in your body when you sit down with a child and the constructive triangles.

Notice it. Do not let it rush you — or rush the child.
