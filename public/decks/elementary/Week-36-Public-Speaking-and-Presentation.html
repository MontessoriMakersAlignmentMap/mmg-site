Montessori Makers Residency Week 36 · Elementary · Month 9 · Language Deep Dive
The first distinction that matters

Presentation is not performance.

Performance

Separation. The performer and the audience.

The authority figure and the people being tested. The one who knows and the ones who are learning.

Presentation

We are all learning. We are all listening. We are all curious.

The presenter is not being evaluated. The presenter is sharing. A completely different energy.

02
The culture is built · over months

Listeners respond with genuine curiosity — not evaluation.

01 · Rhythm

A regular part of the work — not a test.

Sharing learning is part of learning.

02 · Questions

“What did you discover that surprised you?”

Not “good job.” Real interest.

03 · Many kinds

Research, music, drawings, stories, demonstrations.

Many ways to participate.

04 · Scale

Small first. Two peers. One friend.

Lower the stakes. Confidence builds.

03
Some children will always have some anxiety · work with it

The goal is not to eliminate the anxiety. The goal is to help her move forward despite it.

Choice about format. A child who cannot look at the audience might present to a poster. A child who stammers might work with a peer. A child who freezes might present to a small group first.

These are not ways of avoiding the difficulty. They are ways of removing unnecessary barriers — so she can participate authentically.

04
Reframe what the anxiety means

Her heart is racing. She is thinking clearly. Her body is ready. This is not dangerous. This is activation.

“I see you are nervous. That makes sense. You are about to share something important. Your body is getting ready. You have practised. You know this.”

“Let me stand near you and listen.” You are not dismissing the feeling. You are staying with her. You are helping her move through it.

05
A child terrified of speaking in front of the group — but she has completed a beautiful research project on her grandmother’s immigration story. She does not want to present to the whole class. You offer options. She chooses: a small group of three peers. No adults. She speaks. Her voice is shaky at first. But she gets into the story. The other children listen intently. When she finishes — they ask questions about her grandmother. They want to see the photographs. They want to know more. She is not thinking about performing. She is sharing something precious. She discovers that her voice matters — that her story has power — that people want to hear it. That is the beginning.

06
Honour diverse communication styles

When you require one format, you privilege one way of communicating.

What gets lost

You send the message that some ways of thinking are not valuable.

The children who think visually, who move, who need silence to think — go unheard.

The wider frame

Visual art with spoken explanation. A dialogue with another student. Movement. Music. A skill taught. Powerful questions.

Still structure — but flexible enough to honour how she thinks.

07
Beyond the classroom · a world that needs her voice

She will need to advocate for herself. Challenge injustice. Ask for what she needs. Take up space.

This is not a luxury skill. This is necessary for survival and thriving.

In elementary you are building the foundation — teaching that public speaking is not performance. It is a way of contributing to collective knowledge. Taking your place as a thinking person in a community of thinking people.

08
The equity provocation · whose voice is valued

Voice and presence are not equally valued in schools.

Children from dominant groups are more likely to be encouraged to speak. Children from marginalised groups are more likely to be told to be quiet — not so emotional, not to take up so much space.

Explicitly sometimes. Implicitly through who gets called on, whose comments are repeated and credited, whose interruptions are tolerated.

Teach children from marginalised groups that their voice matters. That they have a right to take up space. That they need not wait for permission.

09
Neurodivergence note · not deficits — different ways

ADHD — high-energy, non-linear. Autism — meticulous, reading notes word-for-word. Deaf — in ASL with an interpreter.

These are not deficits in presentation. These are different ways of communicating. Honour them.

10
Preparation · the rituals before the room

Calm is taught. Not wished for.

Stand. Feet flat. Three slow breaths. Feel the floor. Look at the back wall. Find one friendly face.

Teach the sequence. Rehearse it so often the child can do it without thinking. On the day of the presentation — she just does the thing.

A ritual is a handrail in the dark. She reaches for it. It holds.

11
The pause · more eloquent than any word

Slower than you think. Then slower.

Nervous speakers race. A child who learns to pause — just one full second after an important sentence — commands the room.

Teach her — “silence is not failure. The silence is where your idea lands.”

Rehearse by counting — one elephant, two elephants — after each key line. It feels eternal. It is right.

12
The body speaks · before the voice does

Four small things. Taught. Practised.

Stand still

Shifting from foot to foot reads as anxiety. Plant your feet. Let your hands rest.

Find three faces

One left. One right. One centre. Move between them. The room feels seen.

Lift the chin

Not proud — open. The voice travels further. The words carry further.

Hands out of pockets

A small kindness to future self. Hands by the sides, or resting — not hidden.

13
Micro talks · two minutes, twice a week

Practice many small stakes — not one big one.

Two minutes. A favourite object. A page from the book. A question they are sitting with. Every child. Twice a week. All year.

By May, the ten-minute presentation to parents is not a performance. It is a larger version of something they have done one hundred times.

The stakes you protect children from at nine become the stakes that break them at fourteen.

14
Answering questions · the real test

“That’s a good question…” is not an answer.

Teach her three real sentences. “I don’t know — I’ll find out.” “Let me think for a moment.” “Can you ask that again, a different way?”

Rehearse the Q&A as much as you rehearse the talk. Often more.

The talk is prepared. The questions are real. Real is the harder skill.

15
The mistake · the most important moment to teach

She will lose her place. Teach her what to do then.

Breathe. Smile. Look at her notes. Find the thread. Carry on. No apology needed. No “sorry, I’m so bad at this.”

The adults she sees recover with grace — that is the script she will borrow for life.

Rehearse the mistake in advance. On purpose. It is the most generous rehearsal you can give her.
