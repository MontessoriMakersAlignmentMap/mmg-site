Montessori Makers Residency Week 21 · Elementary
Why this matters right now

The midpoint is not a celebration. It is a moment to notice what has shifted.

You have moved through Biology and most of History. Mathematics is coming.

You are supposed to know how you have changed so far — and what preparation you need for what is coming. You are supposed to be noticing what kind of guide you are becoming.

02
What the first six months built

Half a year studying the child.

How they develop.

Biologically. At the second plane. What they reach toward emotionally.

What the materials do.

Golden beads. The shift from concrete to abstract. Faces when ten became a unit.

Their questions as windows.

Why things are. Where they come from. Not interruptions — invitations.

The reality of inequity.

Curriculum as a choice about whose knowledge matters.

Your own conditioning.

What you were taught. What you believe because you were taught it. What you need to unlearn.

Standing beside without controlling.

Prepare the environment. Then step back.

03
The frustration is the beginning

The child is not doing what you planned. That frustration is the beginning of wisdom.

The guide’s job is not to drive learning. It is to prepare the environment — and then step back.

That is a complete reversal of most teaching you have ever seen.

04
Your gifts. Your edges. Both are data.

Neither makes you a better or worse guide. They make you a guide who knows themself.

Where you are strong

Keep building there.

Patience. Energy. Intuition about what children need. Notice what you bring — and use it.

Where you struggle

Ask for help. Work on growth.

Not a weakness to hide. A location to work from.

05
The second six months · what they will require

Mathematics demands precision in a way that biology did not.

One wrong explanation builds into fundamental misunderstanding. You are going to have to know the materials well enough to troubleshoot when a child gets stuck.

Understand not just what to present — but why each material is designed the way it is.

That requires rigor.

06
Less presenting · more facilitating

A different kind of presence. Resisting the urge to help.

Watch children work.

Independently and in small groups. Support without taking over.

Notice when help is actually needed.

The difference between a child who needs intervention — and one who needs more time.

Going Out is coming.

Facilitating research in the neighborhood and world. Safety, agency, relationships beyond the classroom.

07
And documentation · how you honour the work

How do you know what a child has learned? What they are ready for next?

This will feel bureaucratic sometimes. It is.

It is also how you honour children’s learning — by making it visible and intentional.

08
Notice how you have changed

If you are paying attention, you are changing.

How you talk to children.

Different questions. Different pace.

What feels important.

Things that seemed urgent before — no longer do.

Readiness as a moving target.

You used to judge a child for not being ready. Now you just see readiness as moving.

Lecture → question.

You used to lecture. Now you ask.

The materials as partners.

Not as props. As co-teachers you serve.

Surprise as success.

More willing to let children surprise you.

09
Your own childhood conditioning · still in you

Work with what you find. Not against it.

If you were punished for not knowing

You may be harsh with yourself when you do not know the materials.

Work with that.

If you were rewarded for being busy

Sitting with a slow, thoughtful child may make you anxious.

That is information.

If your emotional needs were unmet

You may over-give to children as a way of healing your own past.

That is real and human — and also something to become aware of.

Becoming

More intentional. More grounded. More confident about what you do not know.

Not separate from who you have always been — but new.

11
The equity provocation · not optional

You cannot be an equity-centered guide without the internal work.

Noticing how the world taught you to see people differently based on race, class, ability, gender.

Some of you started already. Some of you are about to. It is going to be uncomfortable. It is supposed to be.

Do it anyway.

12
Neurodivergence note · your own, as a guide

Knowing yourself helps you separate your own needs from what children need.

If you are ADHD, you might be energized by chaos and struggle with the stillness of Montessori.

If you are autistic, you might crave order and struggle with children’s individual paces.

Both are real. Both are workable — once you know them.

13
The arc · month by month, what moved

Not a straight line. A deepening.

Month 1 · 2

You were learning the materials. Memorising sequences. Surviving the day.

The room was louder than you wanted. You talked too much.

Month 3 · 4

You began to see children. Not the group. The child in front of you.

You started trusting the albums less, your observation more.

Month 5

Something cracked. A week felt hard. You doubted yourself.

That doubt was the work arriving. Not the work failing.

Month 6 · now

You ask questions instead of giving answers. You wait longer than you used to.

That is not a skill. That is a formation.

14
One practice · to begin the second six months

Before you speak to a child this week, count to four.

Four seconds of watching before you intervene. Four seconds of silence before you answer.

You will be astonished by how often the child does not need you.

Small practice. Large reorientation.

15
What the children will notice · in you, now

They have watched you for six months. They already know.

They know whose face you light up for. They know whose struggle you sit with, and whose you fix.

They know when you are performing calm — and when you are calm.

Let that be information. Not shame.
