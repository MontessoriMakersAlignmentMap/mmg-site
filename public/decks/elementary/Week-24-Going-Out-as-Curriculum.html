Montessori Makers Residency Week 24 · Elementary
Why this matters right now

The second plane is the age of community consciousness.

Around six or seven, children become aware of the wider world beyond family and classroom. How society works. How communities function. What roles different people have.

Going Out is how you feed this interest. How you move curriculum from the classroom into the real world.

02
The distinction · not a field trip

A field trip is teacher-led. Going Out is student-led — with the guide supporting before and after.

Field trip

Teacher drives. Whole class. One agenda.

Transportation and observation. A break from curriculum.

Going Out

Children drive. Small groups. A research question.

Real investigation. Extension of curriculum.

The principle

Deliberate research. Not arbitrary tourism.

The destination connects to what is being studied. Every outing has a question.

03
Where the guide works · before, during, after

The guide’s job is preparation and follow-up — not management during.

01 · Before

Prepare everything.

  • Choose a destination that connects to the study
  • Set the research question
  • Practice interviewing, sketching, observing
  • Teach the scenarios — what if I get lost?
  • Reach out to the business or site. Get permission.
  • Equip children with tools and trust.
02 · During

You are not with them.

  • Stay at school · or
  • Neutral check-in spot nearby · or
  • Parallel Going Out at the same site
  • The children are leading the research.
  • You are supporting from the background.
03 · After

Now the guide’s work becomes intense.

  • “What did you find out?”
  • “What surprised you?”
  • “How does this connect to what we have been studying?”
  • “What do you want to know more about?”
  • Help them move from raw observation to understanding.
04
Preparing children for independent community research

Four things they need before you release them.

01 · Know the neighborhood

Major streets. Landmarks. How to cross safely.

Built across the year. Not in a single Going Out.

02 · Know the question

Not “go look at things.”

How is the library organised? What animals live in the park? What do people do to help the community?

03 · Prepare for the specific

Practice the thing they will do.

How to ask and listen. How to move near an animal. What details matter in the sketch.

04 · A way to record

Notebook. Map. Checklist. Camera.

The tool depends on the research. What matters is that findings can be captured.

The scary part

You are releasing children into the world.

You have prepared them. Equipped them. Given them a meaningful question. And you trust them.

05
The class studies community helpers. Teacher-led Going Out to the local fire station. Firefighters talk. Children see the trucks. They ask questions. Back at school: “how do firefighters help the community?” Then — children choose next steps. One wants to sketch the truck and plans a follow-up Going Out. Another wants to interview a firefighter’s family member about what it is like to have a parent in that job. A third decides to research fire safety and walks the school checking exits. The initial Going Out opened a door. The children are now leading their own research.

A class studying water. Guide takes them to a nearby stream. Is the water moving? What lives here? Clean or dirty? They sketch. Collect samples for the microscope back at school. Back in classroom: where does this water go? Where does it come from? A small group becomes fascinated and wants to follow the stream upstream. The guide gives permission for a carefully planned independent Going Out. They trace the stream. Find a spring. Come back amazed.

07
After · help them translate experience into knowledge

Children come back with information, observations, questions.

Facilitate the processing. Presentations. Books. Posters. Writing. Translation of experience into something that can be shared.

This is how a Going Out becomes part of what the classroom knows. Otherwise it dissipates.

08
The equity provocation · Going Out is not equal

A child in an affluent neighborhood has more options than a child in a neighborhood without those resources.

A child whose family knows shopkeepers is more comfortable approaching adults than a child whose family is marginalised by the community they live in. A child whose home language is English will interview more easily than a child learning English.

Create Going Outs that honour the neighborhood the children actually live in — not the one you wish they lived in.

Teach children to move through their neighborhood with pride, not shame.

09
This requires work beyond the classroom

Build relationships between your school and your community. So community members see the children as valued researchers — not as problems.

The baker. The librarian. The mechanic. The city worker. You are building a network of adults who will welcome children with questions.

That is not incidental to the work. That is the work.

10
Neurodivergence note · some children will soar, some will shrink

All of this is data about how that child learns best.

The anxious child.

Shorter. More structured. With a trusted peer. Start small.

The child who needs predictability.

Clear start and end points. Advance warning of transitions. A script in their pocket.

The one who soars.

Some children will thrive in the chaos and freedom of Going Out in ways they never do in the classroom.

11
The unglamorous layer · logistics that make the work possible

Independence rests on infrastructure you have built.

Permission

Blanket family consent at the start of the year, not a signed slip every Tuesday.

Communication

A shared phone, a notebook at the front desk, a known time of return.

Money

A small fund they can draw from — bus fare, an admission, a photocopy at the library.

Rehearsal

They have practiced the phone call, the request, the transaction — before the real one.

Freedom rides on preparation. Not on luck.

12
When a Going Out does not work · what that means

A Going Out that fails is still a Going Out.

They got lost. They missed the curator. They argued the whole way there. They came back without the answer.

The work is in what they do with that — not in protecting them from it.

“What will you try differently next time?” is the only question that matters.

13
Your fear · is the obstacle, not the safeguard

Most Going Outs do not happen because the guide was afraid.

Afraid something would go wrong. Afraid a parent would complain. Afraid the child was not ready — when really, you were not.

Name the fear. Say it aloud to a colleague. Do not let it quietly become policy.

Your anxiety is not the same as a child’s unreadiness.

15
Who pays · so that every child can go

If a child cannot go because of five dollars, the school has failed them.

Transport. Admissions. A sandwich because they forgot one. These are not extras. These are the curriculum.

Build a Going Out fund. Make it invisible to the child. Never let them feel the shape of the absence.

A child noticing they could not afford to go — learns something you do not want them to learn.
