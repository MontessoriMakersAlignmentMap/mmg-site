You are graduating from the academic phase.
Now we need to be clear about what we are asking of you.

Montessori Makers Residency Week 51 · Elementary · Month 12 · Capstone
The academic phase and the practicum are different things

You can know Montessori philosophy and still be confused when you are actually in it — with real children, real families, and problems that do not have simple solutions.

The transition into practicum is real. It is not going to be smooth.

You are going to be confused. You are going to feel like you do not know what you are doing. You are going to have moments where you question everything you have learned. That is exactly right. That is how you become a real teacher.

02
What completing the academic phase means

You have the foundation. You are not ready. You are never ready until you are actually in a classroom.

Child development. The Montessori approach. The second plane. The materials. The philosophy. It is in your head and in your heart.

You are not going to understand something and then apply it and get it right the first time. You are going to encounter it in practice and have your understanding deepen and change.

03
What you have committed to

You have thought about what you believe. You have interrogated your own biases. You have committed to equity and justice.

You have spent months reading and thinking and discussing and questioning. You have done hard work. You have probably cried. You have questioned things you took for granted.

That work is real and it matters.

04
What the practicum is really for at the second plane

The practicum is not about getting good at Montessori. You will spend your whole career getting better at Montessori.

The practicum is about learning who you are as a teacher — and what your impact is.

Practice being intentional. Practice making decisions based on what you believe rather than what feels comfortable. Practice being responsive to children without losing yourself in the process.

Learn to notice your impact. Ask whether the impact you are having is the impact you want to have.

05
You see a snapshot, not a full arc

In Primary, you might teach the same age group for three years. In Elementary, you might only teach a child for one year. You will see a snapshot.

That snapshot is not enough to teach from instinct alone. You have to be intentional and thoughtful.

06
What kind of guide MMR is sending into classrooms

Guides who understand development, the Montessori approach, and who are committed to equity and justice.

Not
Guides who think they already know what they are doing.
But
Guides who know they have a lot to learn.
Not
Guides who replicate the classrooms they observed.
But
Guides who adapt and create and think. Every classroom is different.
Not
Guides who ignore the culture and community around them.
But
Guides who learn from families and the community about what children need.
Not
Guides who are colourblind or pretend that inequity does not exist.
But
Guides who understand inequity is real — and who are intentional about building a just classroom.
Not
Guides who think they are supposed to have all the answers.
But
Guides who know how to ask questions, how to be confused, how to say “I do not know” — and let that not-knowing inform their teaching.
07
The work of teaching is not what you think it is

It is not about delivering curriculum. It is about relationships.

Building a community where children feel safe and known and valued.

Regulating your own nervous system so you can be a regulating presence.

Noticing when a child is struggling and knowing how to respond.

Believing in children — even when they do not believe in themselves.

08
The work is hard. The work is also the most important work.

You will be tired. You will question everything. You will fail. You will wonder why you are doing this.

Those moments are real. We want you to know that. We want you to know that it is normal.

You are shaping human beings. You are helping children understand that they belong, that they matter, that their voice has value. You are either reinforcing that their community and culture are valuable — or you are teaching them that other people’s are more valuable.

That matters. It matters a lot.

09
What we are holding for you

We have watched you think and question and grow. We know who you are.

We know

You are committed to children.

You are thinking about justice.

We know

You are working hard to interrogate your own biases and beliefs.

That work is not finished. That work is never finished.

We know

You will show up. You will think carefully. You will adapt. You will care deeply.

That is enough.

10
You do not have to be perfect

You do not have to know everything. You do not have to do everything right. You have to be willing.

Willing to learn. Willing to think. Willing to change.

And to do it with grace, with integrity, and with genuine care for children.

11
What now

You are going into a classroom. You will be terrified and excited and confused.

You will make mistakes. You will learn things that surprise you. You will have moments of beautiful connection with children.

You will question your decisions. You will change your mind. You will become a teacher.

This is what you are ready for. Not to be perfect. To be present. To be thoughtful. To be committed to children and to justice. To be willing to grow.

12
Commitments in writing · the document for the year ahead

Three commitments. Written. Visible. Re-read monthly.

Not resolutions. Commitments. “I will observe one child in silence for twenty minutes each week.” “I will re-read the Great Lessons before every telling.” “I will take ten minutes at day’s end to write one true line.”

Pin them above the desk. A commitment unseen is a commitment unmade.

Most of this year will be forgotten. The three things you pin to the wall will not.

13
The community you carry · the cohort becomes the colleagues

The people in this room are now your colleagues for life.

Save their numbers. Trade classroom photos. Call one when you have a hard week. Send a question. Send a drawing from a child. Send a small victory.

The profession is lonelier than you expect. This cohort is already the cure. Use it.

A voice message from someone who watched you grow this year — on the hardest Tuesday in November — will mean more than any textbook on your shelf.

14
The hundred-day principle · do not change anything for a hundred days

First observe. Then intervene.

Your first instinct, arriving in a new classroom, will be to rearrange. To improve. To implement. Resist for a hundred days. Watch. Learn the existing ecology. Earn the right to change anything.

The lead guide has reasons you do not yet see. The children have habits you do not yet understand. The room was working before you arrived.

Humility in the first term is what gives you credibility in the second.

15
Returning to us · the door does not close

You are a graduate. You are still in formation.

Come back in three years. Come back in ten. Return with questions the classroom has shown you. Return with children you want to bring. Return when something is breaking.

The residency does not end with graduation. It simply moves into a different rhythm.

We remain — the building, the library, the older colleagues, the tradition. Use us.

16
Benediction

Go teach.
Listen to the children.
Listen to the families.
Listen to what the classroom teaches you.
Trust yourself.
And know that we believe in you.

17
The year, in one line

You do not become a teacher by reading about it. You become a teacher by doing it — by failing, by learning from what the classroom teaches you.
