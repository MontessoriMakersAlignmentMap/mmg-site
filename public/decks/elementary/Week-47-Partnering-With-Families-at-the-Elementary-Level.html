Residency · Week Forty-Seven · Elementary

Family partnership at the elementary level is not about communication.
It is about building genuine relationships across the differences that are real.

Montessori Makers Residency Week 47 · Elementary · Month 12 · Behaviour, Community, Capstone
Why this gets harder in Elementary

In Primary, the program is obviously beautiful. In Elementary, fewer materials. More abstraction. More work that does not look like what families expect learning to look like.

A parent sees her child reading a novel instead of doing math facts. Working in a group instead of working alone. Not being tested.

When families have experienced school as sorting — or have good reason to doubt systems — this is when partnership becomes hard. And essential.

02
What changes between Primary and Elementary

You may not be working with families who chose this. You may be working with families who inherited it — and have every right to question it.

Primary

Families often chose Montessori. Visible development. Visible beauty.

Buy-in is often already there.

Elementary

Families may be new, skeptical, or navigating constrained choice.

Development is internal. You cannot show them mathematical thinking — you have to talk about it and show evidence.

03
Relationship begins with knowing

Not knowing about families. Knowing them.

What they care about. Their worries. What they hope for their child. Their history. How systems have treated them. What makes them who they are.

It takes time. It takes vulnerability. It requires you to show up as a real person, not as a role.

04
Building relationships across real difference

If you come from privilege and the family comes from marginalisation, that difference is real. If your educational tradition differs from theirs, that is real. If you have resources and they are struggling, that is real.

You do not erase these differences. You name them. You take responsibility for your own position. You listen to how the differences show up in the family’s experience.

And you find common ground. You both care about the child. You both have hopes and fears. That is real, even when the context is very different.

05
The surface disagreement is not the real disagreement

Your first instinct may be to defend. Your first step is to listen — for what the family is actually worried about.

What the family says

“You are not teaching enough math.”

The surface sentence is not the surface concern.

What she is actually saying

My child will not be prepared for what comes next.

The fear underneath the sentence.

What the family says

“I have a concern about that book.”

The objection is real. So is what is underneath it.

What she is actually saying

My values are not being respected.

Respect is the question. The book is the doorway.

06
Four moves when a family disagrees

Defence loses the conversation. Listening wins back the relationship.

01 · Listen

What is the family actually concerned about?

Before you explain anything.

02 · Source

What experience or belief is that concern rooted in?

Usually not ignorance. Usually history.

03 · Ask

What is the family actually asking for?

Often smaller — and more reasonable — than the fight suggests.

04 · Decide

Modify the practice — or ask the family to trust the process.

Sometimes the concern is legitimate and you change. That is not losing. That is partnership.

07
Scene · she does not see math worksheets coming home

You do not explain philosophy. You show evidence.
The work her daughter has done.
The concepts she has been exploring.
The problem-solving. The mathematical reasoning.

She is not completely convinced. She is less worried. You ask what would help. She asks for a sample. You offer updates. She leaves feeling more confident that math is happening.

08
Scene · religious or cultural concern

You listen without defending. You ask questions. You explain why the material is in the curriculum.
But you listen for whether the concern is legitimate.
Maybe the family is not asking you to remove. Maybe they are asking you to also include.

You can do that. You are not erasing your curriculum. You are expanding it.

09
Communication that actually reaches

Replace the abstract claim with the specific, visible thing her child did.

Abstract

“Children are learning independence.”

True. And unseeable. It slides off.

Concrete

“This is the choice your daughter made about her work on Tuesday.”

A photograph. A moment. A sentence she can picture.

Abstract

“Children are learning collaboration.”

Also true. Also invisible.

Concrete

“Last week your son helped his group solve a problem when they were stuck.”

A story. A specific hour. A specific child.

10
Speak to what the family cares about — not what you wish they cared about

If she cares about academic preparation, show evidence of academic learning.

If she cares about her child having friends, show evidence of social growth.

If she cares about her child being happy, show evidence of joy and engagement.

Meet the family where they are — not where you think they ought to be.

11
Equity provocation · the assumption you have to hold

Authentic partnership requires you to assume that families know their children. That their concerns come from love and legitimate experience — not from ignorance or lack of care.

This is easier when the family looks like you. It is much harder with families from different cultures, different languages, families who have been harmed by systems.

This is exactly the work. This is exactly when you have to assume good faith and build real relationship.

12
Neurodivergence note · listen for this specific family

A family might be grieving. A family might be celebrating. A family might be braced for bullying. A family might be at the start of understanding their child.

Do not assume the stance. Listen to what this family, today, is actually carrying.

13
The home visit · the meeting you should make first

Sit on their sofa before they sit on yours.

A twenty-minute visit to a family’s home, before the school year begins, changes everything. The parent is the host. You are the guest. The power has shifted. The child is watching.

Offer it. Not as a demand. As a small act. Tea. Five questions. Gratitude.

Every conversation for the next nine months is easier because of it.

14
Translation · beyond words, into worldview

“Follow the child” is not self-translating. Neither is “freedom within limits.”

Many families hear “follow the child” as permissiveness. Many hear “freedom” as an absence of standards. Both interpretations are fair given the words.

Translate — into specifics. “By March, she will be doing long multiplication on paper.” “She is reading forty-five minutes a day.” Concrete. Observable.

Philosophy is what we live by. Specifics are what families need to trust us.

15
When family and school want different things

The family wants more homework. Say yes to the question underneath.

The question under the homework request is almost always — “is my child going to be ready?” Answer that question. Not the homework one.

Show her notebook. Show her maths. Show her writing. Walk the family through it. The question usually resolves when the parent sees what is already happening.

Refuse the letter of the request. Honour the care behind it.

Closing question

For your own formation.

How were your own parents treated when you were in school?

What did they know about your learning? What did they not know?

How do you want to be different as you partner with families?
