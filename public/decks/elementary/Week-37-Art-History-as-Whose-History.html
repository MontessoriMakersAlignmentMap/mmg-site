Montessori Makers Residency Week 37 · Elementary · Month 10 · Art and Language Integration
Why this matters right now

To expand what they understand as art. To expand whose traditions they learn from.

The world is full of artistic traditions. Some have been centred and some have been erased.

This is not about making everyone feel good. It is about telling the truth.

02
The honest reckoning

Western European art is extraordinary. But it is a story. A perspective. Not the whole truth.

What the standard story privileges

Paint on canvas. Marble. Perspective. Individual genius. Things you put in a gallery.

Religious figures from Christianity, wealthy patrons, classical themes. Art as the expression of individual vision.

What that centring has done

Sent the message that this is the story that matters.

Erased the traditions of Africa, Asia, the Americas, the Middle East, the Pacific. Erased what many of your students’ ancestors created.

03
The centring is not accidental

It is the result of colonialism. Of who had access to publishing, to museums, to institutional validation.

The art of colonised peoples was often destroyed, looted, taken to European museums, labelled as primitive, treated as artefact rather than art.

This is not neutral. This is history. This is an ongoing reality.

04
What an equity-centred art curriculum looks like

Each tradition studied on its own terms.

01 · Techniques

The geometric abstraction in Islamic pattern-making. Chinese landscape perspective. Japanese printmaking symbolism.

West African textile colour theory. Brazilian street art composition. Each sophisticated. Each different.

02 · Materials

Textiles. Ceramics. Metalwork. Woodcarving. Basketweaving. Beadwork.

Often dismissed as craft — not neutral. A way of valuing some traditions and devaluing others.

03 · Living artists

Alongside history · bring in artists from your students’ own communities.

These traditions are not dead. They are alive. They are evolving. Children can be part of them.

05
A child in your classroom is Navajo. Her parents have noticed she is not confident at school. Does not think of herself as smart. Not engaged. One day — you are teaching Indigenous American art. Navajo weaving. The complexity of the patterns. The mathematical thinking involved. How the designs carry meaning. The child is watching. You see something shift in her face. At break she comes to you: “My grandmother does that.” You say: “I know. She is an artist. She is creating work that connects to a tradition that goes back hundreds of years. That is significant.” Something has changed. She sees her family as connected to something important. That changes how she sees herself.

06
Presenting history to children whose ancestors are not in the standard curriculum

You are correcting something. Restoring something that was erased.

African American quilting patterns. The aesthetics of the Great Migration. Radical Black art movements of the 1970s. This is her inheritance.

Indigenous traditions that span millennia — not as history that ended when colonisers arrived, but as art being created by Indigenous artists today.

“Your people made beautiful things. Your ancestors were artists. Your tradition is sophisticated and valuable.” That is not flattery. That is truth.

07
Cultural pride · foundational, not separate

Not thinking your culture is better than everyone else’s. Understanding that your culture has value.

What has been eroded

Her language is not correct. Her food is weird. Her family structures are not normal. Her art is craft, not art.

Messages received everywhere else.

What art history rebuilds

“Look what your people made. Look at the sophistication. Look at the vision.”

Stronger cultural identity — better mental health, better academic outcomes, better belief that she has value.

08
The equity provocation · said directly

Many of the art treasures in European and American museums are stolen.

Taken from colonised peoples. Displayed as if the collectors were neutral observers. Presented as history — as if the people who made them are no longer making art. Studied as if their current communities do not exist or do not have authority over their own art.

An equity-centred approach does not accept the idea that stolen art displayed in a museum is now human heritage. It is still stolen. It belongs to the people it was stolen from.

09
Neurodivergence note · many entrances to the same ideas

Some children will understand through images — through making art themselves in the style of different cultures — through smaller chunks, not all at once.

Many entrances. Same ideas. Same seriousness.

10
Slow looking · ten minutes on one image

Most adults spend seventeen seconds on a museum painting.

Seat the children in front of one image. Ten minutes. Silent first. Then — what do you see. What do you see that you didn’t see before. What does it make you wonder.

Visual Thinking Strategies — a protocol that treats a nine-year-old as a serious looker.

The skill that lets a child see an image fully — is the skill that lets a citizen see through a manipulated one.

11
Who is in the room · the painting asks

Who is shown? Who paid for it? Who is not in the picture?

A grand portrait. Ask — who served the tea in this room? Who cleaned the floor? Who sewed the dress? Where are they?

The painting does not lie. It simply does not include. Teach the child to see the frame — and the edge beyond it.

At nine, she begins to read absence. The same skill she will use reading news.

12
The canon was curated · not handed down

Great artists you were never shown.

Artemisia Gentileschi

Baroque, seventeenth century. Her paintings hang in the Uffizi — and were attributed to her father for three hundred years.

Hilma af Klint

Painted the first abstract canvases — years before Kandinsky. Hidden by her request for forty years. The story of modernism would not be the story you learned.

Augusta Savage

Sculptor of the Harlem Renaissance. Much of her work destroyed. She taught Jacob Lawrence. She should be a household name.

The Bengal school

Abanindranath Tagore. A modernism built from Indian materials, not imported. Most Western curricula never reach it.

13
Local makers · the artists within walking distance

There is a potter in your town. Invite her.

A quilter. A sign painter. A muralist. A mosaicist. A blacksmith. A screen printer. They are within a few miles of every school.

Art history does not only live in Paris and Florence. It lives where the children live. Insist on this. Plan for it.

A child who meets a living artist understands that art is a practice — not a museum.

14
Curation is a choice · the child should know this

A museum is someone’s argument.

Who chose these objects? Who labelled them? What rooms are larger? What cultures are “primitive” and what cultures are “classical” — and who decided?

These are not complaints. They are the most serious questions in art history.

The child who asks “why is this big and that small?” — is already a curator.

15
The child as curator · the year’s exhibit

Give her a wall. Let her argue.

Twelve images. She chooses. She writes the labels. She decides the order. She stands and walks visitors through. She answers — why this, and not that.

She has just done, at ten, what a curator does at thirty — made meaning by arrangement.

The wall is small. The act is large.
