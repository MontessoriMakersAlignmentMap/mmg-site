Montessori Makers Residency Week 29 · Elementary · Month 8
Why this matters right now

You can now see beyond whether a child follows steps — into the actual mathematical thinking happening underneath.

This is when assessment becomes precise. This is also when you have enough data to say something meaningful to families.

Which means you need to know exactly what to look for — and exactly how to record it.

02
Assessment is not measurement

Not a number or a letter or a percentage. A deep and continuous looking.

What it is not

A test. A score. A day.

Judgement delivered from outside the child’s work.

What it is

A practice of looking — at the same child — week after week.

Watching understanding develop through hands and mind simultaneously.

03
Seven things you are watching at once

All of these are mathematical assessment.

01 · Concept

Does she understand the concept embedded in the material itself?

The material holds the idea. Has the idea landed?

02 · Fluency

Is manipulation becoming more efficient and independent?

Not faster. More certain.

03 · Application

Can she apply it to new situations that have not been taught?

Transfer is the test.

04 · Symbolisation

Can she move it onto paper?

From material to mark. Both still alive in her mind.

05 · Affect

Does it feel possible or impossible?

Willing to struggle? Or shutting down when it is hard?

06 · Social

Can she work alone, ask for help, collaborate without losing her own thinking?

Explain her thinking to others.

04
What to actually observe during work period

Watch the movement. The pace. What happens after. What happens when you ask.

Organising beads by hierarchy before counting — she understands place value. Checking her own work, self-correcting — she has internalised the logic.

Ask: if we took away three units, what would we have? If she counts from one again, versus she immediately says ninety-seven — that is the difference between concrete and conceptual understanding.

05
Thinking questions · not test questions

Many children can do the work but have never been asked to explain it. The quality of the explanation tells you more than the accuracy of the answer.

“Tell me how you organised this work.”
“What would happen if we doubled this number?”
“Can you show me the same amount using the unit beads instead?”
“Why did you choose the golden beads for this problem?”
“Can you explain this to someone who has never seen the materials?”
06
Tuesday morning. She is arranging the teen beads. Thirteen in one row. She tries to push them into a smaller space. “Can they all fit in here?” She tries two rows. Counts. “That doesn’t work.” She tries columns. Eight and five. Counts again. Confused. “I thought I had thirteen but I have thirteen and thirteen.” Moves one unit. Nine and four. “That doesn’t work either.” You say: “What are you noticing about the thirteen?” “They don’t want to stay in columns.” You have just watched her discover that thirteen is prime. You did not tell her. You noted it.

07
Recording · you cannot remember everything

Anecdotal notes work beautifully. A small notebook. In the moment — or immediately after.

A weak note

“Emma worked with the multiplication board.”

Context only. No thinking captured.

A strong note

“Emma organised by products before beginning — she was looking for patterns.”

“Self-corrected twice. Said: I keep making six and seven together, but they should be different.”

What the child did. What material they chose. What was remarkable about the approach. What they said. Memory degrades quickly — especially the small details that actually matter.

08
Portfolio entries · claim plus evidence

Classroom notes are for you. Portfolio entries are for the family — and the child later.

Claim without evidence

“Samir demonstrates conceptual understanding of multiplication.”

Means nothing to a parent.

Claim with evidence

“Samir organised products by size before checking them. He said: I wanted to see how they go up.”

He is looking for patterns — not treating the table as a list to memorise.

Write in language a parent will understand. Not “facility with the linear and unit aspects of the binomial” — “she quickly understood how all the pieces fit together to make the same whole cube.”

09
Communicating with families · bring specifics, ask questions

Ask the family what they notice at home.

Organising toys by size. Playing with recipes. Asking how many days until a birthday. A child who does any of these is doing mathematics.

When families see mathematics in everyday life — they support mathematical thinking at home. That matters profoundly.

10
The equity provocation · look everywhere, or miss whole children

If you only assess when children are sitting with materials, you will miss entire groups of children.

Assessment happens in conversation. In the engineering area. While children draw, build, teach each other, read aloud from a geometry book.

Children who rarely sit with manipulative materials often understand mathematics profoundly — they are thinking through pictures, movement, building, talking.

If your observation does not include all the ways children in your particular room think mathematically — your assessment is not equity-centred.

11
Neurodivergence note · different shape, same mathematics

A child with ADHD may not sit with the golden beads for thirty minutes. They may work with the multiplication board for two minutes in absolute focus — then transfer instantly to a new problem.

This is not less mathematical understanding. It is different mathematical understanding.

12
The running record · small notes, long arc

Assessment begins in the pen you carry in your pocket.

A sentence a day about each child — what they chose, what they avoided, what they mastered. Not for filing. For your own eyes.

At the end of a month, you can read the river of a child. What the test cannot show.

The small habit, kept, becomes the assessment that matters.

13
The conference with the child · assessment as mirror

Sit down. Show them what you have seen.

“I have noticed you choose the bead frame every day this week. I have noticed you stopped at the long division. What do you think is happening?”

Children, even at nine, can describe their own learning — if someone asks.

The conference is an assessment. It is also an act of respect. Both matter.

14
The conference with the parent · what to show, what to not

A parent wants to know if their child is all right.

Not the curriculum checklist. Not the percentile. Show them a photograph of the child’s work. Show them a sentence the child wrote about what was hard.

Then — tell them one thing the child does with integrity. One thing the child is growing into.

Specific. Particular. Unmistakably about that child. That is the assessment a parent can receive.

15
Standardised tests · the conversation you cannot refuse

The tests will come. They are not the enemy — and not the measure.

Prepare children honestly. Tell them what the test is. Tell them it measures a narrow slice. Tell them you already know what they can do.

Do not moralise at them about the test. Do not pretend it does not matter.

Let them walk in calm. That is the only test-prep that actually helps.
