Montessori Makers Residency Week 33 · Elementary · Month 9 · Language Deep Dive
Why this matters right now

Grammar is not separate from real language use. It is the map that explains why language does what it does.

Taught as a thinking tool — not a list of rules — children develop metalinguistic awareness. They begin to see language as a system they can play with, bend, understand.

That is the opposite of the fear-based grammar instruction most adults remember from school.

02
Why Montessori grammar looks different

We do not start with definitions. We put symbols in her hands.

Conventional

Hand a child a sentence. Ask her to identify the parts of speech.

Definition first. She learns to label — not to see.

Montessori

Build the sentence. Remove the adjective. Rearrange. What changed?

She is not learning that adjectives describe nouns. She is discovering the principle herself.

03
The symbol is not arbitrary — it teaches

Colour, shape, size — all of it carries meaning.

Noun · red sphere

Large and solid.

The foundation.

Verb · orange triangle

Pointed upward.

The action. The energy.

Adjective · purple small triangle

Smaller. Same shape as the noun’s companion.

Belongs to the noun. Describes it.

The child does not need to be told any of this. The symbol teaches.

04
Mid-afternoon. A work rug. Grammar symbols spread out. Sentence card: “The fast red fox jumped over the fence.” Red sphere for fox. Purple triangles for fast and red. Orange triangle for jumped. She adds an adverb symbol. Over. Around where? The fence. She studies what she has built. Removes the adjectives. Reads aloud — “The fox jumped over the fence.” Notes the difference. Replaces them. Removes the adverb. Studies again. She is not following instructions. She is exploring.

05
What you will recognise when she understands

She steps out of writing and into analysing her own writing.

At her own desk

“Wait — I need more describing words here.”

“This is confusing because I don’t have an action.” She has internalised the structure.

In peer discussion

“I loved how you used three adjectives in a row — it made me see exactly what you meant.”

Not reciting rules. Discussing the effect of grammatical choices on meaning.

06
The sharp part · grammar as culturally responsive instruction

Much of traditional grammar instruction is really instruction in Standard American English.

Children from other linguistic communities have been shamed for their dialects — labelled as not knowing grammar. When what they actually know is a different, fully valid grammar system.

All children arrive at school speaking a language with a complete grammatical system.

07
Code-switching with agency — not shame

We do not shame the home language while we teach the academic one.

What we honour

Her home dialect has its own internally consistent grammar.

Fully valid. Rule-governed.

What we make explicit

Standard American English is a different system that carries power in certain contexts.

Two systems. Both real. She can move between them because she understands both.

That child has agency over her own language. Her way of speaking is not wrong. It is one system — and she is capable of learning others.

08
Physical manipulation deepens abstraction

A child cannot understand that a noun is a person, place, or thing as an abstract concept.

But she can hold the red sphere. Arrange it. See it always represent the foundation. Discover the principle herself.

Months later — when you ask her to define a noun — she has felt it, moved it, lived it. The definition is the last step, not the first.

The concrete symbol is a scaffold. It is not forever. But it is necessary.

09
The equity provocation · grammar has been a tool of oppression

Grammar pedagogy participates in harm when it is used to correct rather than teach — to establish dominance rather than build understanding.

An equity-centred approach does not ignore Standard American English. It teaches the grammar of academic and professional contexts — in a way that honours what children bring.

Make the relationship between language and power explicit. Give children agency and choice — not shame.

10
Neurodivergence note · concrete, longer, bigger

Some children will need more time with the concrete symbols. Some will need to move their own bodies — act out verbs, statue nouns.

The principle remains: concrete before abstract, exploration before definition, discovery before drill.

11
The grammar farm · a living metaphor, not a decoration

The farm is not the point. The sentences are.

The little farm, the symbols, the games — are a doorway. Used well, a child builds two dozen sentences in an hour and starts noticing how they work.

Used poorly, the child arranges plastic animals and learns nothing about language.

Watch for drift. The sentence is always the work. The farm is just the invitation.

12
Function over form · the harder teaching

A word is not a noun. It plays a noun.

“Running is hard.” Running is a noun. “She was running.” Running is a verb. Same word. Different job.

Teach children to ask — “what is this word doing in this sentence?” Not “what kind of word is it?”

Form is a label. Function is thinking. Hold the higher bar.

13
The verb is action · and the sentence does what it says

“Bring me the red ball.” Now.

The child runs. They bring the red ball. You change the sentence. “Bring me the blue ball.” The adjective matters suddenly. They run again.

They have just experienced, in their legs, that the adjective is not optional.

Grammar, taught at the body. Nothing on paper lands like this.

14
Sentence analysis · in the wild

Bring them sentences from real life.

A line from a novel. A newspaper headline. A sentence from their own writing. An advertisement. A song lyric.

Analyse together. Watch the child discover that the sentence on the back of the cereal box is engineered to do something to them.

Grammar becomes a form of self-defence. As it should be.

15
Across languages · the gift of multilingual children

A child who speaks two languages is your best grammar teacher.

“In Spanish the adjective comes after the noun.” “In Mandarin there is no plural.” “In Arabic the verb often starts the sentence.”

Suddenly English is not the rule of the universe. It is one way, among many, to carry a meaning.

Invite those sentences into the room. Compare openly. The bilingual child is no longer tolerated — they are needed.
