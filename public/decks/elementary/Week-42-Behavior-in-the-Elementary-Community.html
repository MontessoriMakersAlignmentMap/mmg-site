Montessori Makers Residency Week 42 · Elementary · Month 11 · Behaviour, Community, Capstone
Why this matters right now

The second plane is social. Everything that happens in an elementary classroom is filtered through the social lens.

A child’s behaviour is always, always about something happening in the social world. Exclusion. Status. Friendship fractures. Place in the group. Identity. Belonging.

Treat elementary behaviour as an individual problem and you will fail — in the exact moment when her need for belonging is most intense.

02
What shifts at six

A Primary child hits because she does not have language. An Elementary child hits because she has just been told she does not belong.

Primary

Ego-centric — not as a flaw, as development. Kindness is possible; understanding is centred on her own experience.

The other child’s perspective is not fully there yet.

Elementary

She understands herself as a member of a group. Status. Fairness. What others think of her.

Frontal lobe developing. Mirror neurons at full capacity. Comparing, fitting, caring.

03
What adults underestimate

When you are seven and your best friend says you cannot play at recess — that is not a small thing. That is a rupture in your sense of belonging.

If her response is to hit, to exclude back, to hurt — that response is disproportionate, and it makes sense.

She has just been told she does not belong. She is responding to that.

04
Friendship · exclusion · status

Not simple. Not “just kids being kids.” A social world with its own power, currency, and enforcement.

Friendship

About status. About power. About in-groups and out-groups — not just liking the same things.

Popularity is a decision the group has made — and everything she does is read through it.

Exclusion

Not just “not invited.” Being marked as outside. Coordinated. Sustained. Genuine distress.

The brain is focused on survival — trying to regain status, or simply endure rejection.

Status

Who gets to decide in group play. Who gets listened to. Who gets included. Forms fast. Sticks.

Once marked low-status, the same behaviour is read through the mark.

05
The sentence that will change how you see the classroom

The same behaviour that is funny when the popular child does it is mean when the excluded child does it.

The group is reading behaviour through the mark. She cannot behave her way out of the mark. Only the group can lift it.

Which means the work is never only with the child. It is with the community.

06
Dysregulation, second-plane style

A Primary child who is dysregulated might hit or yell. An Elementary child who is dysregulated might do something much harder to see.

Withdrawal

Complete shutdown. Silence. Physical complaints — a stomachache tied to the emotional state.

Dysregulation is quiet.

Relational aggression

Passive aggression. Spreading rumours. Organising other children against someone.

Weapons-grade social intelligence, turned outward.

Rigidity

Extreme control-seeking. Rule enforcement. Panic at deviation from the plan.

A child trying to build safety with her own hands.

07
Trauma does not happen in a vacuum — it happens now, through the group

Fine one-on-one with a trusted adult. Dysregulated the moment she joins the group.

Okay in predictable structures. Destabilised by unexpected social changes. Able to manage during work time. Dysregulated during free play.

The trauma is not the past. It is happening to her now — through the lens of her current relationships and her current safety.

08
Before you intervene — identify the level

Wrong-level responses make things worse. A community problem cannot be solved by disciplining one child.

Individual

Impulse control. Emotion regulation. Needs support with the child, privately.

Work on skill and regulation.

Dyadic

Two children in a dynamic that is not working. A conflict between them — not the group.

Work between the two — with support.

Community-level

Group dynamics. Coordinated exclusion. Normalised unkindness. A culture.

Only the whole community can change a community culture.

10
Why the mark outlived the moment

Her behaviour at the start of the year was normal. She was included. Something shifted six weeks ago.

There was an incident. She said something unkind. It was addressed. But the group never moved past it.

The group decided she was unkind. And weeks later, she is still marked by that decision.

The popular children have coordinated — intentionally or not — to exclude certain others. Some voices matter. Others are dismissed. The hierarchy is hardened. The cruelty has become ambient. No single child’s discipline will reach this. Only the whole community can change it.
12
When the problem is the community, the work is with the community

Bring the whole group into the conversation. Examine what is happening at the group level. Name what children have been enacting.

The community meeting. Restorative practice. Helping children understand they have agency and responsibility in creating the culture they live in.

Much harder than discipline. Much more important.

13
The equity provocation · behaviour is never neutral

The same behaviour in different children is read differently. Because we bring our biases to how we read it.

Assertive

A Black child is called aggressive. A white child is called confident.

Same behaviour. Different verdict.

Emotional

A child from a poor family is dysregulated. A child from a wealthy family is having a feeling.

Same behaviour. Different story.

Physical

A girl who hits is violent. A boy who hits is rough play.

Same behaviour. Different category.

14
What every behaviour response does — whether you mean it or not

When you respond to behaviour, you are always enforcing something about power and belonging.

Either reinforcing who belongs and who does not — or working to change it.

There is no neutral response. There is only an honest one.

15
Neurodivergence note · genuine difficulty, not character

Neurodivergent children often struggle with elementary social complexity. These are not character flaws. They are genuine difficulties that need genuine support.

A child with autism may understand the rules of social interaction intellectually — and struggle to perform them in real time.

A child with ADHD may struggle with the attention demands of maintaining friendships.
