Montessori Makers Residency Week 44 · Elementary · Month 11 · Behaviour, Community, Capstone
Why this matters right now

Not quick fixes. Not adult-brokered compromise. Restorative practice — a real conversation about what happened, what the impact was, and what comes next.

It takes time. It takes skill. And it is absolutely necessary if you want children to develop the capacity for healthy relationships.

02
How conflict changes at the second plane

The conflict has moved from want to harm.

Primary

A want. This toy. This space. This present moment.

Resolve the present. Move on.

Elementary

A harm. Something said. Someone excluded. A betrayal of friendship.

The conflict is about what the relationship means.

03
What Elementary children can now hold

They can distinguish intention from impact. They can hold that a good person caused real pain. They can feel genuine remorse.

They can understand that an apology is not just words. It is the beginning of a process of rebuilding trust.

Which means the resolution has to address the relationship, not just the incident.

04
What restorative practice actually looks like
01
Gather

The people involved. The witnesses. Anyone affected.

Everyone who needs to be there to understand the whole picture.

02
Understand

Not blame. Perspectives.

Person who caused harm speaks. Person harmed speaks. Witnesses. Goal is understanding, not agreement.

03
Account

Real remorse. Real ownership.

The person who caused harm understands the impact. Feels it.

04
Repair

What would actually help?

The harmed person names what they need. The people involved decide what comes next — not the adult.

05
Scene · she was trying to make other children laugh
Guide
What happened from your perspective?
Child who spoke
I was trying to make the others laugh. I didn’t think it was that bad.
Child harmed
I thought we were best friends. I feel like I can’t trust her anymore.
Guide
Imagine if a close friend said something unkind about you to other people. How would that feel?
06
Scene · what would help rebuild trust
Child who spoke
I’m sorry. I didn’t understand until now.
Child harmed
I’m not ready to forgive yet.
Guide
What would help you rebuild trust?
Child harmed
Promise never to say unkind things about me again. Talk to me about things that hurt your feelings — before you tell other people.

They do not move right back into friendship. There is a process of rebuilding that will take time. But they have had a real conversation. They have understood each other. They have decided how to move forward.

07
When the conflict is rooted in identity

You cannot pretend it is just a conflict between two children. You have to name the racism. You have to address it directly.

Bring in more people than the two involved. Parents. Staff. Community members from the same community as the child who was harmed.

The harm is not only to one child. It is harm rooted in systems of racism. The conversation has to account for that.

08
A warning — who does the educating

Do not make the child who was harmed do the work of educating the child who caused harm. That is a burden she did not ask for.

But the child who caused harm needs to be educated. Not by the harmed child. By the adults. By the community.

She needs to understand what racism is, the history behind the stereotype, and the impact on the child who was harmed.

09
Scene · the conversation the community did not want to have
White parent
She didn’t mean it badly.
Guide
The intent does not matter. The impact is that a child was harmed. The conversation is about understanding that harm and deciding what comes next.
Guide
This is not the first time something like this has happened. There is something in the culture of the classroom that is allowing it.
10
Holding a whole community through rupture

You cannot pretend it did not happen. You cannot skip over it. You have to bring the community through it.

Community meetings. Small group conversations. Individual conversations. Whatever the community needs to acknowledge what happened, understand the impact, and decide how to move forward together.

The child who caused harm is also a good person who made a terrible choice. The child who was harmed deserves justice and healing. All of this is true at once.

11
What this work requires of the guide

Regulated in your own nervous system. Able to sit with discomfort. Able to hold multiple truths at once.

You do not get to shortcut your own work. The capacity you cannot hold in yourself, you cannot hold for the community.

12
Equity provocation · not all conflicts are the same

A dispute about who plays the game is not the same as racial harm. If you resolve them with the same process, you have decided they are the same.

You have decided the child who experienced racism just needs to learn to get along.

That is not justice. That is erasure.

13
Neurodivergence note · adapt the process, keep the principles

A child with autism may need the process explained and written down. A child with ADHD may struggle with the length. A child with trauma may be dysregulated by the intensity.

Adapt the process. Keep the core principles intact.

14
The pause · before the circle opens

Not immediately. Not in the heat.

The instinct is to solve the conflict the moment it happens. Resist that instinct. The nervous systems of both children need time to settle before honest speech is possible.

Separate. Water. A quiet room. A book. Ten minutes. An hour. Sometimes a day. Only then — the circle.

A child pushed into repair before she is ready will learn to perform repair — not practise it.

15
When repair cannot happen · and that is the truth

Not every harm can be mended.

Sometimes a child is not safe with another child. Sometimes an adult failed to protect. Sometimes the harmed child needs distance — not dialogue. Restorative practice honours that.

The lesson then is not reconciliation. It is how a community holds someone while they are separate. And how safety is rebuilt, slowly, around them.

A forced repair is a second harm. Do not trade the child’s safety for the adult’s resolution.
