Montessori Makers Residency Week 34 · Elementary · Month 9 · Language Deep Dive
Why this matters right now

By month nine they are writing with increasing honesty. Some stories will carry weight.

Some will reveal things the child might not have otherwise told you. That is the power of creative writing — it gives children a way to process, to explore, to be heard.

Your role is to create the environment where this is possible. Where writing is not performance, not correctness — but expression.

02
Before she finds her voice · she needs to hear many voices

Not just the hero’s journey. Not just the three-act structure.

Your library · a deliberate curation

Folktales and oral traditions from her communities. Contemporary authors from marginalised communities.

Stories without traditional plot. Stories that end ambiguously. Stories where resolution is internal.

The feedback that follows

You do not privilege one narrative structure over others.

You do not tell her the story needs more conflict to be interesting. You ask what she was trying to do.

03
Voice is not a technical skill · it is safety

Voice emerges when a child feels safe to write what is true for her.

Many arrive already code-switching — speaking one way at home, writing what they think is the school voice. When you ask them to write, they write what they think you want to hear.

Your work is to undo this. “I can hear you in this sentence. The way you describe that is so particular to how you see things.”

Over time she settles into her own voice. She stops trying to sound like what she thinks a writer should sound like.

04
Feedback opens the door or closes it

Feedback is a conversation — responsive to what she was trying to do.

What you do first

Listen. Read the piece. Ask her to tell you about it.

What was she trying to say? What did she discover as she wrote? What is she proud of?

What you do next

Offer what you noticed. Tell her what stayed with you.

Ask specific questions. “That moment seemed important. Can you tell me more about what you saw?”

Not every piece needs revision. Not every piece needs a reader other than the writer.

05
The hardest part · four movements

Your instinct might be to comfort, to fix, to report. Your actual work is more subtle.

01 · Honour

“Thank you for trusting me with this story.”

Not performative. She gave you something true.

02 · Listen

Do not interrogate. Do not ask leading questions.

Listen. Be present. Let her know without words that you have heard her.

03 · Assess safety · separately

Address safety directly — with appropriate adults.

Not within the writing conversation.

04 · Continue the space

Do not suggest happier topics. Do not shame her into silence.

Writing might be how she processes.

06
A child is writing a story. Magical forest. Halfway through — the forest catches fire. The animals escape. The trees burn. You read it. Ask her to tell you about it. “I felt the danger in that part. The way the smoke blocked everything out. What made you want to write about that?” She says her grandmother’s house caught fire when she was a baby. She does not remember — but she has heard the story. She wanted to know what it might have felt like. You say: “Your writing helps me understand. Thank you for sharing it.” That is all. You do not psychoanalyse. You do not treat her like she is damaged. You witness. You honour. You let her know her words matter.
Scene · the response that matters

You witness.
You honour.
That is all —
and it is
everything.

07
The lighter side · voice emerging in play

Another child writes something silly, surprising. He is laughing as he tells you about it.

“I wanted to make you laugh.” You say: “You did. This part surprised me. I was not expecting that.”

He wants to read it aloud to the class. You support that. His humour, his way of seeing the world — that is what he is learning about himself as a writer.

That he is a writer.

08
The equity provocation · whose stories have been valued

Teachers have steered children toward safer topics — toward stories that do not make anyone uncomfortable.

Stories about white middle-class nuclear families have been the default. Stories with happy endings. Stories that reinforce the status quo. Other stories have been marked as too dark, too angry, not appropriate.

This is a form of silencing. It teaches children that their stories do not matter. That their experience is not worth writing about.

An equity-centred approach makes space for all stories. Without fear that they will be marked wrong, inappropriate, or deficient.

09
Neurodivergence note · stories in different formats

Some children need stories in other forms — graphic novels, picture books, oral stories, multimodal narratives.

Some will dictate rather than write. Some will have ideas that do not fit a linear narrative structure — that is not a problem. It is their way of thinking.

Honour it.

10
Protect the voice · before you teach the craft

Spelling can wait. The voice cannot.

If you correct every misspelling in the first draft, you will teach the child that writing is an exam. Their voice will quietly leave the room and not return.

Correctness comes on the second pass. The third. The fifth. Never the first.

Tell them so, openly. Say — “this draft is for what you want to say. We will fix the spelling later, together.”

11
Prompts · the ones that unlock writing

Not “what I did at the weekend.” Never that.

Concrete objects

“Describe the inside of your grandmother’s kitchen drawer.” Specific. Unexpected. Senses engaged.

A small stakes

“Write the conversation you wish you’d had with your brother.” The child has something to sort out.

Constraints

“Nine lines. No word longer than six letters.” Constraint is generative, not restrictive.

Point of view

“Tell the story of the day — as the door.” The door saw everything. The child’s imagination wakes.

12
The writer’s notebook · not for teachers to read

A notebook that is hers.

Every child keeps one. You never open it without invitation. Ever. It is the one piece of paper in the classroom that belongs entirely to her.

From it, she chooses what to bring forward — to revise, to share, to make public. Most pages stay private.

This single rule, held for a year, produces more real writing than any curriculum.

13
Reading like a writer · the hidden lesson

Show them the craft in the book.

Read a sentence from a novel. Ask — “why did she put the dash there?” “Why start with ‘and’?” “Why is this paragraph so short?”

The book stops being sacred. It becomes a workshop. The author made choices. The child can make choices too.

Borrow. Steal. Imitate openly. This is how every writer learns.

14
The writing conference · five minutes, once a week

Sit beside her. Not across. Ask one real question.

“Which sentence do you like best?” “Where did the story go cold?” “What are you trying to say here, in one line?”

Listen. Do not correct. Do not rewrite. Hand her the pen.

The conference is a practice — not a marking session. She leaves with one thing to try. Only one.

15
The public reading · writing, honoured aloud

Every few weeks, the room listens.

A child chooses a piece. Stands. Reads it aloud. The room does not comment. The room thanks her.

Ritual matters here. The silence after. The nod. The next child, quietly preparing.

To be read — carefully, without being judged — is the oldest reward a writer knows.
