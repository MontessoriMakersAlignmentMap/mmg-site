Residency · Week Twelve · Elementary

The child who thrived
in Primary has become
scattered, overwhelming,
impossible to focus.

It is not that the environment is wrong. It is that the plane has shifted — and neurodivergence that went unnoticed at age six is now visible because the demands have changed.

Montessori Makers Residency Week 12 · Elementary
Why this matters right now

In Primary the environment contained the regulation.

Spacing always the same. Sensory input regulated. The day’s rhythm predictable. The environment itself held the child.

Elementary is more complex. More freedom means more decision points. More social interaction means more unpredictability. The same child who looked like a perfect match may now be struggling.

Your job: understand why — and build an environment that does not read that struggle as failure.

02
Let us be clear about what we are talking about

Not disorders that need fixing.
Differences in how a brain processes the world.

ADHD · Autism

Attention. Executive function. Sensory integration. Social processing.

Dyslexia · Dyscalculia

Language processing. Numerical reasoning. Often with extraordinary compensating strengths.

Sensory · Anxiety

Nervous systems that are more reactive, more attuned, easier to overwhelm.

Some differences create challenges in environments built for a narrow range of neurotypes. Some bring gifts that are absolutely valuable. Your job is to shape the environment so both can be true at once.

03
The ADHD child · what the transition exposes

In Primary, movement was built in. In Elementary, it has to be engineered.

Primary

Work periods 30–60 min. Highly structured. The child arrives and the activity tells them what to do.

The ADHD brain has rails. Movement is built into practical life and sensorial. No shame for needing to move.

→
Elementary

Two-hour work period. Choose, initiate, carry through, decide when finished.

If the brain struggles with initiation, planning, or task persistence — this freedom becomes paralyzing rather than empowering.

04
Structures that look like freedom

The environment must provide rails.
The rails and the freedom are not contradictory.

i.

A launch list — visual menu of options the child can reference without asking.

ii.

A timer — so the child can see how long they intend to work, and when to re-choose.

iii.

Scheduled movement breaks built into the day — not requested, not negotiated.

iv.

A body-doubling space — work alongside another child or the guide. Another regulated nervous system in the room.

05
The autistic child · when the ground gives way

In Primary, she memorized the day. Learned the safe spaces. Figured out the unspoken rules.

In Elementary, every coping strategy becomes less reliable. Schedule shifts with others’ plans. Work groups form in new places. Social dynamics thicken.

She may experience this as a sudden loss of the ground she thought she had found. This does not mean elementary Montessori is wrong for her. It means the adult needs to build specific scaffolds.

06
Scaffolds the autistic child may need

Need for lower social demand is not a deficit to be overcome by forced exposure.

Space

A quiet workspace that is reliably quiet — not just quieter.

The autistic nervous system registers variance. “Quieter than usual” is not quiet enough.

Social rules

Explicit teaching of group-work expectations.

Not assumed. Not absorbed through modeling. Named, written, referenced.

Debriefing

Time with the guide to unpack social interactions that confused her.

Not therapy. Not correction. Translation.

Solo research

Permission to do a research project alone.

A legitimate choice, not a failure to collaborate.

07
Sensory · seeking and avoiding

These are not exceptions. This is how you prepare an environment for different bodies.

Sensory seeking

Primary’s tactile world was enough. Elementary can feel dangerously under-stimulating.

Heavy-material setup jobs. Outdoor exploration. Tactile notetaking. A legitimate sensory menu.

·
Sensory avoiding

Open space and social proximity in group work become intolerable.

A smaller workspace. Noise-canceling headphones during work period. Permission to retreat without justifying it.

08
Anxiety · what it looks like in this plane

An anxious child may become hyperactive or oppositional — and the adult mistakes it for misbehavior.

She may freeze at the start of work — afraid of making a mistake. Avoid challenges because she feels she will fail. Her nervous system is running faster than the lesson.

A pilot presentation to the guide alone before presenting to the class. A safe word. Large projects broken into small steps. Explicit reassurance that mistakes are data, not failures.

09
The question guides ask

Is elementary Montessori appropriate for this child?

The answer depends on one question: Is the child adjusting to the environment, or is the environment adjusting to the child?

Montessori is supposed to be the latter. That is the entire philosophy. But in practice, many guides implement it rigidly — one vision of what the work period should look like, one way children function.

An equity-centered guide knows the prepared environment is not a fixed thing. It is a living system that transforms based on who is in the room.

10
A nine-year-old appears to have regressed. Engaged in Primary, now wandering from shelf to shelf, starting and abandoning. The guide is about to suggest the family consider other options. Instead, one afternoon she sits with the child at the start of work period. Together they make a list. Little pictures next to each option. “What is one thing you want to start with?” She sets a timer for thirty minutes. That day the child works for an hour.
Scene A · the child was never not ready

The movement
between activities
is now purposeful
rather than chaotic.

Over weeks he starts making his own lists. Asking for timers. Navigating the two-hour period alone.

He needed elementary to be structured differently.

11
During a big lesson on the water cycle, an autistic child stands up and leaves. The guide feels rejected. A few days later the child returns with a drawing and questions about dew point. He had gone home and researched what confused him. He did not leave from disinterest — his brain needed to process alone. The guide changes the protocol. Before big lessons, a heads-up. A preview session fifteen minutes before. An explicit permission to step away.
Scene B · the child was not rejecting the lesson

He still doesn’t love
group lessons.
But there is a structure
that honors how
his brain works.

Including without forcing inclusion.

12
The equity provocation · who gets pathologized

A Black boy with ADHD is more likely to be seen as a behavior problem than a white boy with ADHD.

A Latinx girl who does not make eye contact is more likely to be seen as disrespectful. An autistic white girl who does not make eye contact is more likely to be seen as shy.

Your job is not just to support these children in learning.
Your job is to protect them from having their neurodivergence weaponized against them.

13
A distinction you must hold

Challenging is not the same as dangerous.

An ADHD child who cannot sit still is not dangerous. An autistic child who does not understand social convention is not dangerous. These are neurodivergent presentations. They deserve accommodation.

They do not deserve punishment. They do not deserve the narrative that this child is a problem and will cause problems.

That narrative — especially applied to children of color — gets written into the educational record. It shapes a future.

14
Neurodivergence note · your own brain shapes the guidance

If you are neurodivergent —
you have insights a neurotypical adult cannot access.

That is valuable. Use it with humility — your experience is not all neurodivergent experience.

If you are neurotypical, you are guessing at an experience you do not have. Be humble about that. Listen to neurodivergent adults. Read neurodivergent writing. Do not assume you know what will help.

Ask the children.

15
What the prepared environment becomes

The prepared environment is not a fixed thing.

Visual menus, launch lists, timers.

External structure that makes internal structure possible. Not training wheels — scaffolding.

A reliably quiet space.

Not “usually” quiet. Always quiet. The difference matters to the nervous system.

Movement, sensory menu, retreat options.

Not permissions asked. Permissions designed in.

Explicit language for social norms.

Named, written, referenced. What the neurotypical class absorbs, the autistic child deserves spelled out.
