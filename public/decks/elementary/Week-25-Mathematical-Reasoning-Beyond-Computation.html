Montessori Makers Residency Week 25 · Elementary · Month 7
Why this matters right now

Most of you are watching children who can perform every algorithm with precision — but cannot transfer those skills to novel situations.

This gap between computation and reasoning is the most common misunderstanding in contemporary mathematics education.

The Montessori privilege: materials that make mathematical reasoning visible and tangible. The responsibility: knowing the difference between giving a child materials and giving a child mathematical thinking.

02
Procedural fluency is not mathematical reasoning

Both matter. They are not the same thing.

Procedural fluency

Execute a series of steps correctly, in a prescribed sequence.

Perform the algorithm on the checkerboard. Follow the rules. Manipulate the beads. Arrive at the correct answer.

Competence within a defined system.

Mathematical reasoning

Understand why. See the principles. Transfer to new contexts.

Recognise the structure inside a problem. Connect it to something already understood. Decide how to approach it.

Procedural fluency is doing. Mathematical reasoning is understanding.

03
The tragedy of the over-proceduralised Montessori classroom

A child performs the nine-board exercise with stunning speed. Asked why you multiply ten by ten to get the area of a square — they cannot answer.

No one had ever asked them to think about it. The materials had done the explaining. The child had done the executing. Reasoning had never been required.

The beauty of the materials used for speed rather than for depth.

04
The misunderstanding · materials are not scaffolds

They are not obstacles to get through. They are mathematical models.

The checkerboard is not a scaffold to abstract multiplication. It is a concrete embodiment of the multiplication algorithm itself.

When a child multiplies 34 × 27, they are literally breaking thirty-four into thirty and four, twenty-seven into twenty and seven, and building the product from parts. This is not different from what we later call algebra.

It is the concrete reality of which algebra is the abstract representation.

05
The pedagogical shift · ask different questions

Your questions matter more than your explanations.

Instead of

“What is the answer?”

A test of recall.

Ask

“Why does that work?”

A door to thinking.

Ask

“Could you use a different material? Would you get the same answer?”

Now the structure becomes visible.

Instead of moving on

Linger. Let genuine questions emerge.

Child finishes the multiplication board quickly? Ask them to find all the ways to make 24.

Instead of moving on

Predict. Then test.

What happens if you multiply by numbers ending in five?

Instead of moving on

Organise what you have found.

Into a pattern. A table. A picture.

06
Why computation without reasoning fails to transfer

It is not laziness. It is not lack of practice. The knowledge is bound to the context where it was learned.

The child knows how to do the checkerboard. They do not know that the checkerboard has anything to do with cookies left after eating, or money after spending, or wood after cutting.

The knowledge exists in isolation. It has no bridges to other contexts.

07
Where bridges are built · real contexts

A problem-solving environment is not a worksheet with word problems. It is a real context that demands reasoning.

A store

Selling materials they have made.

Calculating money and change. The answer matters — it is their money.

A recipe

Double it. Or cut it in half.

Multiplication and fractions inside something you can eat.

A space

Measure and compare the rooms.

Area and perimeter as lived dimensions. Not diagrams on a page.

08
A third-grader does the subtraction snake beautifully. Freezes when asked to find 52 − 38 without the material. “What would you do if you only had the beads?” She picks up golden beads. Makes 52. Removes to leave 38. Sees 14 remaining. “Why did you get the same answer both ways?” She stops. She had never asked herself. The algorithm and the beads had been separate skills in her mind — with no connection. When she finally connects them — when she sees that the algorithm is a faster way of doing what the beads show directly — something shifts. She is no longer executing a procedure. She is thinking.

09
Late afternoon. A guide sets up a scenario. Eight containers. Each container holds five seeds. “How many seeds altogether?” A child goes to the multiplication board. Builds it. Eight times five is forty. The guide asks — “Are we collecting seeds eight times? Or five times?” The child stops. The words in the problem suddenly matter. She has been told the structure through language — now she must read it in the problem. The guide asks — “Would you get the same answer the other way?” Five containers. Eight seeds in each. Still forty. The properties of multiplication are becoming real to her. Not abstract. Visible. Verifiable.

10
Your role shifts fundamentally

No longer primarily a presenter of materials. A facilitator of thinking. A questioner.

After a child finds an answer: “You found 1,288. Is that reasonable? Approximately how big should the answer be? How do you know?”

Not to test. Not to be difficult. To teach the child to think mathematically — rather than just to do mathematics.

11
The equity provocation · who gets to reason

Children in privileged schools get instruction that emphasises reasoning. Children in resource-scarce schools get instruction that emphasises computation, memorisation, speed.

The implicit message: some children are capable of real thinking — other children are taught to follow procedures. This happens even in schools with Montessori materials.

If you use your beautiful materials primarily to build speed without reasoning — you are participating in this inequity.

The children who benefit most from slowing down, questioning deeply, staying with concrete materials longer — are the children who are already behind.

12
Neurodivergence note · reasoning can outpace fluency

Many neurodivergent children develop strong reasoning even as procedural fluency takes longer.

Children with dyscalculia or attention-related differences often reason beautifully with more time in concrete materials and more opportunities to connect mathematics to real situations.

Problem-based learning often suits these children better than isolated procedure work.

13
Estimation · the cousin of reasoning we underuse

“Is this answer reasonable?” is a mathematical question.

A child who computes 48 × 52 and gets 250 is not only wrong. They are not yet asking whether their answer is in the right neighbourhood.

Estimation is the voice in the head that says — “that cannot be right.”

Teach them to predict before they compute. Teach them to check after. The computation is the sandwich filling. The reasoning is the bread.

14
The silent decision · when to not give a lesson

Sometimes the reasoning happens in the space you did not fill.

A child is stuck. You have three choices — present the next lesson, ask a question, or say nothing and walk away.

The third choice is a teaching choice too. Often the best one.

Not every struggle needs an intervention. Some struggles need to be left to do their work.

15
The long arc · what reasoning now makes possible later

The children who reason at eight do not hit a wall at fourteen.

Age 8 · 9

They use the checkerboard to find a pattern they notice, not just to complete a sum.

Age 10 · 11

They explain — with words — why the pattern must hold. They are proving.

Age 13 · 14

Algebra arrives as a language for what they have been doing. Letters for numbers do not surprise them.

The other path

Children who only computed often meet algebra as a foreign grammar. They stall.

Your work now is the difference.
