Montessori Makers Residency Week 28 · Elementary · Month 7
Why this is equity work

Children from families with privilege learn financial reasoning implicitly. Children without that privilege are often told finances are “not their concern” — when their families are navigating complex financial realities every day.

Rent calculations. Deciding between utilities or food. Understanding predatory lending. Saving for months to afford something their children need.

In elementary, we can begin to change this. Financial literacy as mathematics with real stakes.

02
Start with value and exchange

Not the price tags we assign — the actual value of labour and exchange.

Exchange

“I trade my labour for money.”

The work I do has value. My time is worth something.

Value

“Is this thing I want worth the work I did to earn this?”

Toy costs five dollars. She earns two an hour. Two and a half hours of work.

Opportunity cost

“If I buy this now — I cannot buy that later.”

Sophisticated economics, concrete for an elementary child. Accessible through experience.

03
The classroom economy · simple structure, real consequences

Earn. Spend. Tax. Shared resources funded by what children contribute.

Earn

Tutoring younger children. Caring for materials. Making items to sell.

Academic. Practical. Creative. All of it is work.

Spend

Materials for projects. Special seating. Borrowing books. Access to an activity.

Real choices with real trade-offs.

Tax

Shared snacks. Shared materials. A shared project.

They experience taxation. They see how shared resources are funded.

The guide’s role is facilitation — not control. Children run the economy themselves. They are solving real problems. They are doing mathematics.

04
Percentages, interest, proportional reasoning

Abstract until they are tied to real decisions. Then suddenly — concrete and urgent.

Five dollars a week for twenty weeks equals one hundred dollars. But at one percent monthly interest — slightly more. She can calculate it. It is her money.

Saving is not just delay. Saving can grow her money. Interest compounds.

05
A boy wants a skateboard. Fifty dollars. He earns two dollars a week. Twenty-five weeks. Too long. He asks for a faster way. Together they explore. Tutoring younger children pays three dollars per session. Bookmarks sell for a dollar each — he could make several per day. He combines tutoring with making and selling bookmarks. Reaches his goal in ten weeks instead of twenty-five. He has learned about optimisation and opportunity cost through his own decisions. Effort and strategy matter.

06
A community dinner to raise money for a local food bank. Budget: thirty dollars. The recipe calls for one pound of chicken per three people. Thirty people. Ten pounds. She compares stores — chicken is two dollars a pound cheaper at one. Rice is much cheaper in bulk. She must decide between the cheaper chicken and the organic option. Does quality matter? Does cost matter? The money goes to families without enough to eat. She reasons through choices she had never had to make. Returns with findings and a shopping list. When the soup is made and sold — her mathematical work mattered. It had real consequences.

07
Noticing the system · mathematics as justice work

Elementary children are not expected to solve inequality. They should be invited to notice it.

Two grocery stores in two neighbourhoods. Same product. Different price. A child said: “That is not fair.” The guide did not dismiss it. “You are right. It is not fair. That is one reason families in this neighbourhood have less money left over after buying food — even though they are working.”

A child who understands that her family spends fifty percent of income on rent, while a wealthier family spends twenty, understands something about structure and opportunity. Not laziness. Not bad decisions. Mathematics.

08
Why this supports all mathematics learning

The motivation shifts. Mathematics is no longer a skill to be learned. It is a tool for something that matters.

Division

From worksheet to paying for something real.

The child who struggles becomes deeply motivated to understand when the money is hers.

Percentages

From abstract to “how much of what we raised goes to the cause?”

Engaged. Because the answer actually matters.

09
Children go home and teach what they have learned

A child who understands compound interest can influence her family’s financial future.

A child who understands percentages can help her family make better decisions. A child who sees how taxes fund public goods understands something about citizenship.

That is not a small thing. That is transformative.

10
Neurodivergence note · concrete enough, abstract enough

Financial problem-solving is concrete enough to hold attention — and abstract enough to develop reasoning.

Children who engage powerfully with concrete contexts but struggle with abstract procedures often thrive in this work.

Children with executive function differences need support in planning and tracking goals over time. The classroom economy provides this scaffolding naturally.

11
The vocabulary · words children need by eleven

Not abstractions. Working tools.

Interest

The cost of borrowing money, or the reward for lending it. A fee paid for time.

Compound

Interest paid on interest. The quiet engine that grows savings — and grows debt.

Debt

Money owed. Not moral. Not shameful. A relationship that can serve you, or trap you.

Budget

A plan that decides, in advance, what your money is for. A form of self-respect.

Say the words. Show the arithmetic. Let them practise it on something real.

12
A classroom economy · not a game, a real practice

Let a real budget pass through their hands.

Give the class a small fund. Let them plan a snack, a Going Out, a seed order. Let them track what is left. Let them argue about what to spend it on.

Every argument is a lesson in priorities — which is what financial literacy actually teaches.

The worksheet teaches almost nothing. The twenty real dollars teaches almost everything.

13
Name the systems · that profit from not knowing

Some industries depend on your ignorance.

Payday lenders. Rent-to-own furniture. Overdraft fees. Buy-now-pay-later. Credit cards with introductory rates.

This is not paranoia. It is arithmetic. Children who understand the math are not easily preyed on.

Teaching financial literacy without naming these is teaching them to swim without mentioning the current.

14
A group is planning a Going Out to the botanical garden. They have $60 for four children. Transit is $3 each, each way. Admission is $8 for children. They have to eat. One pulls out paper. Another starts multiplying. A third says, “we will not make it unless we bring lunch.” A fourth says, “is there a group rate?” They call to ask. There is. They save enough for ice cream. You did not teach this lesson. The trip did.

15
Your own money story · is audible

Children hear how you talk about money.

Your anxiety. Your avoidance. Your joke about being bad with numbers. Your silence when a child asks how much something cost.

If money was a taboo in your house, it is hard to teach it as a neutral subject. Notice that.

You do not have to be wealthy or confident. You have to be willing to be matter-of-fact.
