Montessori Makers Residency Week 39 · Elementary · Month 10 · Art and Language Integration
Why this matters right now

At elementary, children are developing sophisticated taste, preference, identity. They are asking who they are in relation to culture.

If every other tradition appears only in units or as “other,” we have told them something profound about whose heritage belongs in the sacred space of serious study.

That message lands differently on children from dominant culture and children from communities whose music we have marked as anything less than central.

02
Said plainly · what “music history” usually means

A very particular narrative from a very particular geography — and we have called it universal.

What gets called history

Baroque and Romantic composers. Symphony. Piano. Western staff notation.

The standard for writing music.

What it does to a child

Her mother’s lullabies are not on a staff. Her family’s music is call-and-response, built for participation.

We have told her grandmother’s tradition is not valuable enough to document the way we document Mozart.

03
The exclusion is not accidental

European colonisers wrote down the rules of Western classical music as if they were the rules of music itself.

Equal temperament as the correct tuning. The major-minor tonal system as the sophisticated system. Reading staff notation as what it means to be musically literate. None of this is natural. It is all choice.

For two hundred years, schools have been enforcing that choice as if it were truth.

04
The quiet sentence that does the work

“Reading staff notation is what it means to be musically literate.” That sentence is not true. It is a choice that has been repeated until it sounds true.

A child who can hold a polyrhythm in her body is musically literate. A child who can hear the microtones in a raga is musically literate. A child who can improvise over a twelve-bar blues is musically literate.

Literacy is the ability to read and make meaning in a tradition. Plural. Always plural.

05
Children are reading the curriculum

They do not need to hear a teacher say classical music is superior. The schedule says it.

We learn staff notation to access one tradition — while music from other traditions is presented informally, by ear. We bring in a professional classical teacher. We learn “world music” from a recording.

A hidden curriculum — through what music, how much time, what framework we use to organise it.

06
A unit on African music is not the answer

Adding a unit on African music to a European curriculum still centres the European tradition as default — and everything else as special content.

“World music week.” International day. “Cultures around the world.” Intentions are good. The structure still teaches the same lesson: one tradition is home — everything else is a field trip.

The work is not supplements. The work is redesign.

07
Built on literacy · not canonicity

Not a list of dead European composers. Concepts and skills.

Ask differently

What is essential for children to understand about how music works?

How do people create meaning through sound? How do humans organise pitch, rhythm, sound?

Answer from many traditions

African polyrhythmic systems alongside European rhythm. Raga structures alongside Western chord progressions.

Different tuning systems. Music as documentation and resistance. All studied with equal rigour.

08
Four recordings. West African drums. Brazilian samba. Indian tabla. Baroque harpsichord. The conversation between the drums. The mathematical precision of the tabla. The tabla was developed in India hundreds of years ago. A child whose grandmother is from Delhi leans forward. It is not presented as special or other. It is serious music — studied with the same attention as anything else.

09
A provocation children can carry

Why do we know the names of all the European composers — but not the names of the people who created the traditions we call “folk” or “world” music?

The griots of West Africa documenting history through music for centuries. The blues created through the resistance and creativity of enslaved African Americans. Flamenco in Spain as Romani expression.

All music is created by real people. All music has history. The reason we do not know these histories — is because we have not centred them.

10
Three traditions · studied with names, dates, people

Not timeless. Not unchanging. Authored — by specific people, in specific places, under specific conditions.

Griots

West Africa. Hereditary historians documenting centuries of events, genealogy, law — through music.

Music as archive.

The Blues

Created by enslaved African Americans and their descendants. A form developed through resistance, grief, and invention.

Music as survival and authorship.

Flamenco

Romani people in Andalusia, Spain. Rhythmic complexity, vocal traditions, guitar innovation — developed over centuries.

Music as a people’s signature.

11
The equity provocation · not subtle, not neutral

If the only music celebrated as serious is from a culture other than hers, she is learning that her culture is less.

This is one of the ways schools enforce assimilation. Most Montessori teachers will do it with good intentions and no awareness that it is happening.

Examine the music we choose to teach. Ask whose heritage we are centring, whose culture we are marking as other — and what we are teaching children about whose humanity is worth documenting and celebrating.

12
What the child learns to do to belong

She learns to value what is valued by people from dominant culture.

She learns to describe her own family’s music as “just what we listen to at home.” Not serious. Not school-appropriate. Not the real thing.

Assimilation is not loud. It is the slow editing of a child’s own estimation of what she comes from.

13
Neurodivergence note · real access through sound

Some children have profound access to music through their neurodivergence.

ADHD: rhythm and beat regulation create the focus she needs. Autism: perfect pitch, or profound response to tonal relationships.

These gifts are real and valuable — and they are worth noticing and celebrating.

14
Scene · children researching music history

The guide asks: Why do we think of European music as having composers — but African music or Indian music as timeless and unchanging?

The children start researching. They find griot lineages by name. They find the musicians who shaped early blues. They find the flamenco cantaores whose recordings survive.

They are learning the real lesson: it was never that these traditions had no history. It was that the history was never written down for them to find.

15
What this asks of you as the guide

Potentially telling someone: we are not going to spend six weeks on Beethoven’s symphonies.

Being willing to defend the choice to spend the same time on gamelan. On flamenco. On the history of the blues. Using your authority as the guide to say: this is what a different music education looks like.

The courage is not loud. It is the sentence you say calmly in a staff meeting — and the curriculum you hand in on Monday.
