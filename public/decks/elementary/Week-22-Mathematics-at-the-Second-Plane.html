Montessori Makers Residency Week 22 · Elementary
Why this matters right now

In Primary, the materials do most of the work. In Elementary, the guide does.

The materials become less literal, more abstract. Your job changes.

You have to understand the mathematics so deeply that you can support children in moving from what they can touch to what they can think.

02
What changes in their thinking

From working with quantity — to working with operation.

Primary · quantity

What ten is. How much. How many.

Counting. Magnitude. Addition and subtraction with concrete objects. The answer emerges from the action.

Elementary · operation

Not just what addition is — how it works.

What happens when you add. How addition connects to other operations. Mathematics as a system with internal logic.

They begin to think mathematically — rather than just numerically.

03
Holding multiple steps in sequence

In Primary, one operation at a time. In Elementary — add, then multiply. Subtract, then divide.

Not just quantitative complexity. A leap in how they hold information and process it.

Procedural fluency — and more importantly, the ability to think through a problem systematically.

04
The progression · each material requires a little less visual evidence

Concrete → semi-concrete → semi-abstract → abstract.

42
01 · Concrete

Golden beads.

Quantity made visible

Count out 23 and 34. Push together. Count the result: 57. The answer emerges from the action.

02 · Semi-concrete

Checkerboard.

Position means something

A bead on the tens square is worth more than a bead on the units square. Place value enters the hand.

03 · Semi-abstract

Racks and tubes.

Trust the structure

The child cannot see the magnitude like they could in the square of 100. They hold it in mind, trusting the structure.

04 · Abstract

Written numerals.

Now ready for the symbol

They have felt place value in their hands. Seen operations with materials. Now they are ready to work with symbols.

05
The bead bars · another step toward abstraction

The child is no longer counting units. They are recognising the bar — by its colour and its length — and understanding its value.

Nine is longer than three. They still see it.

But they are trusting a symbol — the colour — rather than counting every unit. One more step toward the numeral on the page.

06
When a child arrives without Primary’s concrete foundation

Do not panic. Do not skip.

Assess

Where is the child actually?

Not where they are supposed to be. Where they are.

Give the beads

Even at eight. Even if peers are on the checkerboard.

A child without quantity at their bones will struggle with all abstract math. It is not too late.

Expect speed

They are older. They may move through in weeks.

More cognitive capacity. But they still need the concrete experience.

07
When a child is stuck in the concrete phase

Is it readiness — or a mismatch in presentation?

Some children are visual.

They need to see the square hundred. Keep the visual anchor longer.

Some are kinesthetic.

They need to move beads around. Give them more manipulation before paper.

Some need to talk through.

Not just manipulate. Narrate the problem aloud together.

09
Read the movement

If a child is jumping levels — they are probably not ready.

If a child is stuck at one level — check whether it is readiness or presentation.

Sometimes the shift just takes more time. Sometimes it takes a different material.

10
The equity provocation · math is not neutral

Who gets to learn mathematics, how it is presented, what counts as mathematical thinking — these are shaped by culture and power.

In many schools: children of color tracked away from advanced mathematics. Children from low-income families handed worksheets while wealthy peers get materials. Girls steered away by narratives about who is good at math.

In Montessori — if you are paying attention — you disrupt that. All children get the materials. All move at their own pace.

But you have to be intentional. Mathematical equity is not automatic just because you are using materials.

11
Neurodivergence note · dyscalculia and attention

Manipulatives help. They are not a complete solution.

Dyscalculia.

Patience. Alternative representations of number. Not about effort or intelligence.

Attention challenges.

Shorter work periods. Return often, not for long.

The surprising ones.

Neurodivergent learners who bloom when presentation matches their mind. Let them show you.

12
The language you use · carries the mathematics

Children inherit how you speak about number.

Instead of

“Is that right?”

Try — “Show me how you got there.”

Instead of

“Good job.”

Try — “You kept going when it got hard.”

Instead of

“The answer is…”

Try — “What do you notice about the quantity?”

The principle

Language that praises process over product protects the thinker.

Which is what we are forming. Not a calculator.

13
Word problems · the second plane child’s appetite

They want the human behind the number.

Not “4 + 3 = ?” — but “A ship carried four hundred amphorae to Carthage. A storm broke three hundred. What arrived?”

The operation is the same. The appetite is not.

Let the mathematics live inside a story. That is where this age learns.

14
Mistakes · the most honest material you have

A wrong answer is a window into thinking.

If you rush to correct it, you close the window. You also teach the child that mistakes are shameful.

Sit with the mistake. Ask — “Walk me through it.” Find where the logic turned.

That is where the teaching begins. Not before.

15
Your own relationship with mathematics

If you are carrying math anxiety, the children will feel it.

They will feel your small hesitation before you open the checkerboard. They will feel whose question you welcome and whose you deflect.

You do not have to be confident. You do have to be honest.

“I need to think about this. Let’s work it out together” — is a gift to a child. Not a failure.
