Montessori Makers Residency Week 14 · Elementary
Why this matters right now

You cannot teach real biology without teaching evolution.

It is the single most powerful organizing principle in biology. It explains the diversity of life, the relationships between organisms, all the adaptations that allow organisms to thrive.

And you are teaching in a country where roughly forty percent of adults do not accept evolution — where you may be one of the first people to present this to a child not framed as an attack on faith.

Be thoughtful. Be clear. Be respectful of families while honoring the child’s right to scientific understanding.

02
What evolution actually is

Not progress. Not a ladder. Not a goal.
Differential survival and reproduction.

Some organisms leave more offspring than others. If those offspring inherit traits that helped their parents survive, those traits become more common.

This is staggeringly simple. And staggeringly powerful. Across millions of years, this is how life diversified into the millions of species we see today.

03
The evidence · overwhelming, from four directions

A theory in the scientific sense — a framework that explains enormous evidence and makes testable predictions.

i.

The fossil record.

Organisms changing over time, across layers of rock. Predicted sequences, found.

ii.

Comparative anatomy.

A human arm, a bat wing, a whale flipper — the same bones, modified. Bizarre without common ancestry.

iii.

Genetics.

Organisms share DNA. Mutations create variation. Variation is what selection acts on.

iv.

Evolution in real time.

Bacteria developing antibiotic resistance. Moths changing color. Finch beaks in drought. Right now.

04
Science and religion · different questions

Science and religion answer different questions.

Science asks

How do things work?

Mechanism. Evidence. Prediction. What can be measured and tested.

·
Religion asks

Why are things here? What is my purpose? How should I live?

Meaning. Relationship to the divine. A different kind of truth.

A person can believe God created the universe and understand evolution as the mechanism through which that creation happened. Many people do exactly this.

05
Be transparent about the limits of science

Science can tell you how.
Science cannot tell you whether a divine being is involved.

Science cannot tell you why — in some grand philosophical sense. Cannot tell you whether there is meaning to it. Cannot tell you how to live.

Be honest about this. Say it to the child out loud: “Science studies how the physical world works. Science does not tell you about God or spirituality or what things mean. That is a different kind of knowing.”

06
How to say it to a child

The story. Scientific — and humble about what it is and is not.

“For a very long time, scientists noticed that different animals had some of the same bones and some of the same ways of working. They asked — why would that be? Darwin said maybe all these animals came from the same ancestor a very long time ago and changed over time. And the evidence made sense with his idea. Fossils showing animals changing. All animals sharing DNA because we are all related. This is what we know from science about how life changed and diversified.

Some people also believe that God was part of how this happened — and that is not something science can prove or disprove. Different families believe different things about that.”

A lesson the child can live with
07
Create the container · both can be true

Never say: “If your family does not believe in evolution, they are wrong.”

Do say: “In science class, we study how the physical world works, and evolution is how scientists explain the diversity of life. In your family, you might understand things differently — and that is okay. Both can be true.”

You do not require a child to choose between their family and their learning. You create a container where both can exist.

08
When the child objects

“Humans did not come from monkeys.
We are made in God’s image.”

Do not say: “That is not true.”

Do say: “I hear you. That is what your family believes. In science, we have a lot of evidence that humans and other animals are related because we all share similar bones and DNA and ancestors from a long time ago. But the question of whether God made us is not something science answers. That is something your family talks about.”

You are honoring the child’s family and teaching science. You are modeling that different ways of knowing can coexist.

09
A parent has written: “Do not teach my child about evolution. We believe God created humans.” The guide sets up time to talk. She explains what she will teach — and what she will not. “I am not trying to replace one way of knowing with the other. I am giving your child information about how the scientific world works.” The parent is not happy. But agrees. The child goes home confused — and not traumatized. The parent and child have conversations. The child does not have to choose.

10
A different child asks: “If we evolved from other animals, does that mean we are animals?” The guide is delighted by this question. She says — “Yes, humans are animals. We are mammals. Backbones, hair, milk for our babies. We are also different — we think in complex ways, we ask questions like you just did, we create art and meaning. We are animals, and we are also uniquely human. Both things are true.” The child nods and goes back to work.

Not as separate from nature.
As part of it.

Both things are true.

11
The equity provocation · evolution weaponized

Evolution has been weaponized to justify racism, eugenics, colonialism.

Distorted ideas that some human populations are “more evolved,” closer to some imagined peak. Bad science. Evil use.

Many children in your classroom have heard — directly or subtly — that their own community is less evolved, less civilized, less advanced. The history of science in this country includes a long tradition of using scientific language to justify the domination of people of color.

Refuse to participate. Say it. Out loud. Every time.

12
What you make explicit every time

All humans alive today have been evolving for the same amount of time.

We have all adapted to different environments. We have all developed the traits that helped us survive. No human group is more or less evolved. No human group is genetically superior.

Not something you mention once and move on. Something you make explicit whenever you teach evolution.

Evolution celebrates human diversity as the product of different adaptations to different environments — not as a hierarchy.

13
Neurodivergence note · concrete change, bounded fears

A literal-thinking child may struggle with the abstract timescales of evolution.

Give them concrete examples of change happening now — bread mold. Bacteria. Fast-generation organisms whose evolution is visible in weeks.

A child with anxiety may worry about extinction. Reassure them about what is and is not under threat right now. Precisely. Not vaguely.

14
The guide’s preparation

Before the lesson. Before the objection.

Know the families.

Which children come from faith traditions that frame evolution as opposition? Contact families before the lesson.

Rehearse the limits-of-science speech.

Until it is easy to say in the moment — not improvised under pressure.

Have the no-hierarchy language ready.

For the moment when a child asks if some humans are more evolved. Answer it before it is asked.

Real-time examples on hand.

Moth colour. Antibiotic resistance. Finch beaks. Evolution that can be seen.

15
A caution · when the school forbids it

You cannot teach real biology without evolution. If you are prohibited from teaching it, you are being asked to teach false science.

That is an integrity issue that goes beyond this lesson.

In most cases, you can teach evolution in a way that respects families while being truthful about what science shows. That is the work.
