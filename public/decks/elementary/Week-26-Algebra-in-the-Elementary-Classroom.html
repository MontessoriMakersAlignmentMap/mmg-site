Montessori Makers Residency Week 26 · Elementary · Month 7
Why this matters right now

The conventional answer: algebra belongs in middle school. This answer is mathematically wrong and pedagogically damaging.

Algebra is not a formal system of notation. Algebra is a way of thinking about relationships and structures.

In a Montessori classroom, this thinking develops naturally through the materials — if we know what to look for.

02
Three forms of algebraic thinking in elementary

Seeing structure. Relationships that hold regardless of the specific numbers.

01 · Pattern → generalisation

“Whenever you multiply by ten, it works this way.”

A claim that goes beyond the specific cases tested. True for all cases, not just seen ones.

02 · Working with unknowns

“I know the total. I know one part. I can find the other.”

Logical deduction based on relationships. This is what algebra is.

03 · Functional relationships

“When the length changes, the area changes predictably.”

They might not use the word function. They are discovering it anyway.

03
The materials already contain algebraic structures

Most guides never name this. Slow down and ask — and children begin to see what is already in their hands.

Checkerboard

A number can be decomposed into tens and units in infinite ways — total unchanged.

The concrete reality of which algebra is the abstract representation.

Flat bead frame

You can reorganise quantities while keeping the total the same.

A principle that holds in every addition problem they will ever do.

Bead bars

“What would the seventh bar look like?”

Seven beads. The number of beads equals the position of the bar. Always.

Constructive triangles

Two right triangles this way, always a rectangle.

Thinking algebraically about structure and constraint.

The principle

The materials already contain algebra.

The child just needs the question that makes the structure visible.

04
The journey does not skip the concrete

Extend it. Deepen it. Then gradually remove the physical support — while keeping the thinking intact.

Slow down. Draw attention to the patterns inside their concrete work. Ask them to predict without building.

The material gradually disappears from necessity without disappearing from memory.

05
A child has lived inside the golden beads for months. “I am thinking of a number. It has some tens and some units. When I put all the beads together to show it, I have seventeen beads total. What could the number be?” She tries one ten and six units — only sixteen. One ten and seven units — seventeen. She has worked with an unknown. She was given a constraint. She tested possibilities against the constraint. This is algebraic thinking with concrete support. Weeks later: same question, no beads. She can visualise them. The support is available in her mind.

06
Second year elementary. Multiplication board. Guide sits beside her. “What would happen if you kept your tens row the same, but doubled every number in your units row? Would the products change?” The guide is not asking her to follow a procedure. She is asking her to reason about structure. The child thinks. “If the units row is doubled, the products would be doubled.” — “Why?” — “Because every product uses the units row number. If that is twice as big, the product is twice as big.” The guide writes it down: if we double the units, the product doubles. The child sees her thinking written down.

07
Build thinking first · notation later

Introduce letters late in elementary — if at all.

When notation arrives well

From the child’s own language and need.

“I cannot count them all.” — “What if we used a letter to stand for the length we do not know?” Translating thinking that is already there.

When notation arrives badly

A new procedure to follow. Rules about manipulating letters.

Empty of meaning. The letter becomes a game piece — instead of a representation of something real.

Children who develop strong algebraic thinking through materials first, then learn notation, learn the notation faster and understand it more deeply.

08
The equity provocation · who gets to think algebraically

Algebraic thinking has been reserved for honours tracks. It is presented as something only certain minds can do. This is false.

Children from families with mathematical literacy are invited to think algebraically from babyhood. “Eight apples per pie — we’re making three.” That is algebra.

Children without this background do not have the informal experiences. If we delay algebra until it is formal and disconnected — some children never develop the capacity.

All children deserve to think algebraically. Build this thinking early — through materials — with guides who know what to listen for.

09
Neurodivergence note · some children see the patterns first

Children with pattern-recognition strengths, who think visually and spatially, often excel at algebraic thinking once they see the patterns in concrete materials.

The move from concrete algebra to formal algebraic notation can be difficult for them. The underlying thinking comes naturally.

Do not mistake their struggle with notation for an inability to reason.

10
The equal sign · the most misunderstood symbol

Most children think = means “the answer goes here.”

Ask them to solve  8 + 4 = __ + 5  and watch the confusion. Many will write 12. Some will write 17. Very few read the sign as balance.

This misreading closes the door to algebra later. Every equation they meet will feel like a directive, not a relationship.

Fix it early. Say the word “balance.” Use a scale. Put the answer on the left sometimes. Let = mean what it means.

11
Functional thinking · what happens to one thing when another moves

Before letters — relationships.

In the classroom

“If I add one more child, how many crackers do we need?”

In the botany corner

“Each week the bean grows two centimetres. Where will it be in four weeks?”

In the story

“The ship carried twice as many as the next one down. How many on the sixth?”

The shift

They stop asking “what is the answer?” and start asking “what is the rule?”

That is algebra.

12
Two faces of the letter · unknown, and varying

x is not one thing. It is two.

Sometimes x is a number we do not yet know — the unknown. “x + 4 = 11.” There is one answer.

Sometimes x is a number that can be anything — the variable. “The perimeter of a square is 4x.” Any x works.

Children who conflate these two meanings stall in middle school. Name the difference now, in plain words.

13
Naming the move · so they know they are doing it

When a child notices, say the word.

“You just generalised.” “That is a conjecture.” “You found a rule.” “You proved it.”

Small vocabulary. Huge effect. The child now has a name for a kind of thinking they did.

Later, when someone tells them algebra is hard, their body will remember — I have been doing this since I was eight.

14
Two children at the peg board. One lines up odd numbers — 1, 3, 5, 7. The other says, “when you add two odds, you always get an even.” The first pauses. “Always?” They check. 3 + 5 = 8. 7 + 9 = 16. “Always.” But why? They arrange the pegs in pairs. Two odds, each with one left over. The two leftovers meet. A pair. The child looks up. They have just understood a proof. You were ten feet away. You did not say anything.

15
Your own algebra history · in the room with you

Many of you were told, at thirteen, that you were not a math person.

Algebra was the year the ground gave way. You memorised steps without seeing structure. You passed, or you did not.

That belief is still in your body. It leaks into the room when a child asks a question you cannot immediately answer.

Work on this outside the classroom. Go back. Sit with a friend and the bead bars. Heal it. The children cannot afford to inherit it from you.
