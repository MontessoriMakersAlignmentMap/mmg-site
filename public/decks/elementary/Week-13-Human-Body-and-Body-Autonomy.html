Montessori Makers Residency Week 13 · Elementary
Why this matters right now

How you teach the human body curriculum is not a small thing.

You will navigate questions from families who believe certain information is inappropriate. You will navigate your own discomfort if bodies were shameful or mysterious in your childhood.

You will navigate the reality that for many children in your classroom, their bodies are already sites of violation and fear.

It is a practice in protecting children’s right to information while respecting their physical autonomy.

02
The Montessori framing · comprehensive, not euphemistic

Real names. Real processes. Scientific, not shameful.

The body as system.

Skeleton. Organs. Systems. How the body works — how it grows — how it reproduces.

The real words.

No euphemisms. Menstruation. Puberty. Conception. The language a physician would use.

Human variation.

Bodies come in infinite variations. All of those variations are normal.

A child with this foundation has something for thinking about her own body with clarity rather than confusion or fear.

03
But accurate is not enough

Accurate information is not enough.
Body autonomy is central.

The human body curriculum cannot be taught in a way that violates children’s bodily autonomy. This is obvious — and very much not how human body study is often done.

Children are not teaching tools. Children are not demonstrations.

04
What never happens in this classroom

The child’s body is never the teaching material.

Never

Required to point to parts on their own or a classmate’s body.

The diagrams, the skeleton, the atlas — those are the teaching tools. The child’s body is never on display.

Never

Pressured to engage with materials if they do not want to.

If a child does not want to look at reproduction materials — that is respected. Later. In private. Through books.

Never

Shamed for discomfort.

“You’re being silly.” “This is just biology.” — not in this classroom.

Always

Given a path to the information.

One-on-one time. Books alone. Revisiting later. The goal is access, not exposure.

05
Who is in this room

Many children have experienced sexual abuse or know someone who has. You are always teaching to that reality.

Girls learn young that their bodies are objects to be managed, displayed, controlled. Black and Indigenous children learn their bodies are deemed dangerous. Trans and non-binary children learn their bodies are wrong.

For these children, a shame-free and scientifically accurate human body curriculum is a gift. A chance at a different relationship with their own body.

06
What trauma may need from this curriculum

The materials may not be the trigger. The body memory is.

A child who was abused

May have trauma responses to anatomical materials that have nothing to do with the materials themselves.

A child told her body is wrong

May need explicit affirmation that her body is normal and good — named out loud, not implied.

A child who has experienced racism or fatphobia

Needs the curriculum to include the truth that bodies come in infinite variations and all are normal.

07
Reproduction · where families most often push back

Not indoctrination. Accurate representation of how human reproduction actually happens in the world.

Sperm and egg combine. An embryo grows in the uterus. This is one way humans create new humans.

There are others — adoption, assisted reproduction, surrogacy. Some people want biological children. Some do not. Some cannot. Some raise children without giving birth.

A child who learns this develops a more sophisticated understanding of biology and of human diversity.

08
Conversation with families · honest, not deceptive

When a family says “we do not want you talking about that” —

“I will not specifically teach lessons about that. But if a child asks a question, I will answer it truthfully. All people deserve to be treated with respect.”

The honest response

The guide never uses the curriculum to sneak in what families do not want. The guide also never lies to a child who asks a direct question. Space for honest conversation — not deception.

09
The guide’s own relationship to their body

Children are exquisitely attuned to adult discomfort.

They learn what to be ashamed of from how you respond to questions about bodies. A tight jaw. A flick of the eyes. A voice that changes register.

“That is a good question,” said while looking directly at the child, matter-of-fact — teaches one thing.
The same words, said with a held breath, teach another.

You have to do your own work. Before the lesson. Before the child asks.

12
The equity provocation · how the curriculum can still harm

The human body curriculum can reinforce the exact hierarchies it claims to disrupt.

Celebrating certain body types. Stigmatizing others.

Talking about “health” in ways that shame fat bodies or disabled bodies.

Menstruation as inconvenience rather than biology.

A normal biological process — not a problem to be managed.

Ignoring scarcity and stress.

Many children are developing in conditions that affect their bodies. Say it.

Silence about racism and the body.

Stress levels. Health outcomes. Which bodies get policed. All are biology too.

13
What an equitable human body curriculum actually teaches

Your body belongs to you and only you.

All bodies are worthy of respect. Variations in bodies are normal. Your body belongs to you.

And the world does not always honor this truth — and you have a right to be angry about that.

14
Neurodivergence note · multiple paths to the information

The point is access to information. Not a single path to it.

A child with sensory sensitivities may learn through text and diagrams rather than three-dimensional models. A child with trauma may need to engage with reproduction materials alone, not in a group. A child anxious about her own body may need explicit reassurance that she is normal — while she learns about development.

All paths lead to the same place. The child, equipped with the truth about her body.

15
Before the lesson · your own preparation

Your body. Your history with it. Before the lesson.

Know what you carry.

What were you taught was dirty, shameful, forbidden? That will leak unless you see it.

Know who is in the room.

Which children have histories of abuse, body shaming, medicalization? Prepare for them specifically.

Prepare the language.

Rehearse saying the real words out loud until they do not change your voice.

Prepare the opt-out.

A path that does not require explanation. Books. Later. Alone. Fine.
