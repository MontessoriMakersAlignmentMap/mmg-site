Montessori Makers Residency Week 27 · Elementary · Month 7
Why this matters right now

Geometry is moving from exploration into reasoning.

Children have built shapes, felt their angles, understood relationships through materials. Now the work is to deepen understanding of why — and begin informal proof.

Not writing proofs. Not symbolic rules. Developing the geometric reasoning that proofs rest on.

02
What proof means in elementary geometry

Proof is the act of convincing yourself and others that something is true — by showing why it must be true.

Secondary school picture

Two columns. Given. Prove. Prescribed format.

Proof as symbolic ritual. Often disconnected from understanding.

Elementary reality

Understanding is concrete. Reasoning is visible. Conviction is real.

She has convinced herself — not because someone told her, but because the materials did not lie to her.

03
She takes the constructive triangles. Builds a triangle with sides three, four, and five. Builds another with the same side lengths. They are identical. She cannot make a different shape with those measurements — no matter how hard she tries to vary it. The materials prove to her that the triangle is uniquely determined by its side lengths. If she builds another with these side lengths tomorrow, or next year, it will be identical. Next: bead-bar squares on each side of a right triangle. Square for three — nine beads. Square for four — sixteen beads. Square for five — twenty-five beads. Nine plus sixteen equals twenty-five. She has proven the Pythagorean theorem through building and counting.

04
The van Hiele levels · how geometric thinking develops

You cannot jump levels. The materials are the foundation — not a detour.

Level 1 · Visualisation

“That is a triangle.”

Shape as whole. Not yet the properties that define it. Young primary.

Level 2 · Analysis

Three sides. Three angles. Angles sum to something consistent.

Learning properties through materials. Most second- and third-year elementary children live here.

Level 3 · Informal deduction

If it is a rectangle, it must have four right angles.

Reasoning from properties to conclusions. Strong elementary work can bring children here by third year.

Formal proof — levels four and five — comes much later. Your job is the first three.

05
Equivalence · area as geometric reasoning

Two shapes are equivalent if they have the same area — even when they look completely different.

A child cuts shapes. Rearranges pieces. Discovers the pieces of one shape fit exactly onto another. She has proven something through manipulation.

Area is conserved through rearrangement. Shapes can be decomposed and recombined. The principle underneath much of what comes later.

06
A group exploring angle sums. Paper triangles of every kind. Acute. Right. Obtuse. “Find all three angles. Add them.” Different triangles. Different measures. Every one sums to one hundred eighty. “Do you think this is always true? How could we prove it?” One child folds. Each vertex toward the centre of the triangle. The three angles come together. They form a straight line. One hundred eighty degrees. Other children try. It works every time. They have convinced themselves.

Every triangle.
Every time.

The theorem

in a fold.

07
Spatial reasoning develops differently in different children

Some need to move their own bodies before they can reason about objects in space. This is not a limitation. This is how spatial reasoning develops.

Others need to rotate objects in their minds many times. Others trace shapes with their fingers. The materials support all of these. They are tangible.

The move to holding images in the mind happens through materials — not as a sudden leap.

08
The equity provocation · geometry is not a remedial space

Some children are told they are “visual learners” and directed to geometry — sometimes as a remedial space because they struggle with arithmetic. This is a false and harmful division.

Geometry is not a remedial space for children behind in arithmetic. It is not a fast track for the child ahead. It is a space where different minds can flourish.

Spatial reasoning is fundamental to engineering, architecture, physics. When we restrict access to rich geometric materials, we are making choices about who will have access to these futures.

09
Neurodivergence note · geometry is often where they shine

Children with spatial strengths struggle in computation-heavy mathematics. Geometry is the space where their minds shine and their confidence grows.

Children with dyslexia or language processing differences may find geometric work more accessible than abstract symbolic mathematics. Autistic and ADHD children often develop intense focus on geometric patterns.

Protect their access. Honour their expertise.

10
The language of proof · four small words that change thinking

Teach these words early. They carry the work.

Conjecture

“I think this is true.” Said out loud. A claim to be tested — not defended.

Example

A single case where it works. Useful — but not enough.

Counterexample

One case where it fails. And the conjecture falls. A gift, not a failure.

Always

The hinge. The child who asks “always?” has crossed into proof.

Listen for that word. Honour it when it comes.

11
Proof · is not yet explanation

A child convinced is not yet a child communicating.

The hands convinced them. Now — can they convince someone else? Without the material in front of them?

That second step is the beginning of formal proof. It is hard. Most children will not reach it at eight.

But ask. Invite it. “Can you draw it for me so I can see what you saw?” That request plants the seed.

12
One question · asked more than any other

“Why does that have to be true?”

Not “why is it true.” That invites explanation. “Why does it have to be.” That invites necessity.

Necessity is the mathematical mood. A thing is true because it could not be otherwise.

Keep this question in your pocket. Pull it out when a child declares something. Watch their face change.

13
Beyond the right answer · the richer geometry

A proof is a story about why.

A correct answer ends the work. A proof extends it — into the next shape, the next size, the next dimension.

When a child proves something, ask “would this still work for any triangle?” — and watch them travel.

The answer was the door. The proof is the house.

14
Your discipline · holding the silence while they think

Most proofs die because the adult spoke first.

The child is on the edge of seeing it. Their face is still. Their hand has stopped moving. The room is quiet. This is the thinking.

If you speak, you save them from the discomfort — and from the proof.

Your discipline is not in what you say. It is in what you can stand to not say.

15
The long thread · why Euclid is in the room

The child folding paper today is joining a line two thousand years long.

The Egyptians stretched a knotted rope. The Greeks asked why. Euclid wrote it down. Every geometry student since has been working in that inheritance.

Tell them. At nine, it matters.

They are not doing schoolwork. They are joining a human project.
