Montessori Makers Residency Week 31 · Elementary · Month 8
Why this matters right now

Solid geometry is not just an extension of plane geometry. It requires different thinking.

Volume is not a bigger version of area. Three-dimensional space involves coordinate systems and perspective that engage spatial reasoning in new ways.

A child with strong spatial reasoning in elementary is better positioned for mathematics, science, and engineering. Spatial reasoning is also equity work — certain groups have been encouraged to think spatially, and others have not.

02
Volume is not abstract · she builds it

A rectangular prism — three, four, five — holds sixty unit cubes.

The experience

She fills the prism with unit cubes. Counts. Sixty.

Then builds a different one. Different count. A pattern emerges.

The generalisation

Three times four times five.

The formula is not a rule to memorise. It is a generalisation of something she has experienced.

The child builds the concept before encountering the abstraction.

03
Classification emerges from structure — not arbitrary rules

She holds a cube frame. Feels the structure.

01 · Count

Twelve edges. Eight vertices. Six faces.

Felt before named.

02 · Compare

Are all the edges the same length? All faces the same shape?

Cube — yes. Rectangular prism — no.

03 · Classify

Some polyhedra have triangular faces. Some rectangular. Some mixed.

The categories emerge from manipulation, not from a textbook.

04
Transfer of understanding · 2D to 3D

A face of a three-dimensional shape is a two-dimensional shape.

If she knows the area of a square — she can find the surface area of a cube. Area of each face, added together.

She does not need to be told the formula. She derives it from what she already knows. This is the marker of deep conceptual knowledge.

05
Nets · the bridge between flat and solid

Unfold a cube. You have a net. Fold the net. You have a cube.

What she is doing

Folding and unfolding patterns — discovering which work and which do not.

Not all patterns fold into valid nets. Some sides will not align.

What she is developing

The spatial thinking that matters later for coordinate systems.

She understands that space has structure.

06
She has built a rectangular prism — two, three, four. Twenty-four unit cubes. You ask: “How many cubes fit in a prism three, four, five?” She closes her eyes. Visualising. You can see her thinking. Eyes open. “Sixty?” — “How did you figure that out?” — “I can see it in my head. If I make it one more on this side, it gets bigger.” She builds it to check. Sixty cubes. She calculated three times four times five. She understands the formula because she has built the relationship — and because she can see three-dimensional space in her mind.

07
Spatial reasoning · as important as computation, perhaps more

Can she visualise a shape from a different angle? Predict what would happen if she rotated it? Understand how pieces fit into a whole?

Fundamental to mathematics, science, architecture, engineering, the visual arts. Often under-developed in schools — because we prioritise symbolic computation over spatial thinking.

In Montessori it develops through materials, through manipulation, through exploration. A child with strong Sensorial foundations extends naturally into sophisticated spatial thinking.

08
The equity provocation · spatial reasoning is taught or withheld

Girls develop strong spatial reasoning when they are given materials and opportunities.

They are often discouraged from spatial play from childhood — given dolls instead of blocks, told that building is “not for them.” By elementary, some have internalised that they are not spatially minded.

A girl who stops developing spatial skills loses access to entire fields. The same is true for children from cultures where certain kinds of play have not been available or valued.

Name it when you see it: “You are thinking about how those pieces fit together in three dimensions. That is geometric thinking.”

09
Neurodivergence note · geometry as first language

Some children have vivid spatial reasoning but struggle with language or symbolic representation.

Three-dimensional materials let them demonstrate their thinking without written work or verbal explanation.

A child who hyperfocuses on geometric structure is developing deep mathematical understanding. Protect their access to this work.

10
The Platonic solids · five objects, a cosmos of thinking

Every one a proof about the nature of space.

Tetrahedron

Four triangles. The smallest closed shape. Let them hold it. Let them notice it has no rest.

Cube & octahedron

Duals. Stand one inside the other. Faces become vertices. A kind of quiet magic.

Dodecahedron

Twelve pentagons. The shape that seems to say the universe is allowed to be beautiful.

Icosahedron

Twenty triangles. Only five such shapes exist. Ever. Ask them — why only five?

That question is the beginning of a life in geometry.

11
Nets · unfolding a solid into a plane

To unfold a shape is to see its bones.

A cube has eleven distinct nets. A tetrahedron has two. Let a child find them. Let them argue about whether two are really the same.

This is topology walking in a child’s fingers.

Keep paper and scissors nearby. Keep your comment to yourself.

12
Volume · felt by filling, before it is calculated

The formula comes last. The sand comes first.

Pour sand from a cube-shaped box into a pyramid of the same base and height. Three pours. Exactly. Let the child discover the ratio — not memorise it.

Fill a cylinder with water. Pour it into a cone. Three times. Space is telling them something.

When the formula arrives at eleven, they will say — of course. They already knew.

13
Mental rotation · the invisible skill

Turning a shape in the mind is a muscle.

Research shows this skill is practicable — not innate. Children who build with blocks, who draw from observation, who fold and unfold — get stronger at it.

Children who are not given the chance — often arrive at calculus unable to visualise.

The block corner is not childish. It is the gym for a future mathematician.

14
The architecture of the classroom · teaches geometry too

Children read the space they move through.

Where are the corners? What is the height of the ceiling? How does light fall? Can they see outside? Can they measure what they see?

Give them tape measures. Give them the task of drawing the room to scale.

The classroom is your largest three-dimensional material. Use it.

15
Your own spatial history · becomes the room’s ceiling

Were you allowed to build? Were you allowed to take things apart?

Many adults arrive in the classroom shy of solid geometry. They say — “I am not good at visualising.”

That sentence, said once, will cap the child’s horizon.

Do your own building this year. Fold a dodecahedron yourself before you offer it. The hand re-teaches the hand.
