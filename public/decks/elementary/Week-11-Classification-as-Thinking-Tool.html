The same century that
built sensorial materials —
also built scientific racism.

Classification is one of the most powerful thinking tools the human mind has invented. It is also one of the most dangerous.

Montessori Makers Residency Week 11 · Elementary
Why this matters right now

Before the child asks the question,
you need to be ready.

“So if evolution is real, and humans evolved from less advanced animals — doesn’t that mean there is a hierarchy of how advanced humans are?”

A guide who has only thought about classification’s pedagogical beauty is not prepared to protect the child in that moment. You need answers that are true, honest, and that do not leave that child believing science supports racism.

02
What classification actually trains

The mind makes meaning through organization.

Notice what is essential.

Sorting leaves by shape trains the eye to see what matters — and what is incidental.

Rank characteristics.

Some traits matter more than others for a given purpose. This is judgment.

Try another system.

“What if I sorted them by where they live instead?” Multiple systems, each revealing.

Think about anything.

Ideas, experiences, feelings, ethical questions — the logic of classification transfers.

This is abstract thinking. This is logical reasoning. This is the foundation of scientific classification — and of how a mind learns to think.

03
Why classification comes before the hawk

The taxonomy exercise is not about memorizing phylum.

It is about training the mind in the logic of classification. About helping the child see that nature is not chaotic — that there are patterns to how organisms relate.

The child who has learned to think this way can then read about any specific animal and automatically ask the right questions: What defines its group? How is it similar? How is it different? What helped it survive?

04
The difficult truth

The same logic of classification has justified some of the worst horrors in human history.

i.

19th-century Europeans took Linnaeus’s system — built for plants and animals — and applied it to human beings.

ii.

They measured skulls. Compared proportions. Built elaborate arguments for superior and inferior races.

iii.

This was not fringe. This was mainstream science, taught in universities, used to justify colonialism, slavery, genocide, eugenics, apartheid.

iv.

The logic itself is neutral. The humans using it are not. We bring prejudice. We bring desire for power.

05
And it did not end

That logic is still present every time someone suggests

— that some group of people is more or less capable. More or less evolved. Closer to or further from some imagined peak of human development.

It is present in standardized testing. In how learning differences get pathologized. In how children are sorted into tracking systems. The elementary guide must hold both truths at once.

06
The guide’s double job

Teach the first. Protect against the second.

Essential

Classification as a thinking tool.

Powerful. Necessary. The way the human mind builds structure from complexity. Teach this with care and precision.

Betrayal

Classification as a tool for human hierarchies.

A betrayal of science. A betrayal of children. Refuse this explicitly. Do not wait for the moment.

07
Begin by being explicit

Classification is a human tool.

Not objective truth. Different cultures classify the same organisms in different ways — all valid, all useful for different purposes.

Indigenous plant classifications are often organized by use or habitat rather than evolutionary relationship — and they encode thousands of years of ecological knowledge Linnaean classification cannot capture.

When you teach Linnaean classification, you are teaching one way of organizing knowledge. A tool — which, like any tool, can be used well or badly.

08
What evolution actually teaches

A bird’s wing is not a final form.
It is a solution to a problem.

Characteristics change over time through evolution. They are not fixed, not essential. They are adaptations shaped by millions of years of environmental pressure.

This is profoundly different from racial thinking — which treats human characteristics as fixed markers of an internal “essence.” Evolution says: there is no ladder. Only the beautiful fact of organisms shaped by the worlds they live in.

09
Addressing the history directly · not separate from the lesson

A child in elementary is old enough to hear this plainly.

“For a long time, some people tried to use the tools of classification to say that some human groups were better or less advanced than others. Scientists have proven this is not true. All humans have the same capacities. All humans have the same value. We use classification to understand plants and animals — but we never use it to rank human beings.”

A child who has been told this explicitly is inoculated against the moment when someone suggests otherwise.

10
Two nine-year-olds, sorting organisms into a dichotomous key. Has a backbone? Has hair? Then a question they can’t answer — “Do fish give birth to live babies or eggs?” One child looks up: “But how do we know these questions are the right ones? Who decided backbones matter more than, like, whether it lives in water?” The guide pauses. This is the moment.
Scene A · the question the guide has been waiting for

“So scientists
are not discovering
how things are.
They are deciding
how to organize them.”

The guide pulls out three systems — scientists’, a traditional Indigenous one, a gardener’s. Each valid. Each different.

Both powerful — and a reason to be careful about what we organize, and why.

11
A month later. A different child, reading about human evolution. His question has been building: “So if humans evolved from other animals — does that mean some humans are more evolved than others?” The guide has been waiting. She does not panic. She tells him — evolution is not a ladder. We have all been evolving for the same amount of time. People used to claim some humans evolved more than others, and they used that false idea to hurt people. That is completely wrong.
Scene B · the moment you prepared for

She does not panic.
She has been
waiting for this question
for weeks.

Evolution does not work like that. All living things right now are equally evolved. Just in different directions.

“All human beings — no matter what they look like or where they come from — are equally human and equally capable.”

12
The equity provocation · who is in this room

Many of the children in your classroom have already been classified as deficit.

Sorted into special education because their thinking does not match the standardized model. Streamed into lower tracks because their background did not match the school’s expectations. Told — directly or indirectly — that their community, their race, their family structure is inferior.

If you teach classification without teaching its history, you are teaching children that systems of hierarchy are natural and scientific. You are making them vulnerable to someone later using the same logic against them.

13
What forcefulness sounds like

This is a tool for understanding nature.

It is not a tool for ranking people.

And if anyone ever tries to use it that way — that is bad science, and that is wrong.

Forcefulness, here, is not an edge on your voice. It is the clarity of refusing to leave it implicit.

14
Neurodivergence note · different minds, same tool

An autistic child may be drawn to classification with great precision and satisfaction. This is beautiful.

An ADHD child may struggle with the sequential logic of a dichotomous key — but may excel at generating the questions that guide the classification.

Neither is better. Neither is worse. Both are thinking with the tool in their own way.

15
The guide’s preparation

What you prepare before you open the cabinet.

Three systems on the table.

Linnaean. Indigenous. Vernacular. Let the child see that each reveals something different.

Language prepared, not rehearsed.

Know what you will say when the racism question arrives. Say it before.

Let them build a key first.

Before handing over the scientists’ key, let the child discover what a key is by making one.

Name the history once, clearly.

Not as an aside. Not in a whisper. As part of the lesson.

16
The week, in one line

The tool is neutral.
The teacher is not.
Teach classification
with its history
or teach it wrong.
