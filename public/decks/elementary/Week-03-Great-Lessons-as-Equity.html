Montessori Makers Residency Week 3 · Elementary
The Great Lessons as designed · a framework with a bias

The Great Lessons are
built on a single,
consequential premise:
that human progress
is linear and measurable.

Hunter-gatherer → agricultural → civilized. Each stage an advance on the last. Each stage measured by the standards — and achievements — of Europe. This makes the lessons sequential, coherent, compelling for children to follow. It also makes them incomplete in specific, repairable ways.

02
Context · the intellectual world Montessori inherited

Montessori was writing in
a European scholarly world
that placed its own
civilization at the
apex of human development.

She pulled from the science, anthropology, and history that surrounded her. Much of it was shot through with assumptions about race, progress, and civilization that we now recognize as false. She cannot be faulted for working with the tools of her time.

But we can be faulted for continuing to work with those tools now — when better ones are available.

03
What each lesson quietly teaches · a bias audit

The surface content is history. The structural content is a value system.

Coming of Humans

Early humans as primitive — lacking sophistication, awaiting agriculture.

What it erasesHunter-gatherer cultures that sustained themselves for millennia. Sophisticated ecological knowledge. Rich spiritual and artistic traditions. The choice to stay.
Coming of Agriculture

Agriculture as liberation — the moment humans took control of the food supply.

What it erasesThe contingency. The trade-offs. The resource scarcity, hierarchy, and warfare it also enabled. That it emerged in many places for many reasons.
Coming of Writing

Writing as the beginning of civilization — the moment knowledge could accumulate.

What it erasesQuipu. Oral traditions preserving legal codes, history, and spiritual teaching with precision. Every other way humans have recorded what mattered.

Present these lessons as designed and you teach children that some ways of being human are advanced, some primitive; some histories matter, some do not. The cosmic gift becomes a cosmic message about whose place is valued.

03
Same lesson, two tellings · the Coming of Humans

The story is the same story. What lands on the child is not.

Traditional telling

Early humans as hunter-gatherers — primitive, not yet having discovered agriculture.

The guide shifts tone at agriculture. Now humans are “advanced enough to control the food supply.” Now civilization becomes possible.

What a child whose ancestors maintained hunting and gathering cultures hears: your people were primitive. They did not achieve advancement.
Expanded telling

Early humans as explorers — developing knowledge of the places they lived, tools and stories suited to their contexts.

Agriculture told not as the one right choice but as one strategy among many. The sophistication of cultures that maintained other ways for millennia is named.

What that same child hears: your people developed strategies for living that worked. Your ancestors’ knowledge was sophisticated and real. Your ways are part of the human story.
05
Three questions to ask before you tell any Great Lesson

A diagnostic. Before you open your mouth.

01 · who is centered

Whose civilization does this story present as the reference point?

If “advanced” means “like Europe,” you have a problem to name.

02 · who is erased

Whose ancestors go unmentioned in the telling?

Silence is a message. Children hear it even when adults do not.

03 · who must translate

Which children must do extra work to find themselves in this story?

That extra work is not neutral. It teaches them where they belong.

Scene · the expanded lesson on writing

Quipu. Songlines.
Cuneiform. Oracle
bones. The Cherokee
syllabary.

Humans found many ways to preserve and transmit knowledge. Writing was one way — developed in certain places, for certain purposes. Not the only way. Not the inevitable way.

The child whose ancestors’ knowledge was kept in story and memory does not need to adopt someone else’s definition of civilization to find her people in the cosmic story.

07
Small edits, large consequences · language moves for the expanded telling

The story changes at the level of single sentences.

Traditional

“Then humans learned to farm, and civilization began.”

Expanded

“In some places, humans began to farm. In other places, they chose to keep moving with the land.”

Traditional

“With writing, humans could finally record what mattered.”

Expanded

“Humans have always found ways to remember. Some carved, some knotted, some sang, some wrote.”

08
Who this is for · every child in the room

The expanded telling is
not only for the child
whose ancestors were erased.

Every child in the room is being formed by the story they hear. The child whose family’s way is centered learns that hers is the standard. The child whose family’s way is erased learns that hers is absent.

Neither of these is the child you are trying to raise.

09
A caution · what the expanded telling is not

This is not a slogan. It is not a unit on diversity.

  • ×You are not replacing the Great Lessons with a different curriculum.
  • ×You are not adding a “multicultural week” to compensate.
  • ×You are not performing awareness.
  • ✓You are telling the same cosmic story — more accurately.
  • ✓You are making room for the children you actually have.
  • ✓You are honoring what is true.
10
Preparing to tell · what you do before the children arrive

The expanded telling
is a research practice.
Not a performance.

Before you tell the Coming of Humans, you spend a week reading Indigenous scholars on hunter-gatherer cultures. Before you tell the Coming of Writing, you study quipu and songlines the way you would study cuneiform. You do not extemporize your way into expansion.

The children will feel the difference between a guide who knows the expanded story and one who is gesturing at it.

11
The equity provocation · this is not betrayal, this is love

You are not discovering
hidden equity in
Montessori. You are
doing the work of
equity.

You are taking a framework that is powerful but incomplete and making it whole. This is not betrayal. This is love. It is taking the best of what Montessori offers and extending it in directions she was not able to extend it.

The fundamental Montessori principle is that the child matters. A cosmic story that erases her ancestors cannot serve the child in front of you. Honoring the principle requires repairing the story.

12
Hard conversations · what to say when asked

Someone — a parent, a colleague, a board member — will ask: “Isn’t this just politics in the classroom?”

You can say:

“The original telling was also political — it just matched the politics of the room it was designed for. We are matching ours to the children we actually teach.”

You can also say:

“Accuracy is not politics. We now know more about human history than Montessori did. We are teaching what we know.”

13
What children notice in the expanded telling

They catch it. Every time.

“My grandma’s people did that.”

Said quietly, to no one, the first time a child finds her family in the story.

“Why didn’t they just farm?”

The moral-reasoning engine kicks in. This is what you wanted.

“Who decided they were primitive?”

A second-plane child interrogating a premise. She can do this. She will.

15
Four practices to build this week

Concrete, doable, by Friday. Pick one.

01 · audit one lesson

Take one Great Lesson script you use. Mark every sentence that assumes a European reference point.

02 · name five writings

List five non-alphabetic systems of recording. Find an image of each. Put them in the classroom.

03 · map the children

Quietly list where each child’s family is from. Ask: does my current telling show up for them?

04 · read one scholar

One Indigenous or scholar-of-color historian, on one topic you teach. Before next week.

16
A note on neurodivergence and the expanded telling

The child who processes
differently is also the child
who hears every implication
in the story.

Autistic children often detect contradictions and value-loaded language more precisely than their peers. An inconsistent cosmic story — one that claims universality but centers one people — registers for them as wrong, not just incomplete.

Accuracy is an accessibility feature.
