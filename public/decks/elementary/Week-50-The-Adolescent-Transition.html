Residency · Week Fifty · Elementary

You will not teach them in the third plane.
But the work you do with them now
is shaping who they will be when they are adolescents.

Montessori Makers Residency Week 50 · Elementary · Month 12 · Behaviour, Community, Capstone
Why this matters right now

Elementary is not an end in itself. It is a bridge.

Some children will stay in Montessori. Some will move to traditional schools. All of them will become adolescents.

The work you are doing now is either preparing them for that — or not.

02
What the third plane asks of young people

Who am I? Where do I fit? What is my role in the world?

Identity

Forming values, beliefs, self.

Abstract thinking comes online.

Brain

Literally rewiring.

Capable of new thought. Also fragile.

Social

From small community to identity in a larger world.

Peers, belonging, relationship, sexuality.

Rebellion

A feature, not a bug.

If there is no rebellion, something is wrong.

03
What elementary years build toward adolescence

Five capacities the adolescent will need — and elementary builds.

Intellectual

History, science, literature, math — the foundation of abstract thought.

Real content. Expect them to think.

Moral

Fairness, justice, restorative practice, equity.

A moral framework to navigate hard questions.

Independent thinking

Making choices. Having ideas. Disagreeing respectfully.

She will need her own worldview.

Community

Real belonging. Real democracy. Solving problems together.

An adolescent needs somewhere she belongs.

Trust in her body

Movement, play, physical competence.

Her body is about to change.

04
How you actually build it

Not by controlling her. Not by being permissive. By building genuine capacity and genuine trust.

Do not dumb down the curriculum. Ask her what she thinks. Be genuinely interested, even when it is different from yours.

Do not punish disagreement. Build a community where people disagree and work it out — not one where everyone agrees and everyone is nice.

Honour her body. Make space for movement and physical challenge.

05
Prepare her by telling her what is coming

Talk about puberty. Talk about sexuality. Talk about the emotional intensity of adolescence. Talk about the brain changes. Do not pretend adolescence is not coming.

Tell her what is normal and healthy. Tell her what to expect of her own body, her own heart, her own thinking.

06
The third plane — if they continue in Montessori

Adolescents need real work. They need to contribute. They need to feel that their work matters.

Traditional school

Adolescents are still infantilised — told what to do, managed, controlled.

Treated as children who must be contained.

Montessori adolescent program

Real responsibility. Service to community. Treated as emerging adults.

Trusted. Challenged. Expected to contribute.

07
Scene · the child asking hard questions about fairness

She is questioning your authority. In a traditional system, she would be told to stop asking hard questions.
In your classroom, you engage with her questions. You help her think more deeply. You celebrate her moral passion.

You are preparing her for adolescence, when she will need that moral passion to navigate a complex world.

08
Scene · the child who is starting to hide

She loves learning. She is starting to worry that her peers will think she is weird for loving it.
In your classroom, you make it cool to be smart. You build a culture where thinking deeply is valued.

You are preparing her to be willing to be authentic in adolescence — to pursue her interests even if they are not popular.

09
Equity provocation · adolescents are treated with suspicion

A Black adolescent is seen as aggressive. A poor adolescent is seen as dangerous. A transgender adolescent is seen as confused.

If you teach elementary without preparing children for this, you have failed them.

Prepare them to be proud of their identity. Prepare them to resist dehumanisation. Prepare them to know their community is valuable and that they belong.

10
Neurodivergence note · adolescence gets harder

Social complexity increases dramatically. The sensory environment of traditional schools is often overwhelming.

In elementary, help her develop self-awareness about her neurodivergence — and self-advocacy.

Teach her to understand her needs. Teach her to ask for what she needs.

11
The grief · yours, hers, both real

You raised her. Now she must leave you.

Six years. You know the way she holds a pencil. The way she laughs. The name of her rabbit. The fight with her sister. Every ordinary, hard-won detail.

Grief is the correct response to this. It means the work was real.

Let it be felt — in staff meetings, in your own journal, with your partner at home. Do not numb it. Let the child see you care enough to mourn.

12
The rupture she needs · a developmental necessity

She must push you away to become herself.

The warm child of elementary will, in adolescence, be distant. Cool. Even dismissive. She is not rejecting you. She is doing the developmental task of separating from the adults who raised her.

Take no offence. Expect it. The child who cannot separate at twelve — separates badly at twenty.

Your quiet, steady presence — at a respectful distance — is what she needs. Not your hurt feelings.

13
The body changing · in year six, under your watch

The body speaks before the child is ready.

Growth spurts. Menstruation. Voice changes. The new self-consciousness. The child’s body is announcing a transition her mind has not yet caught up with.

The classroom must be a place where this is not whispered about, not joked about, not made into spectacle. It is simply part of being alive.

A private stash of pads, in a small labelled cupboard she knows about, will matter more than any lesson you plan this year.

14
The peer group becomes everything

Her friends are now her mirror.

The opinion of her peers, at twelve, weighs more than your opinion. More than her parents’. That is not a failure. It is developmentally correct.

Your work now is to curate the peer group. The room. Who is in the room, and how they speak to one another, becomes the oxygen she breathes.

At six, you managed her behaviour. At twelve, you manage the culture.

15
The last days · a ritual you must create

Mark the ending. Do not let it leak out.

A meal. A walk. A letter from each younger child. A reading of favourite pieces. A naming of what she has given the community. A formal goodbye.

Cultures that mark their transitions — hold their adolescents more gently. Cultures that do not — send them out unmarked, and bewildered.

Her twelve-year-old self will forget most of the year. She will remember the day she was sent off. Make it good.

17
Closing question

Look back. Then look forward.

What do you remember about your own adolescence?

What did you wish someone had prepared you for?

How can you prepare the children you are teaching so that they are ready?
