Residency · Week Forty-Six · Elementary

In Primary, a neurodivergent child’s difference may not be obvious.
In Elementary — where work is collaborative and the social world is complex —
that difference becomes visible.
And sometimes it becomes a problem.

Montessori Makers Residency Week 46 · Elementary · Month 11 · Behaviour, Community, Capstone
Why this matters right now

The elementary classroom is built on collaboration. Groups. Community meetings. Self-management. Complex social dynamics.

Every one of those things can be exhausting, confusing, or overwhelming for a neurodivergent child. These are not behaviour problems.

They are genuine difficulties. If we do not understand them, we will fail these children.

02
The move from explicit rules to implicit ones

The social world of Elementary runs on implicit rules. A child who understands the explicit ones may still be baffled.

Explicit

Take turns. Share materials. Raise your hand.

These are the rules she already knows.

Implicit

Care about your friend’s feelings. Show interest in what she cares about. Notice when a joke isn’t a joke.

Nobody will teach these. But they are the ones being graded.

03
How neurodivergence gets read as character

What the group sees is not what is happening. She is not rude. She is not mean. She is not obtuse.

Autism

Takes turns perfectly — and her friends think she does not care about them.

She followed the explicit rule. She did not know the implicit one.

ADHD

Interrupts. Speaks before thinking. Seems selfish.

She was not trying to be mean. But she gets marked as unkind — and the mark sticks.

Either

Misses sarcasm, tone, anger. Takes jokes literally.

Marked as obtuse. She genuinely did not understand what was happening socially.

04
Collaboration for the neurodivergent child

The best thing and the most exhausting thing — at the same time.

Enriching

The energy of collaboration is what her brain needs. She may be more regulated in a group than alone.

The social stimulation is medicine.

Exhausting

Reading cues, managing impulses, staying focused while people move and talk.

She may come home and be unable to manage anything else. The day depleted her.

05
Specific strategies · ADHD in group work

You modify the environment to meet her. You do not ask her to become someone she is not.

Chunking

Shorter segments of group work.

Reset more often.

Role

A specific job that holds her engaged.

Timekeeper. Brainstormer. Scribe.

Movement

Stand. Fidget. Step out and return.

Movement is regulation.

Signal

A nonverbal cue between the two of you.

A tool for her impulse — not a public reprimand.

06
Specific strategies · autism in group work
Teach the implicit

Say it out loud. Make the unwritten rules written.

Explicit teaching of implicit social logic. This is not dumbing down — it is showing respect.

Visual support

A structure card. A script. A role checklist.

Something she can see and return to when the social noise overwhelms.

Social partner

Pair her with a peer who naturally explains social things.

Not a caretaker — a translator.

Graduated time

A time-limited break from group work — while you build the skill.

The break is a bridge, not an exit.

07
Modifying community meeting — without excluding

A child who cannot speak in large groups speaks to the guide privately. The guide conveys what she said.

Overwhelmed by the space? Sit somewhere else. Take a break. Rejoin.

Needs movement? A fidget. Permission to stand.

The goal is never to exclude. The goal is to find the way she can authentically participate.

08
Build on strengths — deliberately

Her neurodivergence is not something to overcome to participate. Structure the collaboration so it is what makes the collaboration richer.

Autism

Extraordinary attention to detail. Careful. Makes sure everyone is heard.

Let her be the scribe. The documentarian. The person who notices what the group missed.

ADHD

Creative. Associative. Generates what others cannot.

Let her be the brainstormer. The one who sees around the problem.

09
Scene · a role that turns a trait into a gift

A child with ADHD has good ideas. He interrupts. He gets distracted. He misses when others are talking.
You give him a role. He is the timekeeper. He tracks how long the group has worked. He signals the transitions.

You tap your nose when he is about to interrupt. He pauses. He waits. He does not feel ashamed. He has a tool.

10
Scene · a role that holds her inside the meeting

Community meeting overwhelms her. The unwritten rules confuse her. She does not know when to talk and when to listen.
You give her a visual of the meeting’s structure. You give her a role.
She is the person who makes sure everyone has had a chance to speak.

Her script: “Has anyone else not spoken yet?” Her autism does not disappear. She now has tools. She can participate. She is being held by the structure.

11
Equity provocation · neurodivergence read through bias

A Black neurodivergent child is marked aggressive. A low-income neurodivergent child is marked difficult. Neurodivergence is always read through the lens of bias.

You have to be willing to see the neurodivergence underneath the bias.

Resist the narrative that the child is bad. See the child as a child whose brain works differently.

12
A note for neurodivergent guides

If you are neurodivergent, you already understand some of what these children are experiencing. Your experience is valuable.

And it is specific to you. Do not assume your accommodations will work for another neurodivergent child.

Ask. Learn. Individualise.

13
Friendship scaffold · elementary social work is explicit

Some children need the social curriculum taught.

“When someone says hello, you say hello back.” “When someone is talking about their weekend, you can ask — what did you do?” Not manners. Not compliance. Scripts. For children who do not read them automatically.

Explicit. Rehearsed. Without shame. The same way we teach long division.

The autistic child who is taught social scripts kindly — has a tool for life. The autistic child left to infer — is abandoned in plain sight.

14
Movement · the accommodation most easily given

Let her stand. Let her walk.

The ADHD child in particular — but most children — do not concentrate better sitting still. They concentrate better while moving. A wobble stool. A walking path. A standing desk. A five-minute lap of the garden, returning to the lesson refreshed.

Movement is not a behaviour to manage. It is a cognition to respect.

Every Montessori classroom is already built for this. Lean into it.

15
Language shift · what we say, and stop saying

Three phrases to retire. And what to say instead.

Retire

“Why can’t you just sit still?” “Use your words.” “Look at me when I’m talking.”

Try instead

“Do you need to move?” “Take your time — I’ll wait.” “I’m listening even if you’re not looking.”

Why it matters

Each old phrase asks the neurodivergent child to perform neurotypical. Each new phrase asks a real question.

The quiet gift

Neurotypical children benefit too. These phrases work for everyone.

The accommodation for one child becomes the culture of the room.
