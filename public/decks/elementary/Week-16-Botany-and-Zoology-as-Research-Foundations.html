Montessori Makers Residency Week 16 · Elementary
Why this matters right now

Your job is not to be the person who knows things.

Your job is to be the guide who helps children find out what they want to know — and then supports them in the work of finding answers.

Botany and zoology are where this becomes obvious. Where the rubber meets the road.

02
The inversion · who asks the question

In elementary Montessori, the child asks.

Traditional science ed

The teacher asks the question. The child finds the answer.

A predetermined destination. The adult is the source of both the question and the validation.

·
Montessori elementary

The child asks. The guide helps organize the question and find resources.

Starts with wonder and observation. Not with a predetermined answer.

03
What research actually is

A method of thinking that transfers across every discipline.

Observe carefully.

Careful attention to detail. Matters in everything.

Ask precise questions.

Not “how do plants work?” — “why are these leaves so thick?”

Gather evidence.

Field guides. Experts. Direct observation. Many sources.

Examine what others have found.

Standing on shoulders. Not reinventing the wheel.

Make an argument from evidence.

Not “I believe.” — “Here is what I observed, here is what it means.”

Know how to find out.

You do not have to know everything. You just have to know the path.

04
Botany · the child’s obsessions are the curriculum

A child fascinated by succulents. Spending weeks on why the leaves are thick and fleshy.

A child wanting to know how a seed knows to grow roots down and leaves up. A child obsessed with how plants climb. How vining plants find what to grab onto.

These obsessions are sacred. They are not distractions. They are the primary curriculum.

The guide removes obstacles and provides resources.

05
Zoology · adaptation as the unifying question

“Why does the owl have eyes on the front of its face while the prey animal has eyes on the sides?”

Beaks and feet. Life cycles. Metamorphosis — by actually raising caterpillars. Different animals adapted for different environments. Animals that live in similar places having similar adaptations even when they are not related.

The child is learning zoology — and also evolutionary thinking.

06
Your not-knowing is the asset

If you are an expert on every plant and animal, children become dependent on you for answers.

They learn that the way to find out is to ask an adult who already knows.

If you do not know — and you model how you find out — children learn a more valuable skill. You model the behavior of a scientist. Admitting what you do not know. Then figuring out how to find out.

07
The sentences that build researchers

Not “the answer is…”

“I do not know why the ants are organized that way. Let’s look at what we know about ants and see if we can figure it out.”

“I do not know. Let me find a resource that might tell us.”

“I do not really understand this either by just looking at pictures. Would you want to contact someone who knows about fish?”

08
The child saw a bee visiting flowers. Now wants to know everything. How do they know where the flowers are? How do they make honey? The guide does not lecture. Together they list the wonders. Videos. A beekeeper’s journal. Labelled drawings. The guide suggests interviewing a local beekeeper. The child writes questions. A week later, a phone call. The child comes back with pages of notes. “She said bees can see ultraviolet light — that is how they see patterns on flowers that we cannot see.”

10
A child studying sea creatures. Still confused about how fish breathe underwater. Pictures and diagrams have not clicked. The guide says — “I do not really understand this either by just looking at pictures. Would you want to contact someone who knows about fish? Maybe they could explain it better than we can figure out together.” The child writes an email to a marine biologist. Two days later, a detailed explanation and a video link. The child watches it. “Oh — now I get it.”

11
The environment · set up for independent investigation

The guide does not stand between the child and the resource, explaining everything.

Collections.

Seeds. Shells. Insects. Feathers. Touchable, sortable, returnable.

Field guides.

Organized to be used. Not locked behind an adult.

Sketching materials.

Journals for documentation. The naturalist’s basic tool.

Propagation materials.

For watching plants actually grow. Weeks, not minutes.

Live creatures.

An ant farm. A terrarium. A butterfly garden. What the space will support.

The guide, available.

For questions that deepen thinking. Not for explanations that cap it.

12
When the investigation takes them into emotional territory

A child troubled that predators kill prey. A child upset that species are disappearing. A child distressed by factory farming. These are not problems with the curriculum. These are the curriculum working.

Your job is not to protect them from the reality. Your job is to hold space for their feelings while helping them understand complexity.

“It is hard to understand that animals hunt and eat other animals. That is part of how nature works. It is also true that humans have changed how much hunting happens and where animals live. There are people working on this. What do you want to know more about?”

13
The equity provocation · nature is not elsewhere

Botany and zoology can position nature as something that happens elsewhere. Or as something the child is part of, here.

An urban guide studies the plants in cracks, the insects on rooftops, the birds in window boxes. Nature is everywhere, even in spaces that seem abandoned.

And also — having to study nature in marginal spaces is not normal, is not how all children live, and is not okay. A wealthy child with access to pristine nature needs to learn that not all children have this, and why that is unjust.

Botany and zoology are not neutral subjects. How you teach them either reinforces nature-as-privilege — or honours investigation wherever the child lives.

14
Neurodivergence note · many paths to the creature

Deep special interest is exactly what elementary Montessori is designed for.

Anxiety around insects or animals.

Study through photography, video, diagrams — rather than live observation.

Sensory sensitivity to textures.

Explore plants through observation and drawing — not by touching every part.

Intense special interest.

A child who dives far deeper than others into one plant or one animal. That is not a problem. That is the design.

15
A child’s obsession is not a distraction

A child might spend weeks on succulents. On how seeds know which way is up. On how vines climb.

These obsessions are sacred in Montessori. They are not distractions. They are the primary curriculum.

The guide removes obstacles. The guide provides resources. The guide does not redirect to something more “balanced.”
