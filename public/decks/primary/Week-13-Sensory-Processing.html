Montessori Makers Residency Week 13 · Primary
Opening

If you are not listening
to how a child
processes sensation —
you are designing curriculum
for a child who does not exist.

The nervous system tells you everything you need to know before the lesson begins.

02
Why this, why now

Three months in.
You have been
watching nervous systems
all along.

Some children move toward complexity. Some away. Some can not stop spinning the bells. Some cover their ears at the sound of them.

These are not behavior problems. These are sensory processing profiles — and they are the foundation of everything you will understand about how this child learns.

03
— Part One —

A spectrum.
Not a disorder.

Three sensory profiles. None better, none worse. All in the room — and all asking different things from the same materials.

The three profiles

Watch the nervous system
in real time.

— Profile one —

Sensory seeking.

Needs more input to feel registered. Spins the bells longer, harder. Dumps the color tablets. Hums while working. Presses hard.

— Not disruptive. Self-regulating.

— Profile two —

Sensory avoiding.

Needs less input or careful control of it. Two color tablets at a time. Recoils from textures. Covers ears at the bells. Chooses materials she can pace.

— Not difficult. Protecting her system.

— Profile three —

Sensory discrimination.

Slower to notice subtle differences. Needs the most obvious contrast first. Asks to repeat the same activity several times before the distinction lands.

— Not less intelligent. Differently paced.

05
The principle

The material is the scaffold.
Your adaptation is the respect.

The same color tablets serve the seeker who wants to make patterns and the avoider who wants two colors at a time and the child who needs the most obvious contrast first.

The sensorial concept stays intact. The nervous system gets what it needs. This is not lowering the bar — it is moving the door.

06
— Part Two —

When sensory needs
come first.

A dysregulated brain is not available for learning. The lesson waits. This is not a failure of planning — it is professional practice.

The neuroscience · in three movements

Higher executive function
goes offline when the
nervous system is in crisis.

— You see —

Bouncy. Edgy. Cannot settle.

A lesson now will bounce off. The brain is not in the room.

→

— You offer —

Heavy work. Proprioceptive input.

Pink tower carried carefully. Bucket of water. Mortar and pestle.

→

— Then —

Ten minutes. Available.

Now the lesson lands. Not because she complied — because her brain came back.

08
The opposite intervention · the shut-down child

Make the environment
smaller. Quieter. Fewer choices.

A child who is overwhelmed and shutting down needs the inverse of the seeker. One material. A quiet corner. The soft fabrics, the felt balls. Something completely within her control.

You are not lowering your expectations. You are removing the sensory barrier so she can access learning at all.

10
An eight-month-in child walking past the pink tower without looking. He goes straight to the bells. Rings one. Rings two at once. His whole body is moving.
Scene · the four minutes that came first

The bells were not
a distraction. They were the lesson.

If I had interrupted with a presentation, I would have lost him. Instead, I watched. He rang the bells for four minutes. The pace slowed. His breath calmed.

He brought one bell to his ear and listened closely. He was regulating. I moved closer. Offered the sandpaper letters. He accepted immediately. His nervous system needed what the bells gave him before he was available for learning.

11
Scene · the lesson that became smaller

I cleared the lesson.
Offered two colors.

She froze when I presented twenty-four color tablets at once. Her whole body went tight. The shut-down was visible.

Two colors, side by side. She relaxed. Matched perfectly. Next day, four. Then six. By Friday, the full set — not because she was forced, but because her nervous system had learned that this material was safe.

A child sitting upright at a low table with two color tablets in front of her, just placed side by side. Her hands are still in her lap. Her shoulders have dropped.
12
— Part Three —

Whose sensation
do you honor?

The same behavior gets read differently depending on the body it lives in. Your biases are already in the room.

The equity provocation

Sensory seeking gets called
"creative" in one body —
and "disruptive" in another.

A white, wealthy child who needs movement is "kinesthetic." A Black or Latino child with the same nervous system is "hyperactive." A child from a high-poverty home arriving starved for input is "wild" instead of asking for what her brain needs.

If sensorial education is going to be equitable, you have to interrogate whose sensory needs you honor automatically and whose you dismiss. Then change it.

14
A note on the umbrella

The diagnosis is not
the information.

Autism, ADHD, dyspraxia, dyslexia, processing differences, anxiety. Some autistic children are seekers. Some avoiders. Some both at different hours of the day.

Individual variation matters more than diagnosis. The nervous system in front of you is the only nervous system you can actually serve.

15
A note on non-speaking children

Sensorial may be
more essential —
not less accessible.

Non-speaking children, children with motor differences, children who process language slowly, children on the autism spectrum — often have acute sensory processing differences.

The Sensorial materials offer them something that does not require words. Pay attention here. They are doing the same fundamental work — building neural pathways for abstraction — through a door that is open to them.

16
A way to look — starting tomorrow

Two questions to hold
before any presentation
in the sensorial area.

First — is this nervous system regulated right now? If not, what does it need from me before the lesson?

Second — which door of this material is open to this child today? Smaller? Bigger? Slower? Louder?
