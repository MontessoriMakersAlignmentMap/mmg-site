Residency · Week Thirty-Two · Primary

Most primary classrooms
teach “where we live.”
Montessori teaches
seven continents
to a three-year-old.

Not because the child “needs to know” them. Because the absorbent mind takes in vocabulary effortlessly at this age, and because giving a child the word Africa is giving her a piece of the world.

She meets the colors on the painted globe. She meets them again on the continent puzzle map. Again on the folder of animals from Africa. Again on the basket of images of food from South America. The colors are a thread she follows through the classroom.

02
The two globes · land and water, then continents

A sandpaper globe.
Then a painted globe.
Then a map.

The first globe has only two textures — sandpaper land, smooth blue water. She feels the difference with her finger. She learns the Earth is mostly water. Before any name is given, she has a body-level sense of land and sea.

The second globe is painted with the seven continents in their colors. Now the land becomes specific. The continents are introduced by name through a three-period lesson. Then she meets them again as a flat puzzle map — and the transition from sphere to plane is itself a quiet geographical insight.

03
Land and water forms · geography as vocabulary

“Island” is a word
a three-year-old
can meet in her hand — a piece
of clay surrounded
by water in a tray.

The land and water form trays are some of the most beloved materials in the primary classroom. An island. A lake. A peninsula. A gulf. A bay. Each is a small sculpted landscape, half in water, half in land. She pours. She touches. She learns the word.

Ten years later, she will read the word “peninsula” in a textbook and feel the cool clay of a tray under her fingertip. The word will never be abstract to her.

04
A three-year-old at a small table with a wooden tray in front of her. The tray holds a sculpted landform — an island of green clay surrounded by a space ready for water. She is carefully pouring from a tiny glass pitcher of blue-tinted water into the tray, watching the water find the contour around the island. Her whole face is focused. A paper name card sits above the tray: “island.”
Scene · the island tray

She is making
an island.

The pour is slow. She watches the water meet the edge of the land. She sees the land become surrounded. This is the definition of an island — and she is reading it with her hands.

Every verbal definition of “island” she will ever meet is less vivid than this moment.

05
The continent baskets · a peopled world

A basket of real things
from each continent.
Photographs of children.
Music. A small object.

Beside the map, a basket for each continent. Inside: photographs of families, images of markets and homes and landscapes, small cultural objects — a wooden carving, a piece of fabric, a postcard. The child handles them. She listens to a piece of music. The continent stops being an outline and becomes a place where people live.

Be careful here. The basket is never a costume. It is never a caricature. It is a respectful window offered to a three-year-old, chosen with the same care you would choose a window into her own life.

06
The equity provocation · whose geography

A curriculum that
starts with “our
country” teaches
the child she is
at the center.

A curriculum that begins with the globe — and only later locates her country within it — teaches the child she is one part of a very large whole. These are not equivalent framings. They produce different adults.

Be alert to the story your continent baskets tell. Every continent is someone’s home. None is exotic. None is primitive. All are present-tense.

07
Closing

Take this into the week

When you introduce a
continent to a child — are
you handing her one of
the places the world
actually is? Or are you
handing her a backdrop for
the place she already knows?
