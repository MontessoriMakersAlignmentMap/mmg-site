Residency · Week Twenty · Primary

The moveable
alphabet is not
for writing
before reading.

It is for encoding before decoding. And the difference — between those two sentences — changes everything about how you present it.

Montessori Makers Residency Week 20 · Primary
Opening · a material often misread

Most guides use it.
Many misunderstand
what they are facilitating.

If you treat it as a
head-start on writing —
you miss the point entirely.

02
Why this, why now

You are about
to watch children
make words
before they can
hold a pencil.

Some of you will see this as a shortcut — a way to get writing done before reading catches up. It is not.

The moveable alphabet separates the cognitive act of encoding from the motor act of writing. It reveals something profound about how language works. And if you understand what is happening, you can support it with precision.

03
— Part One —

Encoding and
decoding are
different
cognitive acts.

Different brain networks. Different directions of travel. Related — but not the same.

Two operations · opposite directions

The skills are related.
The skills are distinct.

— Encoding —

Thought
into print.

idea → sound → symbol

Take an idea in your head. Find the words. Break the words into sounds. Match each sound to the letter that carries it. Compose.

— Decoding —

Print
into thought.

symbol → sound → idea

Take a symbol on a page. Figure out the sound it represents. Blend sounds into a word. Find the idea the word is carrying. Comprehend.

05
What traditional literacy asks the four-year-old brain to do

When you ask her to hold a
pencil and segment a word —
you are asking for both at once.

— Taught together —

Fine motor and
encoding, at the
same moment.

Her hand is learning to hold the pencil. Her fingers are fighting for coordination. Her attention is on grip. Her brain's resources are going to motor planning — not to sound-symbol correspondence.

— The cognitive work gets crowded out. —
→
— The moveable alphabet —

The hand is
freed. The mind
is free to think.

No grip. No tip. No pressure. She lifts a letter, listens to the sound, arranges. Her brain is free to focus on the sound-symbol relationship — which is the actual work.

— Encoding becomes available. Early. —
06
— Part Two —

The first
encounter.

The child sits with you. She has phonological awareness. You bring the box. You say one word, slowly. Here is what happens next.

The first encounter · a presentation you will give hundreds of times

One word. Said slowly.
Then the child does the rest.

01

You say it.
Together.
Slowly.

"Dddogggg." She says it with you. The sound stretches. The word becomes hearable as pieces.

— Stretch. Don't spell. —
02

You ask
what she
hears first.

Not what it starts with. What she hears. "The 'd' sound." Her ear is doing the work — not her memory.

— Ear, then eye. —
03

She
finds the
letter.

She looks across the box. Her fingers hover. She picks up the d. Sound-to-symbol — made by her own hand.

— Correspondence made visible. —
04

She
arranges.
Reads.

Three letters in a row. She looks at them. Something shifts in her understanding of language.

— "I made a word." —
08
The moment

The first time this happens

The child often looks
stunned.

She has just taken an idea from inside her mind, broken it into sounds, found letters, and made it visible. She has done segmenting, phoneme-grapheme matching, ordering, and composition — all in one quiet act.

This is not a shortcut to reading. This is a foundation for understanding how language actually works.

09
— Part Three —

When the mind
is ready and
the hand
is not yet.

Fine motor development and cognitive development do not always arrive together. The moveable alphabet lets the mind run ahead while the hand catches up.

A girl, four years and eight months old, sitting cross-legged beside a small green mat. On the mat, three wooden letters in a row: s — u — n. Her hand is still resting on the last one. She is not looking at the guide. She is looking at the word she just made.
Scene · late January · Rosa · 4y 8m

Her mind is ready.
Her hand is months
away from a pencil.

Six weeks with sandpaper letters. Last week you brought the moveable alphabet. "Suuuunnnn." She found the s, the u, the n. Arranged them. Came back the next day and built sun again. Then asked to build sit.

Paper and pencil hold no interest for her yet. That is fine. Her phonological awareness is strong. The moveable alphabet gives her encoding while her body continues to develop.

11
Scene · mid-January · Marcus · 5y

He has not written
a single letter with a
pencil — and he
is already
composing.

Four months with sandpaper letters. Strong phonological awareness. Encoding intentionally since November. Building words, taking them apart, building new ones.

He is beginning to ask about words he sees around the room — "is that how you spell door?" When his hand is stronger in March and pencil writing becomes available, it will be almost mechanical. The cognitive work is already done.

A boy around five standing at a low table, having just laid out a long row of moveable-alphabet letters across a green mat. His expression is not surprise — it is the calm satisfaction of a child who has been doing this for weeks and is now looking around the room for his next word.
12
The equity provocation · whose mind we insist on locking to whose hand

Fine motor development is
not evenly distributed
when children arrive at school.

Children with sensory integration differences may develop fine motor differently. Children whose experience has been limited in threading, in scissors, in tools — develop it later. When we insist that children must write with a pencil before they can be considered ready to express themselves in written language, we exclude from expression children whose bodies are still developing.

The moveable alphabet says something different. Your mind is ready. Let us encode. Let us compose. Let your hand catch up. That is an enormous gift.

13
A neurodivergence note · this is not remediation. This is justice.

Dysgraphia.
Motor planning
difficulties.
Fine motor
delays.

Children who cannot yet access written composition through pencil can access it through the moveable alphabet — long before their hands are ready.

They get to do the cognitive work of encoding without waiting for motor development. This is not remediation. This is removing a barrier that was never about their minds.

14
A way to look — starting tomorrow

Three things to watch for
at the moveable alphabet
this week.

First —

does the child stretch the word before she reaches for a letter? That tells you her ear is leading.

Second —

when she misses a sound — the middle vowel, a final consonant — does she hear the gap once it is laid out, or does she not yet?

Third —

after one word, does she reach for a new word on her own? That is the child telling you the door has just opened.
