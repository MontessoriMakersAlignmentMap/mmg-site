Residency · Week One

Who was Maria Montessori,
and why does her story
matter for equity today?

Montessori Makers Residency Week 01 · Primary
Opening

A scientist, a doctor,
a radical observer.

Maria Montessori is often remembered as a gentle saint who loved children. She was that, and so much more. She was also a scientist who fought for the children of Rome who had been overlooked — because she believed their minds were as capable as any wealthy child's.

Her story matters for equity not because she got everything right, but because she started with a radical question: what if the children the world has overlooked are actually showing us how human beings learn?

02
Why this, why now

The question
you are carrying
into the year.

You are starting this residency after years of watching what traditional education has done to children in underserved communities. Some of you are in public schools. Some in independent schools trying to expand access.

All of you are probably carrying the same question: can Montessori education, as it exists today, be a tool for justice — or is it just a privileged echo chamber?

That question lives in Montessori's origins.

03
— PART ONE —

The making
of a scientist.

Before the method. Before the classroom. Before the name on the door.

Origins
1870
Chiaravalle · Italy
PHOTO Young Maria Montessori · portrait, ~1900, B&W.

Middle-class family, in a moment of enormous upheaval.

Italy had just unified. The industrial revolution was reshaping cities. Poverty in Rome was visible, immediate, and brutal.

Her family believed in education as a pathway to dignity, and gave her an education unusual for girls at that time. She wanted to be an engineer.

She applied to medical school and was rejected — multiple times — because she was a woman.

05
A scientist first
1896

One of the first women to graduate from medical school in Italy.

She was trained as a scientist first. Trained to observe, to measure, to form hypotheses, to test them, to revise them. She was not trained to teach. She did not come in through a path of loving children.

She came in through a different door: how does the human mind actually work?

06
The hospital ward
1897
Rome · Hospital psychiatry

Working with children the medical establishment had written off — called, in the clinical language of the era, "idiots" and "imbeciles."

She watched what was actually happening.

These children were not broken.
They were hungry for the world.
They were just being given nothing to eat.

She designed small objects they could touch and manipulate. Children labeled ineducable learned to read and write. Some scored as well on achievement tests as children in regular schools. The establishment was shocked. Montessori was not.

07
The founding room
1906
San Lorenzo · Rome

Casa dei Bambini — a school for the children of parents who worked.

Built for the poorest children she could find.

San Lorenzo was not a place the wealthy sent their children. The walls were broken. There was no money for materials.

And in that space — with children who had nothing — Montessori developed the method we study today. All of it.

08

The radical question

The problem is not the child.

The problem is what we have given
the child to work with.

Change the environment. Change the materials. Change the expectations. The child will show you what is actually possible.

She believed this literally — for all children. That intelligence wanted to activate itself, wanted to grow, wanted to contribute. This was her great gift: she centered the child's own drive to learn.

09
Complications

A human being,
shaped by her era.

To honor her legacy honestly, we also hold its complexity.

01

Her political moment.

She lived in fascist Italy and, early on, engaged with Mussolini's government because it promised education reform — though she later broke with the regime and rejected its racial theories.

02

The science of her era.

Her belief in human perfectibility through education carried assumptions from the science of her time — including ideas adjacent to eugenics that we now recognize and move beyond.

03

How the method traveled.

As Montessori spread, it was often adopted by wealthy families — sometimes held up as a marker of privilege rather than a tool for liberation.

10
— PART TWO —

Two rooms.

Walk into San Lorenzo, 1907.
Then into a wealthy New York apartment, two decades later.

Casa dei Bambini, ~1910 — historical B&W photograph of children at work
Scene one
1907
San Lorenzo · Rome

Bare walls. Small furniture made for children's bodies. Thirty children — many malnourished, all poor.

Children polishing shoes. Sorting buttons by size and color. Tracing letters in sand. No chaos. No noise.

Not compliance — children finally given something real to do, treated as intelligent human beings rather than empty vessels.

12
Scene two
1927
A wealthy New York apartment

A Montessori "classroom" set up in the living room. The materials are beautiful. Everything is expensive.

But the child is bored. Too many choices. No experience with real work. Freedom without structure.

The child will eventually reject the method. What actually happened is the method was transported into a context where it no longer made sense.

An ornate 1920s interior — pristine, expensive, uninhabited by real work
13
The equity provocation

A method built for liberation — has it reached the children it was built for?

In many places today, Montessori is practiced beautifully — and in many others, the name travels further than the practice. Families pay for something that looks like Montessori, but misses the depth and structure she designed.

This isn't about blame. It's what happens when a method designed for liberation grows up inside systems of privilege. Our work is to return it to its roots.

14
A question to carry

Sit with this

If Montessori is a tool for liberation —
whose children is it actually liberating?

Many of the children who could most flourish in this environment do not yet have access to it.

That gap is the invitation — and the work ahead of us.

15
A note before we close

Keep them
at the center.

The children Montessori worked with in 1897 were neurodivergent — children with what we would now call autism, intellectual disabilities, learning disabilities. She built the method, in part, because traditional education was failing them.

Keep neurodivergent children, children with trauma, children still learning English, and children from systematically excluded communities at the center of this conversation — not at the margins.
