02
Why this, why now

Your entire
language program
depends on this.

Many of you will present the sandpaper letters beautifully. Some of you will present them as if you are teaching letter recognition — or letter formation.

You are not. You are teaching that words are made of sounds. That sounds can be heard, isolated, moved, ordered. Once you understand that, your observation becomes precise. You will see immediately which children have the awareness — and which do not.

03
— Part One —

What PA
actually is.

The conscious understanding that language is made of sounds — not letters, not meaning, sounds — and that those sounds can be heard and played with.

What a child with PA can do · five listening capacities

Listen for these
in the children in front of you.

01

She hears that cat and cap start with the same sound.

02

She can clap the syllables in her own name without being taught the word.

03

She can come up with a word that rhymes with bat.

04

She can delete the beginning sound from cat — and hear what is left.

05

She can blend sounds: if you say c · a · t, she hears cat.

—

This is the work you are actually watching for.

05
Three distinctions · where one ends and the next begins

It stands alone.
It is foundational to everything else.

— First —

Phonological
awareness.

Hearing that words are made of sounds. Playing with those sounds. The ear alone.

— No print required. —
— Then —

Phonics.

The connection between those sounds and the letters that represent them. Sound maps to symbol. Symbol maps to sound.

— The bridge to print. —
— Then —

Reading.

Decoding words on a page and understanding them. Meaning-making at speed.

— What reading is for. —
06
The research is unambiguous

Children who have it
in kindergarten
typically become
successful readers.

Children who cannot yet hear and manipulate the sounds in words — typically struggle. This is not about intelligence. It is not about language richness in every dimension. A child can be articulate and expressive and still lack phonological awareness.

And — most important — it responds to teaching. It responds to play. It responds to attention.

Five materials you already use — read now as phonological-awareness work, not as isolated activities.

Five tools you already have · read as PA instruction

The work is not called
phonological-awareness building.
But it is.

01

Oral language.

Songs, chants, finger plays, rhyming games, noticing sounds in the environment.

— Training ears. —
02

The bells.

Discriminating pitches very close to one another. The ear learns to hear subtle difference.

— Auditory discrimination. —
03

Golden beads.

"One, one, one…" Rhythm. Unit. Sequence. Preparing the ear for syllable work.

— Rhythmic units. —
04

Sandpaper letters.

Not the letter name. The sound. "Sss," not "ess." Visual meets auditory in the fingers.

— Phoneme-grapheme. —
05

Moveable alphabet.

She hears "cat," isolates the first sound, finds the letter. This is phonemic awareness made visible.

— Encoding. —
09
— Part Three —

When a child
has gaps.

You will see this by January if you are watching carefully — perfect letter tracing, perfect repetition, and still no phonological awareness underneath.

When you notice the gap · what actually to do
— A sequence, not a worksheet —

You do not move forward
with more letters.
You stop — and play.

01

Work with rhyming — mat, cat, hat. Exaggerate the sound. Have her move when she hears a rhyme.

02

Clap syllables in names, in foods, in anything she loves. Make rhythm audible.

03

Play sound-isolation games — "What do you hear at the beginning of sun?"

04

Read aloud books with sound patterns. Do fingerplays. Make sound sequences audible and manipulable — not on paper. In the day.

Scene · mid-January · Samuel, new in the room

His repetition
is perfect.
His phonological
awareness of English
is not there yet.

He speaks a home language that shares only some phonemes with English. His brain is still sorting two sound systems. He is not being difficult.

You do not give him more letters. You give him four weeks of rhyme, exaggerated sound, movement to sound. He begins to catch rhymes. The awareness is forming. Now the letters can return.

12
Scene · early in the year · Maria

She arrived
already able
to hear the sounds.

An English-speaking home full of books and language play. She saw the sandpaper letters and immediately began asking: Can you make that sound? What words start like that?

She is asking for the moveable alphabet before you have offered it. She is encoding within days — because the phonological awareness was already there. She needed the room. She did not need you to build the foundation.

13
The equity provocation

PA is not evenly
distributed when children
arrive at our doors.

Children who have been read to, who have heard nursery rhymes, who have played language games, who speak one language consistently — often arrive with solid PA. Children learning English as an additional language, children whose home languages do not map onto English phonemes, children whose experience prioritized communication over language play — often need more explicit, more intentional work.

This is not a deficit. This is difference. And when we move quickly to letters and assume all children arrive with PA, we advantage the children who already had it — and leave behind the children who were ready to build it with us.

14
A neurodivergence note · auditory processing differences

Not every
brain builds
PA through
listening alone.

Children with auditory processing differences may struggle when the work depends purely on the ear. They may need visual supports, multisensory approaches, more repetition.

This is information that helps you adapt — not a limitation that says they cannot learn.
