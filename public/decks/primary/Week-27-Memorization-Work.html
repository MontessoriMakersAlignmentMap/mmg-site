Residency · Week Twenty-Seven · Primary

Times-table memorization
is the last step of
the math sequence.
Not the first.

Most mathematics curricula begin with fact fluency and hope understanding arrives later. Montessori reverses the order. The child memorizes what she has already understood — not what she is supposed to take on faith.

Opening · the order has been reversed

The child does not
memorize in order to
understand.

She memorizes because
she already has.

02
The sequence · from quantity to automaticity

Three phases the child passes through before
the memorization charts ever come out.

— Phase 1 —

Concept with the beads.

She has constructed the operation with quantity. She knows what 3 × 4 means because she has gathered three groups of four beads, many times.

→
— Phase 2 —

Practice with the stamp game.

She has performed the same operation with symbolic quantity. She has seen 3 × 4 result in the same answer, in the same way, tens of times.

→
— Phase 3 —

Memorization with the charts.

Now — and only now — she meets the multiplication chart. She does not memorize strangers. She memorizes old friends.

03
The charts · what they add, what they do not add

The charts do not
teach the child
anything new.

They give her a place to consolidate what she already knows. They shift the calculations from the foreground of her attention to the background, so that when she meets a problem, her mind is free to attend to the meaning, not the computation.

Memorization is a reward for understanding — not a substitute for it.

04
A seven-year-old on a rug with a long paper chart of the multiplication tables in front of her. Small wooden tiles with answers printed on them are stacked beside the chart. A few tiles have already been placed on the chart — 2×2, 3×3, 4×4 — the diagonal she knows best. Her hand is on the tile for 6×4, pausing, not quite placing it.
Scene · the diagonal first

She does not
memorize the facts
in rows.

She builds the chart. She starts with the diagonal — the squares, the ones she knows first. Then fills outward. The chart reveals patterns nobody had to point out to her: that the table is symmetric; that certain products repeat.

Children who build their own times table carry it differently from children who were handed one. It is theirs.

05
The cost of reversing the order

A child made to memorize
before she understood
loses both.

She loses the understanding, because she was given no time to build it. She often loses the memorization too, because the facts are not anchored to anything. Ask an adult who “was never good at math” what 7 × 8 is. Listen for the hesitation. It is from second grade.

Montessori’s sequence is not slower. It is the one that doesn’t require re-teaching later.

06
The equity provocation

Timed drills are a
sorting mechanism,
not a teaching
mechanism.

Timed math drills reward children with fast processing speed, strong auditory memory, and supportive home practice. They punish children with processing differences, anxiety histories, or no one at home to quiz them. They do not teach math. They sort children into those who can perform math under pressure and those who cannot.

The Montessori memorization work has no stopwatch. The work is done. Or it is not yet. That is all.

07
A neurodivergence note · memory-retrieval differences

Slow
retrieval
is not
failed
memorization.

Some children’s math facts are in them — but not at the speed expected in a mainstream classroom. They can derive. They can reconstruct. They simply will not win a timed flashcard race.

A child who can derive 7 × 8 from 7 × 7 + 7 in three seconds knows mathematics more deeply than a child who recalls 56 in one. Do not flatten the first child’s knowing to match the second’s speed.

08
Closing

Take this into the week

When you watch a child
memorize a math fact, are
you watching her cement
something she built,
or cover over something
she never understood?
