Montessori Makers Residency Week 14 · Primary
Opening

Stop using the three
period lesson to
move children forward.
Start using it to
understand them.

It is a diagnostic. It tells you — if you are listening — everything about a child's readiness, processing speed, and relationship with language.

02
Why this, why now

Weeks of
Sensorial later.
Now language
arrives.

Some children explode into naming right away. Some touch a material once and walk away. Some work with the same object for weeks without asking you its name.

The three period lesson is supposed to scaffold naming. What it actually does is reveal everything — readiness, pace, what the child already knows without being told.

03
— Part One —

Three periods.
One assessment.

Naming. Recognition. Expression. Only one of these tells you whether learning actually happened.

The three periods · a structure

Period two is the assessment.
Period one and three are around it.

I
— Period one —

Naming.

— Input —

"This is red. This is red. This is red."

Not a question. Not a conversation. A sensory presentation of information paired with a word. The child receives and stores.

II
— Period two —

Recognition.

— Assessment —

"Show me red. Can you find red?"

It tells you whether the learning in period one actually happened. The assessment. Everything hangs here.

— Where the truth lives —
III
— Period three —

Expression.

— Output —

"What is this?"

The child names the object without prompt. Confirmation that receiving, storing, and retrieving have all occurred.

05
The pivot
II.

If a child cannot do
period two — they are
not ready for period three.

Not because they are slow. Not because they need another presentation. They have simply not stored the name yet. The neural pathway has not been built.

Most guides do period one, watch the child echo the name, and jump to period three. But echoing is not learning. Period two is where you find out.

06
— Part Two —

What period two
reveals.

"Show me red" is the richest assessment moment in early childhood education — and most guides barely notice it.

Four children, four readings

Watch where the eyes go
when you ask the question.

— Eyes on the objects —

She chooses confidently.

Integrated the sensory information. Can recognize reliably. Trusts her own perception.

Period three is safe — and may already be there.

— Eyes on your face —

She checks you for rightness.

Learned that adults are the source of truth. Not trusting her own senses. Important information.

Work in the area with no presentation. Let her notice alone.

— Hand touches many —

She is still processing.

Careful, not slow. Checking. If she lands correctly — she is learning the right way. If she never lands, she is overwhelmed.

Stay. Do not rush the signal.

— Chooses, then doubts —

She distrusts her own knowing.

Discrimination too fine, or a history of shamed mistakes. She needs her perception to be the only validation.

Repeat period two. Say nothing. Do not confirm.

08
A six-year-old at the color tablets with Joanna beside him. He points to red. Then touches blue, lifts his hand, touches blue again before deciding.
Scene · what Joanna did not do

She did not rush
to period three.

Lucas succeeded in period two. He was careful. He checked blue twice. He was processing — storing. Joanna said "good showing," nothing about right or wrong, and left the tablets on the mat.

Two days later, Lucas picked up a red tablet on his own. He looked at it. He said, "Red." She did not respond. She observed. Period three arrived when he chose to offer it — not when she forced it.

09
— Part Three —

Four ways guides
ruin period three.

A moment that should be a quiet triumph — turned into a test, a drill, a correction, or skipped altogether.

Four mistakes · and what to do instead

Stop doing all four of these.

01

The premature
jump to three.

Period two was not solid. You did period three anyway. You did not advance the child — you forced her to guess.

02

Over-extension
after success.

She names red. You ask again. And again. You have already gotten the information. One time is enough. More teaches performance for the adult.

03

Judgment creeping
into the question.

She is wrong or slow or hesitant — you correct, coach, repeat. Period three is not a teaching moment anymore. It is a testing moment. You observe. You store. You change your next step.

04

Skipping period three
when it matters most.

Because you are nervous she will fail. But period three is the assessment — if you never ask, you never know. Do period three. Let the answer be information.

11
— Part Four —

Children who do not
speak the word.

The three period lesson does not disappear. It changes. Period three is still there — but expression takes many forms.

Re-cast · for the child whose language is elsewhere

The structure holds.
Expression does not
require speech.

— Period one · still —

Naming.

You speak the name. But you also pair — a written word, a sign, a picture. You provide sensory and linguistic input together.

— Watch to see if he is receiving.

— Period two · same —

Recognition.

"Show me." He points. Hands you the object. Looks at it. Nods. The assessment happens. You learn whether he has stored the name.

— Exactly the same as for any other child.

— Period three · adapts —

Expression.

You show. You wait. A gesture. A sound. A behavior three days later — he brings all the red objects to you. That is expression.

— Your job is to recognize it, whatever form it takes.

13
The equity provocation

Period three assesses
sensory discrimination —
not pronunciation.

A child says "dat" instead of "that." A child answers in her home language. A child's accent or dialect sits outside yours. If you hear these as evidence the child has not learned — you are not assessing color. You are policing language.

Separate sensory learning from language learning, completely. The child knows the color. That is what period three asks. Everything else is your bias.

14
A note on processing differences

Period two tells
you how her
brain works.

Some children are fast processors. Some careful. Some auditory, some visual, some kinesthetic, some relational. Period two reveals all of this — if you are watching.

Children with receptive-expressive language gaps will often succeed in one and two, and struggle in three. Not because they do not know. Because retrieval is harder than reception. Let the assessment be accurate. Do not mistake a language difference for a learning one.

15
A way to look — starting tomorrow

Three discipline moves
inside the three
periods.

First — do not skip period two. Ever. It is the only period that tells you whether learning has happened.

Second — watch where her eyes go. The objects, your face, all the choices at once. That is the data.

Third — when period three arrives — ask once. Observe. Do not correct. Store what you learned. Change your next step.
