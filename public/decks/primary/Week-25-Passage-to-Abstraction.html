Residency · Week Twenty-Five · Primary

The beads do not
disappear.
They move from
her hand into her mind.

The passage to abstraction is the most misunderstood moment in a Montessori mathematics sequence. It is not the day the material is taken away. It is the day the child no longer needs it — and she, not you, is the one who tells you so.

Montessori Makers Residency Week 25 · Primary
Opening · a misconception to retire

New teachers often
worry: “when do I
take the beads
away?”

You do not take them
away. The child
leaves them.

02
Why this feels difficult · the pressure you are under

Every external
pressure asks
you to rush
abstraction.

Standardized tests ask for the symbol. Parents want to see the worksheet. Admissions want grade-level equivalence. The child down the hall, or at another school, is already “writing the equations.”

Every one of those pressures asks you to accelerate past the phase the child is actually in. And every acceleration pays a price, five years from now, that no one will trace back to this week.

Your job is to protect the child from the adult’s panic.

03
— Part One —

Hand. Paper.
Mind.

The passage is not a single step. It is an arc, and the child moves through it in her own time. She does not skip stages. She does not need to be hurried.

The arc · from material reality to mental image

The material never leaves her.
It changes address.

— Stage one —

Material only.

She solves every problem with the beads in front of her. She counts, gathers, exchanges, separates. Paper is not yet involved.

This stage is long. Weeks or months. Do not shorten it.

— quantity lives in her hand —
→
— Stage two —

Material with paper.

She does the operation with beads. Then she records what she did on paper. The symbols are a log of what the beads showed her.

Eventually she begins to write before she moves — predicting the result before verifying it.

— notation becomes a friend —
→
— Stage three —

Paper only.

She solves on paper. The beads are on the shelf. She does not look at them. If she gets stuck, she goes and gets them. No one stops her.

She returns to paper when she is ready. The material remains available — for the rest of her schooling.

— the beads live behind her eyes —
05
How to read the child · what to watch for

The signs of readiness are behavioral.
You do not administer them.

— Not yet —

Signals she still needs the material.

  • She sets up the beads and uses every single one — counting carefully, checking her count.
  • She asks for the material when given a problem on paper.
  • Her errors become frequent when the beads are removed.
  • She returns to basic setups — ten-bars, squares — as if to re-anchor herself.
  • She is visibly more relaxed with the beads in her hand.
— Ready —

Signals she has internalized the structure.

  • She sets up the beads and then barely uses them — glancing, skipping over large portions.
  • She begins writing the answer before the setup is complete.
  • She shows boredom with the physical work — wants something harder.
  • She can describe the operation in words without touching anything.
  • She answers small problems in her head and reaches for the beads only when the problem is big.
06
— Part Two —

Two failure
modes. Both
come from
the adult.

Rushing abstraction, or clinging to the material long past its usefulness. Both interrupt the child’s own timeline. Only one is visible to outsiders.

Failure mode one · removed too soon

She is solving it
on paper. It looks fine.
It is not fine.

A child whose material has been taken away before her understanding was grounded will still perform the operation. She will memorize the procedure. She will produce correct answers on familiar problems.

Then, a year later, she will meet a problem that is slightly different. She will freeze. The procedure does not cover this case. She has no underlying structure to fall back on. She concludes she is not good at math.

This is a loss that shows up in fourth grade, not in first. The teacher who rushed her will not see it. You will.

08
Failure mode two · kept too long

A child who wants
abstraction and is
held in the material
will simply stop.

This failure is less famous but just as real. A child who has internalized the concept and is being made to keep laying out beads she does not need begins to experience mathematics as childish, tedious, pointless.

She is not being served by the material anymore. She is being slowed by it. Her interest migrates. She avoids math work. When she meets harder problems, the flame has already gone out.

A guide who only knows the first failure mode will produce children who suffer the second.

09
A seven-year-old kneeling at a green mat with her paper and pencil beside her. The golden-bead tray is set up — a hundred-square, two ten-bars, and a small pile of loose units arranged neatly. Her pencil is already on the paper writing numerals. Her eyes do not look at the beads. Her left hand rests flat on the mat beside them, unused.
Scene · early March · a child leaving the material

She has set the
beads out. And is not
looking at them.

She is not ready to put them away — she would not say that if asked. But she is no longer using them. Her hand is writing. The beads are a comfort, a witness. A tether, in case she needs to come back.

You do not comment on this. You do not praise it. You do not suggest she put them away. She will stop setting them up when she is ready. That day will come. It is not today.

10
One thing the child must never hear

“You are too old
for those.”

No child, at any age in the Montessori sequence, is ever too old for the material she is working with. A nine-year-old who encounters a hard fraction problem and walks to the shelf to retrieve the fraction circles is not regressing.

She is doing something a research mathematician does: returning to the concrete form of a concept to verify her thinking. She is showing you the habit you built in her.

Protect this habit. It is one of the most valuable things she will carry out of your classroom.

11
— Part Three —

Individual
timing. Never
a cohort.

Two children of the same age, doing the same operation, may be two years apart in their passage to abstraction. Neither is ahead. Neither is behind.

The equity provocation · the child who looks “behind”

The child who is
still using the
material is often
exactly where she
needs to be.

In conventional classrooms, the child still needing concrete support at age eight is flagged, interventioned, pulled out, labeled. In a Montessori environment, she is allowed to finish.

The child whose understanding is built slowly and fully becomes a stronger mathematician than the child whose abstraction was forced. She just took longer to look finished.

13
A neurodivergence note · the length of the runway

Some children
need the
material
for years
longer.

Children with working-memory differences, dyscalculia, or processing-speed variations may still be working with concrete materials when their peers have moved on. This is not a deficit. It is a different runway.

A child given the concrete pathway as long as she needs it arrives at abstraction with real understanding. A child denied it arrives nowhere.

Your job is not to move her. It is to guarantee that the ground under her does not disappear.
