The Timeline of Life is one of the most vertigo-inducing materials in the elementary canon. The primary child does not get it yet — but she is getting ready. She is getting the sense that the present is very small, and the past is very large.

Montessori Makers Residency Week 35 · Primary
In primary · history begins with the child

The first timeline
a child meets
is her own.

A strip of paper, divided into the years of her life. On it, photographs: her as a newborn, her first birthday, her learning to walk, her first day at the classroom. She arranges them in order. She sees, for the first time, that she was someone before she remembers being someone.

This is also how birthdays are celebrated in many Montessori classrooms — the child walks around a candle (the sun) once for each year of her life, holding a globe, while her photos are passed around the circle. Time is introduced as something she moves through.

Then · larger timescales

Her life. Her parents’
lives. Her grandparents’.
Then: the children
who lived in this
place long ago.

The scale widens only as fast as the child can follow. A six-year-old can hold “when your grandmother was little” as a concept. She cannot yet hold “ten thousand years ago.” Honor that limit. Do not rush the scale.

A look ahead · what elementary will bring

The Timeline of Life · a strip of paper where all human history is the last inch.

First life4.0 bya
First animals600 mya
Land animals400 mya
Mammals200 mya
Primates65 mya
Humans300 kya

This is the Timeline of Life as elementary children meet it. Unrolled, it can be fifty feet long. The arrival of humans is the last fraction of the last strip. When a child sees this — really sees it — something changes about how big her life feels.

04
A classroom in morning light. A candle on a low yellow rug. A five-year-old boy holds a small globe in both hands, walking slowly around the candle in a wide circle. The other children sit cross-legged around the outside of the rug, watching. A basket in front of the candle holds five photographs — one for each year of his life — arranged in order. His face is serious with ceremony.
Scene · the birthday walk

He has just
gone around
the sun
five times.

For each year, a photograph is offered up. His parents have written something for each one: a sentence about what he loved to do at that age. He is meeting his own history with the same reverence a child in elementary will meet the history of the earth.

The concept underneath is the same: time is cyclical, and I am moving through it.

05
The equity provocation · whose past

Personal timelines
can wound a child
whose early
years were not
photographed.

A child in foster care. A child from a refugee family who fled without belongings. A child whose early months were spent in the NICU and not in photo albums. A timeline that requires baby pictures is a timeline that divides your classroom.

Offer alternatives. A drawing she makes of what she thinks she was like. A story her current family can tell. A single object that matters to her. The shape of the timeline is the lesson; the photographs are not.
