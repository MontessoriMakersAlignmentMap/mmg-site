Residency · Week Twenty-Four · Primary

Addition, subtraction,
multiplication, division
— are not four things. They are
four questions you can
ask of the same beads.

Once you see it, you cannot un-see it. And once the child sees it, she does not experience the operations as four separate skills to be memorized. She experiences them as variations on a single act — the handling of quantity.

Montessori Makers Residency Week 24 · Primary
Opening · a reframe

Traditional math
teaches four
operations as four
separate units.

Montessori teaches
one material — and
four ways of asking
about it.

02
Why this matters · what the child escapes

She does
not experience
mathematics as
arbitrary.

A child taught addition one year, subtraction the next, multiplication a year after that — spends most of her mathematical life re-learning the same material under different names. And wondering why it keeps changing.

A child taught operations as variations on a single sensorial experience sees the relationship immediately. Subtraction is the inverse of the thing she just did. Division is the inverse of the other thing.

She is not being told. She is discovering.

03
— Part One —

Four questions.
One material.

The same golden beads that taught her ten-ness now teach her every operation she will meet for the next six years. Her hand already knows the material. Only the question changes.

Each operation is a question · each gesture is the answer

The beads stay the same.
Her hand changes shape.

+
Addition

“What do we have altogether?”

Two children each bring a tray of beads to the rug. The quantities are combined.

— gesture: gather —

Two groups become one group.

−
Subtraction

“If I take away — what is left?”

One pile is divided. A portion is removed. What remains is counted.

— gesture: separate —

One group becomes less than itself.

×
Multiplication

“If we do this again and again — what do we have?”

Several children bring the same quantity. All are combined.

— gesture: repeat —

Addition of equal groups.

÷
Division

“If we share this equally — what does each get?”

One pile is distributed evenly among several children. Each child receives one portion.

— gesture: share —

One group made equal.

05
What the child discovers about the relationships

Addition and subtraction
are the same motion.
Reversed.

She gathers two trays and counts the result. Then she takes one of the trays back and counts what is left. She has just performed addition, then subtraction — with the same beads, the same hands, the same thought.

The same is true of multiplication and division. Multiplication gathers equal groups. Division takes that gathering apart. They are not opposites to be memorized. They are the same act, performed forward and performed backward.

A truth you cannot communicate with worksheets. Only with quantity, in her hand.

06
— Part Two —

The moment
ten ones become
a ten.

Every struggling mathematician you have ever met was, at some point, a child who never understood why you carry the one. In the Montessori classroom, that moment is not explained. It is felt.

The sensorial basis of carrying and borrowing

She is adding 8 + 5.
She now has 13 unit beads on the tray.

Thirteen loose
beads — but we
only ever keep
nine.

The guide says: “We have more than nine ones. What do we do when we have ten ones?” The child knows — because she has done it a hundred times with the decimal system. Ten ones become a ten.

She counts out ten loose beads. She exchanges them for a ten-bar. She carries the ten to the tens column. Three ones remain.

She has not carried anything abstract. She has physically moved a quantity from one place to another — because that is what the quantity required.

08
The child who felt it · the child who memorized it

Two fourth-graders facing
47 + 38. Watch the difference.

— Memorized the algorithm —

“Put the 5 down, carry the 1.”

He does it correctly. But he cannot say why. When the problem is slightly unfamiliar — three digits, a zero in the middle, subtraction that needs regrouping — he stops.

The rules have always been arbitrary. When a new one is needed, he has no way to derive it. He waits to be told.

— Felt the operation —

“I have too many ones. I have to exchange.”

She does it correctly and can explain each step. When the problem is unfamiliar, she thinks back to the beads. “What would I do with the quantity?” She figures it out.

There are no rules to forget. There is only the structure of quantity — which she already knows.

09
Three six-year-olds kneeling together around a long green mat. Each child is placing a small tray down in a row. Every tray holds the same arrangement of golden beads: two bars of ten, a square of a hundred, and four loose units. A fourth child — the one whose work it is — is at the head of the mat, about to combine all three trays.
Scene · first multiplication · primary

She does not know
she is doing three
times one hundred
and twenty-four.

She is doing something much more obvious. She is combining three identical trays. She will count the result. She will discover it is 372. Later, the guide will tell her: this is called multiplication.

By the time she meets the symbol 3 × 124, she has done this thirty times with different quantities. The symbol is a record of something she has lived.

10
Division · the most social operation

Division begins as
fair sharing.

The child has a tray with a quantity on it. Three other children come to the rug with empty plates. “Can you share this equally among these three friends?” She begins placing beads — one here, one here, one here — until the tray is empty.

She counts what is on each plate. They are all the same. That is division. It is also — and this is not incidental — a moral act.

The child who learns division through equal sharing carries forward a mathematics entangled with justice. She will not easily forget that the numbers stand for something real.

11
What she is also building, underneath the math

Operations feel logical,
not arbitrary.

When she meets a new operation — long division, fractions, algebra — she does not expect to be told the rule. She expects to figure it out. Her prior experience of mathematics is that it makes sense if you handle it carefully enough.

This disposition is the single most valuable thing a primary-age child can carry into the rest of her mathematical life. More valuable than any fact, any algorithm, any speed.

12
The equity provocation · concrete operations as the floor

Abstract algorithms
sort children by how
much math support
they get at home.

A child whose parents drill times tables at the kitchen table can survive an abstract mathematics curriculum. She memorizes. She fills in the worksheet. She looks fine. A child whose parents do not — or cannot — has nothing to survive it with. She falls behind and is labeled a non-math child.

Concrete operations eliminate the gap. The beads are in the classroom. They work for every child. No child arrives behind. No child is dependent on home support to access the concept.

13
A neurodivergence note · on dyscalculia and the concrete path

Children with
dyscalculia
often thrive
with the
beads.

Dyscalculia is not an inability to do mathematics. It is a specific difficulty with numerical symbols — with holding and manipulating them in mind.

A mathematics built on concrete quantity — beads that can be touched, moved, counted — gives these children a working pathway that abstract symbol systems close off.

The child who “cannot do math” is often the child who cannot do the notation. Give her the beads, and watch.
