Montessori Makers Residency Week 19 · Primary
Opening · what the research has overturned

For fifteen years the picture has been clear

Bilingualism is not
cognitively taxing.

It is cognitively
enhancing.

That older advice — "pick one language to give the child a fair start" — has been thoroughly debunked.

02
Why this, why now

You are
standing in a
decisive place.

Language study begins now. If you have children learning English as an additional language — or growing up with more than one at home — you are in a position to build on the neural gifts they have.

Or — subtly, without meaning to — to send the message that the home language is something to leave behind. The Montessori approach can honor multilingualism beautifully. Or it can quietly undermine it. The difference is in what you believe and what you do because of it.

03
— Part One —

The bilingual
brain.

Three findings from fifteen years of neuroscience — and the one conditional on which the gift depends.

Three findings · what the research actually shows

Two language systems —
developing simultaneously,
not sequentially.

01

Enhanced
executive
function.

Cognitive flexibility. Working memory. Inhibitory control. Bilingual children score higher on all three — the brain trains itself by constantly managing two systems and choosing.

— Practice is the mechanism. —
02

Earlier
metalinguistic
awareness.

They understand, sooner than monolingual peers, that words are arbitrary labels, that language itself is a system — that there are multiple ways to say the same thing.

— Foundational for reading. —
03

Phonology,
grammar,
vocabulary —
all parallel.

Not one language paused while the other catches up. Two complete systems, both in development, both building neural architecture at once.

— Not a limitation. A gift. —
05
The caveat · additive vs subtractive

The advantage only emerges
when both languages are
valued, used, developed.

— Additive bilingualism —

Both languages
grow.

Home language is honored at home and at school. The child develops two systems. The brain gets the practice. The gift arrives.

— The cognitive advantage is real here. —
— Subtractive bilingualism —

One language
replaces
the other.

The family moves, everyone shifts to English "to help." The home language declines. The child is becoming monolingual, slowly. The advantage disappears.

— Looks neurologically different. —
06
Where the decision gets made

Your classroom is one of the
places where the home
language is either honored
and supported —
or it isn't.

If the implicit message is that English is the language of learning, of success, of belonging — the child's brain starts to abandon the home language. The bilingual advantage disappears. And it disappeared in your room.

07
— Part Two —

What you
can actually
do.

You don't have to speak every home language of every child. You do have to make visible that every home language matters in this room.

Even without fluency · what to do tomorrow

Visible. Consistent.
Intentional.

01

Learn greetings
in the languages
of your community.

Not every language every day. Regularly. The message is: all languages are here.

02

Put books
on the shelf in
home languages.

Even two Spanish books among eight English says something. Feature them. Read them aloud.

03

Present sandpaper
letters in both
sound systems.

Where you share a language with the child, let her see the phonetic similarities and differences.

04

Invite families
to share their
language.

Ask for recordings of songs. Ask grandparents to come tell a story. Learn names in the right language.

09
The moveable alphabet · not only in English

She can encode in
Spanish. Or Mandarin.
At the moveable
alphabet.

This is not a detour from English. This is deepening her understanding of how language works. She is discovering that the phoneme-grapheme principle holds in her home language. The sounds break down. The system is similar and different.

She is doing this in the language where her phonological awareness is strongest — because her exposure there is deepest. English follows. It does not have to come first, and the first language does not stop when English arrives.

10
The subtle undermining · things you may not realize you are doing
— What the child's brain registers —

You can care about equity
and still send the message
"your language does not belong here."

She speaks in her home language.
You respond with a confused look, or "use English, please."
Her brain registers that home language is a problem in this room. The work of abandoning it begins.
You celebrate writing —
but only the English writing goes on the wall.
What is visible is what matters. Her home-language composition is not visible. Therefore it is not school.
Spanish books are on the shelf —
but never read aloud.
Decoration is not honoring. Children read adults' attention, not shelves. If you do not touch it, it does not count.
You talk to families about reading —
and only mention English books.
The message to the family: the real literacy is English literacy. Home-language reading is a hobby.

Scene · Jing · a family's well-meant shift

The parents chose
English at home
to help her.

She understands Mandarin. She no longer speaks it. Her phonological awareness in English is emerging. Her Mandarin — the language of grandmother — is quietly receding.

You call the family. You share what the research says. They agree to speak Mandarin intentionally at home again. You learn to greet Jing and her family in Mandarin. A parent teaches the classroom a song. By spring, Jing is encoding in both.

12
Scene · Diego · beginning to recover what was slipping

He understands
Spanish. He prefers
English. He will not
pick up the Spanish
books on the shelf.

A Spanish-speaking home. Two years of English-dominant preschool. Early signs of language loss.

You begin reading Spanish books aloud, regularly. You present sandpaper letters in both languages — making the similarities and differences visible. A Spanish-speaking family member comes in to tell a story. By midyear, he checks out Spanish books. By spring, he is asking to build Spanish words with the moveable alphabet.

A boy about four standing at the classroom library, eyes moving across a bilingual display of picture books. His hand is halfway toward a Spanish title. The posture of a child remembering something he had almost let go of.
13
The equity provocation · the honest choice

Their brains are plastic
and powerful. They will
learn English. Quickly.

But if that is your only goal, the home language will diminish. And the child will lose something — access to family members who only speak the home language, cultural knowledge embedded in that language, the cognitive advantages of true bilingualism.

Or — you make the deliberate choice that real bilingualism is part of your educational mission. That you will honor it in your environment and your practice. That is an equity choice. It matters.

14
A neurodivergence note · both languages, not one

A dual-language
learner who is also
neurodivergent
needs you in both
languages.

A dyslexic child learning in two languages may need explicit phonics instruction in both. A child with a speech-sound disorder may face different challenges in each language system — because the phonologies are different.

This is not more complicated. It is more information about what each child actually needs.
