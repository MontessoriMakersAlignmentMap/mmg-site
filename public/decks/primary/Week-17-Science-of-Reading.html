02
Why this, why now

Language
begins now.
The pressure
is intense.

Parents want reading. Schools want reading. Districts want benchmarks. Montessori guides want meaning and joy.

The science of reading tells us what actually works. Your job is to understand all of it — so you can hold Montessori practice and evidence-based decoding, and hand neither one over.

03
— Part One —

Reading is
not natural.

Your brain did not evolve to read. Reading is an invention. Learning to read requires the brain to rewire itself intentionally — and that has implications for how you teach.

Two knowledge systems · where they actually live

Not contradictions.
Not automatic allies.

— The science of reading —

Explicit.
Systematic.
Multi-sensory.

Phonological and phonemic awareness, phonics, fluency, and comprehension are interdependent systems. They must all be present, all be strong, all taught with intention.

— Reading does not emerge. It is built. —
— Montessori philosophy —

Prepared
environment.
Intrinsic interest.

Indirect teaching. The absorbent mind. Materials that invite the work rather than impose it. Children coming to language because it is irresistible.

— The classroom teaches. The adult supports. —
— What the informed guide holds —

The sandpaper letters are explicit phonics instruction — and they are sensorial exploration. Both at once. You are not choosing.

— Part Three —

Where Montessori
has quietly
diverged.

The places where practice, over time, has drifted from what the science is now telling us clearly.

Where Montessori has diverged — and how to repair it
— Three assumptions the science asks us to revise —

The prepared environment
is necessary. It is not sufficient.

"The materials do the work."
The materials are powerful only when the guide makes the phoneme-grapheme connection explicit. Your observation. Your presentation. Your follow-up.
"Oral language is enough."
A child can be articulate, expressive, and still lack the explicit awareness that words are made of sounds. That awareness has to be taught.
"Writing comes before reading."
Encoding does come first — when phonemic awareness is in place. For children whose awareness is still building, encoding is not yet available.
09
Your role · from ambient to intentional

You are not standing back
letting it happen.
You are guiding.

— The common misreading —

Set the materials
out and trust
the environment.

Pass a child through sandpaper letters, trust repetition, move on if she seems fine. Assume a rich room will do the work without your attention.

— This privileges children who already had it. —
→
— What the science asks of you —

Facilitate
phonemic
awareness with
precision.

Present with intention. Draw attention to the sound at the beginning, middle, end. Watch for the exact moment encoding becomes possible.

— Montessori done with evidence-based precision. —

A four-year-old boy tracing a blue sandpaper letter with his first two fingers, mouth shaped around a sound. His movement is almost rhythmic — like a song he is teaching his own hands.
Scene · 10:30 a.m. · January · Marco

His hands and his ears
are learning
at the same time.

Three weeks with the sandpaper letters. Today he is picking them up almost rhythmically, saying the sound, setting it down.

You sit beside him and name what you see: "You heard it. That sound is in cat and in cup. Your ears are learning." You are not testing him. You are making visible the phonemic awareness he is already building.

11
Scene · later that morning · Sadie

S-A-D-Y.
She has invented a
phonetic spelling —
and it is exactly right.

She cannot hold a pencil. Her fine motor has not arrived. But her phonemic awareness has.

Her encoding is running ahead of her awareness of conventional spelling. The moveable alphabet gave her access to written language before her hand was ready. The science says this matters. Montessori has been saying it for a hundred years.

12
The equity provocation

The children who arrive
with phonemic awareness
already in place — do not
need you as much.

They came from homes rich in oral language, nursery rhymes, books. Children learning English as an additional language, children whose home language shifts with the classroom, children whose exposure has prioritized communication over language play — they need more from you, not less.

When we assume the environment is sufficient and fail to make the phoneme-grapheme connection explicit for the children who need that explicitness, we reproduce the inequality that was already there.

13
A neurodivergence note · dyslexia and structured literacy

Some children need
more explicit
than a typical
Montessori classroom
provides.

Dyslexic brains often require structured literacy instruction that is more intensive, more repetitive, and more systematic than ordinary presentations.

This is not a failure of Montessori. This is Montessori adapted to how this brain works. You recognize what she needs. You provide it — sometimes outside the normal flow of the classroom day.

14
A way to look — starting tomorrow

Three observations
to begin the language year.

First —

which children can already hear the first sound in a word? Which cannot yet?

Second —

when you present a sandpaper letter, are you only saying the sound? Or are you making the phoneme-grapheme connection visible in words the child already knows?

Third —

which children seem to be building awareness through the ordinary flow — and which are quietly signalling that they need something more deliberate from you?
