It is not about
shielding children
from failure.

Control of error dismantles the power structure where you get to decide when a child is wrong. That is an equity issue.

Opening

The material
is already telling
the child she is
wrong.
And still, you are speaking.

You are removing control of error from the material — and putting it in your mouth.

02
Why this, why now

When did
you learn best?

When someone told you you were wrong — or when you discovered it yourself? When you were corrected in the moment — or when you noticed the mistake and fixed it?

We remember the mistakes we find. We understand the reason. We fix them ourselves. We own the learning. Children are no different. They are, in fact, more capable of this than you think.

03
— Part One —

Who gets to
say wrong?

The authority to declare a child's work correct or incorrect — and where that authority has quietly been living.

The shift · where the authority lives

Two classrooms.
Same material. Different
power structure.

— Where many of us were raised —

The adult is
the judge.

You use your authority to declare rightness and wrongness. The child has no choice but to accept your judgment. She has no agency in the assessment.

— The child is the subject. —
vs.
— What Montessori asks of you —

The material
is the feedback.

She tests her hypothesis. The material responds — fits, balances, matches, or does not. She adjusts. She tests again. She is in charge of the assessment process.

— She is the scientist. You are the witness. —
05
A five-months-in child at a mat with the knobbed cylinders. She has just placed a thick cylinder into a thin hole. It does not fit. She sits. Looks at it.
Scene · no one spoke

The material
told her.

She took it out. Chose another. It fit. She smiled to herself. No one told her she was wrong. No one told her she was right. She knew.

When she finished, she carried the box back to the shelf. She did not look at me. She did not need confirmation. She had learned to trust the materials — and herself.

06
Scene · the adult would not stop talking

"Good job."
"You're doing it."
"Ooh — try that again."

Same material. Same child's age. A different guide, a few feet closer. Talking throughout.

The child learned to work for the adult's approval. She did not develop independence. She did not learn to trust herself. She learned that whether she had succeeded depended on what the adult thought.

A guide kneeling beside a child at the knobbed cylinders, leaning in, mouth open mid-sentence. The child has paused and is looking up at the guide's face, not the material.
07
— Part Two —

When the material
will not do it
for you.

Pink tower, cylinders, color tablets — built-in. Sandpaper letters, drawing, early writing — not. This is where most guides fail the equity work. This is where you correct.

Extension · where control of error can live

You do not correct.
You relocate the
authority.

— What you were doing —
— Where the authority moves —
Sandpaper letters.
"That's not quite how you trace it."
The sensory feedback — smooth or rough, recognizable or not. Her finger tells her.
Free drawing.
"What a great dog!" (It was a horse.)
Her own intention — "What did I want to draw? Does it look like what I imagined?" Her mind is the test.
Early writing.
"That letter is backward."
"I notice it looks different when you trace it that way. I wonder what would happen if you tried it this other way."
09
— Part Three —

Your language
is the correction.

You will not realize how much evaluation is in your speech until you try to take it out. Start listening to yourself.

Four phrases · all of them evaluation

All four remove agency
from the child. All four
position you as the judge.

"Good job."

"That's right."

"Almost."

"Not quite."

— The child already knows whether the tower stayed balanced. She can see it. You do not have to confirm it.

11
Say what you see · reflective, not evaluative

Describe.
Do not evaluate.

— Stop saying —
— Say instead —

"Good job."

→

"You traced that letter three times."

"That's right."

→

"You matched eight colors."

"Did you do it correctly?"

→

"Are you done? Does it look the way you wanted it to?"

You are returning the assessment to the child. Slowly, she stops looking at you for confirmation. She looks at her own work.

12
— Part Four —

For the child
who has been hit
for mistakes.

This is where control of error stops being pedagogy and becomes the most immediate equity work you will do.

For children whose mistakes have been punished

Even gentle correction
still means: the adult is
the one who decides.

A child arrives having been hit for mistakes. Yelled at. Shamed. If you are still correcting — even kindly — you are reminding her that adults are still the judges.

When the material is the feedback, something different happens. She learns that mistakes bring information, not punishment. That she can try and fail and try again without fear. Her relationship with learning begins to heal.

14
A note on standards

This is not lowering
the bar.

The child still has to make the cylinders fit. Still has to match the colors. Still has to form the letter correctly. The standards are exactly the same.

What moves is the authority. She meets the standard because she sees the feedback — not because you told her to. And that is the equity move.

15
The equity provocation

Your default teaching style
was designed for a child like you.
Not for the children in your
classroom.

If you were raised in a home where mistakes were safe, your nervous system is calibrated to safety around failure. When you correct a child for a mistake, you are using your privilege to impose your authority on a nervous system that has not been protected the way yours was.

The equity move is to notice this, and to change it.

16
A way to look — starting tomorrow

Three moves
for your mouth.

First — when a child makes an error with a material that has built-in control of error: say nothing. Let her find it.

Second — when she looks at you for rightness: ask instead. "Are you done? Does it look the way you wanted it to?"

Third — catch yourself before "good job." Replace with what you actually saw. "You stacked the tower and it stayed balanced."

17
Closing

Take this into the week

What would change in your
classroom if you stopped
correcting children's mistakes —
and started trusting them to
notice and fix their own?
