The Montessori classroom
is inhabited.
The plants are not

Botany in primary is not a science unit. It is a daily relationship with living things. The child waters, dusts, names, and draws — and in doing so learns that the world outside her window has the same kind of claim on her attention as she does on it.

Montessori Makers Residency Week 33 · Primary
Opening · botany as practice, not subject

Children are not
taught about plants.

They are given
responsibility for them.

02
Care is the curriculum

A three-year-old
with a small watering
can and a cloth is
doing botany.

She fills the watering can at the sink. She carries it across the room. She waters the plant. She takes a soft cloth and wipes the dust off the leaves, one leaf at a time. She returns the can. This is a forty-minute lesson in plant biology that is not named one.

She learns what a plant needs. She learns what it looks like when it does not get what it needs. She learns that living things change visibly under her care. These are the foundational insights of biology — held in a cloth.

03
The leaf cabinet · naming what grows

The shapes of leaves.
A vocabulary the child collects from her own walk home.

ovate
lanceolate
cordate
lobed
linear
palmate

The leaf cabinet is a drawer of wooden leaf shapes. Each leaf has its name. The child traces its edge, hears its name, carries it to a matching photograph — and then, crucially, begins to see those shapes on her walk home.

04
A four-year-old at a classroom windowsill with a small tin watering can. A spider plant is on the sill, its fronds reaching out. Her tongue is poked between her lips with concentration. She is pouring the water slowly, in a thin stream, exactly where the soil meets the base of the plant.
Scene · the morning watering

This plant is
hers to look after.

Not hers to decorate with. Hers to attend to. She has agreed to this. Every morning, when she arrives at the classroom, her plant is part of what she checks on. She is learning what it means to be responsible for something alive.

This is the root of environmental consciousness — not a curriculum unit about climate change, but a child who already knows, from age four, that a living thing can depend on her.

05
The equity provocation · nature is not a luxury

A living classroom is
an equity commitment.
Not every child lives
near a garden.

Access to growing things — soil, leaves, seeds, roots — is not evenly distributed. A child from a high-rise without a window box arrives at your classroom with no prior experience of responsibility for a living thing. Do not treat the plant shelf as incidental. For some children it is the only garden they will know.

The cost of a few plants and a watering can is one of the highest-leverage equity investments in the primary room.

06
Closing

Take this into the week

How many living
