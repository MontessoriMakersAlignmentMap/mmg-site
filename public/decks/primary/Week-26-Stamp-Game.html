02
The material · hierarchically color-coded

Each tile bears a numeral.
The color tells her which hierarchy.

1
Unit — green
10
Ten — blue
100
Hundred — red
1000
Thousand — green

The colors are not decorative. They are the same color code she will meet in the bead frame, the bank game, large bead frame, and eventually the checkerboard. By giving the hierarchy a color, Montessori gives it a permanent identity.

A transition material is never a demotion and never a graduation. It is a precise trade — keeping what the child still needs, releasing what she has outgrown.

The trade · kept, released, introduced

The stamp game is a deliberate subtraction
from the golden beads. Nothing is arbitrary.

Hierarchy
How a ten is distinguished from a unit
By size and shape — a bar is longer than a bead.
By color only — the tile is the same size either way.
Quantity
How much each piece represents
A ten-bar actually contains ten beads. You can count them.
A “10” tile represents ten — but is not made of ten parts.
Exchange
How carrying and borrowing work
Ten ones gathered into a bar.
Ten unit tiles traded for one ten tile. Same structure. Different form.
What it builds
The concept of quantity itself.
The habit of using a symbol to stand for a known quantity.
05
A six-year-old at a small wood table by a window. Her stamp game tray is open beside a white sheet of paper on which a long addition problem has been written. Neat columns of green, blue, red, and green tiles are laid out on the table — already in process. Her hand hovers over the unit column, about to gather ten green tiles.
Scene · the first stamp-game problem

She is not doing
anything new.

She recognizes this. It is addition. It is what the beads did. The green tiles are the ones. The blue are the tens. She gathers ten greens. She exchanges them for a blue.

The operation has not changed. Only the form has. She now has a material small enough to live on a table, portable enough to travel between problems, close enough to paper that she can see the relationship.

06
Why not skip straight to paper

The stamp game is the
rehearsal for written
arithmetic.

The columns on her paper will eventually look like the columns of stamps. The carrying on her paper will eventually look like the exchange of tiles. She is practicing the layout of written arithmetic with her hands.

A child who goes straight from beads to paper often loses the column logic in the transition. A child who spends months with the stamp game never loses it. The columns are in her body before they are on her worksheet.

07
Pace · the stamp game is as long as it needs to be

Some children
stay for
months.
Some move
on in weeks.

There is no calendar for when a child leaves the stamp game. There is only the child’s own indication that she is moving through the problems faster than the tiles can keep up.

When she begins to solve problems without setting up the stamps — answering from memory, using paper only, reaching for the tiles only to check — she is already on the next bridge.

The bridges are not timed crossings. They are places to stand.

08
The equity note · a full transition, or no transition

Every transition
material Montessori
built is a gift
the conventional
classroom does not give.

The child asked to jump from manipulatives straight to a worksheet is the child most likely to be told she is bad at math. The stamp game closes that gap. It is scaffolding, engineered.

Do not skip it in the name of efficiency. It was designed by a physician who watched children fail without it.
