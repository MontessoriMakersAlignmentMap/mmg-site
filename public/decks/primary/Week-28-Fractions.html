A four-year-old handles a circle divided into thirds, names it “a third,” fits it back together, and returns it to the shelf. That is fractions. No panic. No prerequisites. A quiet introduction that saves her from fear later.

03
What happens in the child’s first year with them

She handles them as
a shape puzzle. That
is the whole lesson.

She removes the pieces. She puts them back. She notices two halves complete a circle. She notices three thirds complete a circle. She is not taught this — she discovers it, because the material will not close if she does otherwise.

When she asks “what is this one called?” she receives the names: a third, a fourth, a fifth. The words enter alongside the quantities. No numerator. No denominator. Not yet.

By age five she can say “three-fourths” while pointing at three of the four wedges. She is not doing mathematics. She is building a vocabulary for a concept she will meet formally in five years.

Scene · daily life · the apple at snack

The first real
fraction a child
meets is edible.

Long before she handles the circles, she sees an apple cut into fourths. She gets “a fourth.” She knows there are three other children and each has a fourth. Four fourths make the apple.

This is not an isolated lesson. It is the fabric of daily life in the classroom. Fractions are a language spoken here.

05
What she avoids meeting in fourth grade

She will never
encounter a fraction
as a frightening
new symbol.

The average American child meets the fraction ½ for the first time on a worksheet, at age nine or ten, written in an unfamiliar notation, with rules that appear arbitrary — add these across but those don’t cross, invert and multiply, find a common denominator.

The Montessori child meets ½ at age four — as a piece of metal she can hold in one hand. When the symbol arrives, it is the symbol for something she has known for six years.

06
The equity provocation · the fraction cliff

Fourth grade is
where American
mathematics loses
millions of
children.

Longitudinal data shows U.S. math performance falls off a cliff around grade four — and the proximate cause, again and again, is fractions introduced as alien symbols with procedural rules. Children conclude, correctly, that nobody has told them what these things are.

The Montessori child is immune to this cliff because she has been handling fractions since preschool. The remedy for a grade-four problem lives in the primary-age classroom.
