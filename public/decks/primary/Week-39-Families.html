Residency · Week Thirty-Nine · Primary

A Montessori
classroom that is
separate from the
child’s home is not
a Montessori classroom.
It is a bubble.

The child is the same person at home and at school. If the two places share no common language, no shared rhythms, no respect — she lives across a seam. The work of partnering with families is the work of closing that seam.

02
Translate the classroom · stop using jargon in letters home

Your language signals who belongs. Audit it.

Classroom language
Family-facing translation
“She had a normalized morning.”
→
She was calm and focused today. She chose her work and stayed with it for a long time.
“We presented the moveable alphabet.”
→
She used wooden letters to spell a word from the sounds she hears. Today she spelled c-a-t.
“He’s working on his sensitive period for order.”
→
He really needs things to happen the same way every day right now. This is typical at his age, and it passes.
“Three-period lesson on binomial.”
→
We introduced her to a new puzzle cube today. I’ll show it to you at pickup if you’d like.

The vocabulary of a training program is precious to those who earned it. It is exclusionary to those who did not. Use it with colleagues. Translate it for families — every single time.

03
Parent conferences · evidence, not impressions

Bring your
observation notebook.
Leave your
adjectives at home.

“He’s such a joy” is a compliment. It is not a conference. A conference is: here is what he chose on Monday, and Tuesday, and Thursday; here is how long he stayed with it; here is what he moved on to. From that, the family knows something they did not know when they walked in.

End every conference with a question, not an instruction. “What does he do on Saturday mornings at home?” The answer is always useful.

04
A classroom doorway at 3:15pm. A father is crouched down, eye-level with his five-year-old daughter. The guide is crouched beside them, showing something on a small piece of paper — the word the daughter spelled today. The father is nodding, smiling, asking the daughter a question about it. The daughter is beaming. No one is standing over anyone.
Scene · everyone at the same height

The guide is
crouched.
That is the
whole lesson.

A parent conversation that happens over a child — with her standing silent between two towering adults discussing her — teaches the child that the school-home relationship is one in which she has no voice. A conversation at her height teaches the opposite.

Bring the paper. Bring the observation. Bring yourself down to the level where she is. The partnership starts there.

05
What the family knows that you do not

You see her
six hours a day.
They see her the
other eighteen.

Nobody in the classroom knows the child as well as the adults who raised her. Nobody knows her sleep, her fears, her siblings, her joys, her griefs like they do. Any conversation in which the teacher is the expert is a conversation that has gotten the ratio wrong.

The family is the expert. You are the specialist. The relationship works when both those statements are true in the room.

06
The equity provocation · whose engagement counts

When you say a family
is “not engaged” —
what has the school offered them
that they could
actually come to?

An 11am weekday conference excludes anyone who works hourly. A muffin breakfast excludes anyone who doesn’t have morning flexibility. An English-only letter home excludes anyone whose first language isn’t English. Before you conclude a family is absent, interrogate whether the invitation met them.

Evening conferences. Translated letters. Phone calls instead of emails for families who prefer them. Interpreters arranged ahead of time. These are engagement, not accommodations.
