Montessori Makers Residency Week 21 · Primary
Opening · a tool, not a system

The question is not
whether decodable texts
have a place in a
Montessori classroom.

The question is what
we think they are for —
and for whom.

02
Why this, why now

It is mid-January.
Some children
are stuck
in a specific way.

Consolidated the pink words. Can segment, can blend. And still — cannot yet transfer phonetic knowledge into running text.

Reading instruction elsewhere in the country is having a loud conversation about decodable texts. Your residents are hearing it. They need a Montessori-native way to think about the question.

03
— Part One —

What they are.
What they
are not.

Decodable texts are organized by phonetic progression. Leveled readers are organized by everything at once. The difference matters.

Two different tools · often confused

One controls phonetic
pattern. One varies everything
at the same time.

— Leveled readers —

Difficulty rises on many dimensions at once.

Word frequency. Sentence length. Concept complexity. Picture support. A Level F is harder than a Level C in multiple ways simultaneously.

Mixes knowns and unknowns. The child's cognitive resources split between decoding, vocabulary, syntax, and meaning-making — all at once.

— Decoding is one of many loads. —
— Decodable texts —

Only the phonetic work is doing the work.

A short book built on a controlled sequence of phonetic patterns. Short a, then short a with consonant blends, then short i — the progression is phonological, not conceptual.

The story stays simple — sometimes silly — on purpose. Everything else is stable. The child's brain gets to spend itself on sound.

— Isolates known from unknown. —
05
Critical distinction · what they are not

A decodable text is
not your primary
reading method.

The Montessori environment still holds: reading for meaning is central. Children learn to read by reading. The prepared environment contains real books with real stories. None of that changes.

What decodable texts do is accelerate phonetic consolidation for children whose processing systems need more reinforcement than the pink and blue series provides implicitly. Not a substitute. A bridge.

06
— Part Two —

Two uses. Same
material.
Different purpose.

The same stack of decodable books can be a justice tool — or a gatekeeping one. The difference is in how you decide a child gets one.

The same text · two very different uses

The book is neutral.
The decision is not.

— Responsive support —
— Tracking system —
Who gets one?
The specific child whose observation tells you her nervous system needs this particular phonetic practice right now.
Every child, sorted into levels. Or: the children labeled "behind." A group decision, not a child-specific one.
Why this child?
You have watched her. The pink words are solid. The transfer to reading has a lag. You responded to evidence.
A scope and sequence said so. A benchmark said so. An outside program said so. Not the child.
What is tracked?
Her reading trajectory. When the consolidation happens, the bridge is put away. She returns to real books.
Her "level." Reported to families. Used to sort children into categories of ability — which then stick.
What children learn
Different children need different supports. That is normal and honored.
Some children are "advanced." Some are "behind." Reading ability is a fixed trait.
08
What you actually say to the child

Children
understand
supports.
They use them
everywhere.

You might say, simply

"I notice you are working really hard with these pink words. I want your brain to have a chance to practice them in a real book. These books have simple stories — but their words only use the sounds you have been practicing. When you finish these, you will be ready for the blue series like the rest."

Honest. Clear. Not pretending the decodable is the same as the blue series. Children are not confused by different tools for different jobs. They are confused when we pretend those tools are interchangeable.

09
— Part Three —

Sofia.
Late January.

A six-year-old who has consolidated half the pink series. She can name sounds. She cannot yet read the word cat on the page without going back to the sequence.

A six-year-old girl seated at a small table with a short, thin paperback open in front of her. The book has only a few words per page. Her finger is tracing under "The cat sat on the mat." Her mouth is shaping the sounds slowly — not reading aloud for the guide, reading aloud to herself.
Scene · late January · Sofia · six years

After four weeks
of a single
decodable book,
something shifts.

The repetition. The predictability. The fact that her brain does not have to hold many variables at once. Her system has room to consolidate.

Then she picks up a blue book. Not perfectly — but she reads it. The decodable did what it was supposed to do. She was still doing sand letters and pink words. The decodable was the thing her particular nervous system needed in that moment.

11
Mid-February · a different child · a harder call

A strong reader. Moving
through the blue series
with ease.

Someone suggests he would benefit from a structured decodable sequence — a digital tool that tracks his progress, assigns points, moves him through phonetic patterns methodically.

What would you gain? Efficiency. Tracking. Data. The appearance of rigor.

What would you lose? The choice that defines Montessori reading. The child's agency in what to read. His connection to meaningful stories. His trajectory toward the library of the world.

He is not your patient. The first child was.

12
The equity provocation · when a bridge becomes a gate

In many schools, decodable
texts are not a bridge.
They are how you track children.

You tell a family their child is reading at a "Level 2" when what you actually mean is that her phonological processing is developing at an expected pace but her sight-word bank is small. That language problem belongs to the system — not the child.

It serves people with power. It lets you sort. It lets you tell some families "advanced" and others "behind."

The moment you turn decodable texts into a leveling system, you have broken the Montessori model. You have turned a support tool into a gatekeeping one.

13
A neurodivergence note · this is access work

Dyslexia.
Processing
speed
differences.

Some children, particularly those with dyslexia or processing-speed differences, will benefit from decodable texts in ways that are simply not available through the pink and blue series alone.

This is not a philosophical compromise. This is an equity tool. It is providing access to a child whose nervous system is doing real phonological work and deserves a material that meets it.

14
Closing

Take this into the week

trying to implement?
