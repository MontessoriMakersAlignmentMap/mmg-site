Residency · Week Nine

Ready — not old enough.

How to know when a child is ready to move on. Two different questions, and only one of them is answered by her birthday.

Montessori Makers Residency Week 09 · Primary
Opening

You can teach a child
the sensorial materials
at three years old.
You will break them.

The question is not whether she is old enough. The question is whether she is ready.

Those are two completely different things — and this week is observation work at its most essential.

02
Why this, why now

Read the child
in the present moment.

Two months into observation. Patterns are starting to surface. Some children are about to finish Practical Life and transition into Sensorial.

This week is not about scope and sequence. It is about reading a child now — and making a decision that honors both her development and her capacity.

03
— Part One —

What readiness
is not.

Stripping away three of the most common substitutes — age, compliance, and interest.

Readiness, defined

Not readiness to sit still.
Not readiness to follow directions.

Readiness is the neural organization, the impulse control, the fine motor foundation, and the genuine curiosity to engage deeply with something new.

It shows up as quiet competence — not as being fastest or most accurate. It shows up as a child who can make an error and adjust without falling apart.

05
The confusion I see most often

Interest and readiness
are not the same thing.

— Interest alone —

A four-year-old is desperately interested in writing.

She sees older children writing. She wants in. Her interest is real. Her readiness may not be.

Hand strength is not there. Phonetic foundation is not there. You can force her hand through the motions. She will not be ready.

— Interest meeting readiness —

Repeated return. Focus. Self-direction.

She asks for the color tablets. She returns to them for weeks. Not because you suggested it. Not because it comes next in the sequence.

She returns because something in her development is calling her toward that work. That is the signal.

06
Both errors are real

Moving her too early is as
harmful as holding her
back too long — for different reasons.

Too early: she cannot succeed. She begins to depend on you. Moves from material to material because nothing holds her. The lesson she learns: I need an adult to work.

Too late: she has already integrated the learning. She makes a game of the work. By month five she has checked out. The lesson: school is compliance and repetition, not real pursuit.

07
— Part Two —

Three things
you are watching for.

The hand. The concentration. The relationship to difficulty.

The three lenses

Together, over time —
they tell you if a child
is ready.

01
— The hand —

Does she place — or slap and grab?

Can she manipulate objects with care? When she makes an error, does she notice? Can she make the correction herself?

02
— The concentration —

Can she return? Twenty minutes without a prompt?

Is she able to come back to an activity? When she is done, does she reset the material — or leave it in pieces?

03
— Difficulty —

Does she persevere?

When you give her help, can she use it and return to work? Or does she need you to repeat the help every time she hits the same edge?

09
Beyond the three lenses · the quiet signals

Six smaller signals you can
only see if you are looking for them.

i.
She chooses her own work without scanning the room for an adult's reaction.
ii.
She carries the material with two hands and walks slowly — without being reminded.
iii.
She watches an older child work for several minutes, then tries it herself days later.
iv.
She accepts redirection without it costing her the rest of the morning.
v.
She makes tiny self-corrections mid-work — without commentary, without seeking praise.
vi.
When she finishes, she sits back. There is satisfaction. She does not immediately need the next thing.
10
A three-year-old at the color tablets with the guide beside her — hand steady on the work, eyes flicking up to the guide's face between each move.
Scene · readiness the guide missed

Physical readiness.
Nervous system still catching up.

On paper, she was ready. Good hand control. She returned to activities. She could concentrate.

But she was still working through anxiety about transitions. The work required abstract thinking — so she needed reassurance. She became dependent on it. It took weeks to rebuild. Observe the whole ecosystem, not just hands and focus.

11
Scene · readiness the guide saw — and waited on

The other error.
She was ready in October.

Hand strength: there. Concentration: thirty minutes at a stretch. She had begun watching the older children at the pink tower. The guide wanted to be sure.

By December, the child stopped watching. By January, she would take the pink tower out and arrange it as a wall, a road, a fence. The work had been waiting too long. Her interest passed before her invitation came.

A four-year-old in the corner with the pink tower laid horizontally across the rug — turned into something that is not the pink tower at all. She is humming.
12
The equity provocation

If you only look for verbal markers,
you will miss the quiet child entirely.

In many schools, the children moved forward quickly are already advantaged. Their families talk to them, read to them, take them places. Guides read cultural familiarity and verbal fluency as readiness. They are not the same.

A quiet child may be deeply ready — and invisible. A child whose family has not done the bedtime reading routine may have extraordinary hand control and focus. Watch across the whole child — across time — or you will over-represent the same children in advanced work every year.

13
A note on different profiles

Watch the child.
Not the profile.

A child with ADHD may have excellent hand control and deep interest in sorting — with fragmented concentration. A child who is autistic may have intense focus, and rigid thinking.

A child with early trauma may have excellent behavior and be checking out emotionally. Compliance is not readiness. Give these children time. Observe closely. Readiness exists for them too — and may look different.

14
— Part Three —

What you do
with what you see.

The decision moment. Held lightly. Made anyway.

A simple frame for the decision

When the signals are mixed —
how you act on them.

— Interest present —
— Interest absent —
Readiness present

Invite. Offer the lesson within the week. Step back and let the work do the rest.

Wait — and prepare the ground. Place the next material visibly nearby. Let her notice it on her terms.

Readiness absent

Strengthen the foundation. Stay in earlier work she can succeed at. Honor the interest by showing it is on the path.

Observe more. Do not move. Do not push. Look for what is calling her — somewhere else in the room.

16
A note on your own pressure

Be honest about whose readiness
you are reading.

The pressure to move children forward is often your pressure — not theirs. The parents asked. The director glanced at the binder. The other guide moved her child.

These are real pressures. They are not data about the child. Name them quietly to yourself, set them aside, and look again at what she is actually showing you. Her timeline is not your performance review.

17
Closing

Take this into the week

Choose one child who is showing
signals of readiness. What is she
showing you? What are you
still uncertain about?
