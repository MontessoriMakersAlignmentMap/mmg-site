The most important
skill of a
Montessori guide
is not presenting materials.
It is noticing children.

Observation — the careful, written, non-judgmental recording of what a child is doing — is the engine of every decision you will make all year. Without it, you are guessing. With it, you are responding to an actual person.

The first discipline · observation is not interpretation

What you write down
must be what you saw.

Observation

“Maya worked at the pink tower for 18 minutes. Built it three times. The second time, the cube order was reversed. She paused, disassembled, rebuilt correctly.”

Inference (not observation)

“Maya was frustrated with the pink tower today. She’s being a perfectionist again. Probably tired.”

The left column is evidence. It can be returned to, compared, shared. The right column is a story you told yourself about the evidence — and it will bias every decision that follows. The first discipline of observation is telling these two apart.

Nothing dramatic. That is the point. A short log, written in the moment, repeated daily, becomes — over months — a record of a child becoming who she is.

03
How to observe · the stool in the corner

Sit down. Be boring.
Do not make eye
contact. Do not help.

A small stool, placed in the quietest corner of the room. A notebook. A pen. You become a piece of furniture for twenty minutes. You do not intervene unless a child is in danger. You do not respond to eye contact beyond a small smile. The children learn — quickly — that this stool means you are not available. And then, they get on with their lives.

What you witness in those twenty minutes is the classroom as it actually is when you are not directing it. That is the classroom you are preparing for. Not the one that behaves when you are walking through it.

04
A small wooden stool in the corner of a primary classroom. The guide sits on it with a bound notebook open on her lap, writing quickly with a pencil, her face calm and attentive. She is not looking at the page. She is looking across the room at a child working with the long rods. The child — absorbed, unaware of the watching — is laying rods end to end along a blue mat.
Scene · writing without looking down

She is writing
without looking
at the page.

Because looking down would miss the next moment. Her handwriting will be bad. The observation will be good.

She will transcribe this evening. She will clean up the notation. But the evidence was captured in real time — and that is what matters. You cannot re-observe a morning.

05
What observations are for

Three uses.
Presentation decisions.
Parent conferences.
Catching yourself.

Presentation decisions. Which child gets which lesson next week, and why. If a child has been working steadily with the spindle boxes for ten days, the teen boards are next. Observation is the evidence that tells you so.

Parent conferences. Not “he’s doing great” but “here is what he has worked on for the past six weeks, in his own choice, for this many minutes.” Evidence turns conferences into partnerships.

Catching yourself. Which children appear in your notes every day? Which ones almost never? Your observation log is a mirror of your attention. And your attention is not evenly distributed.

06
The equity provocation · who is unseen

Every Friday, open
your notebook and
find who is missing.

There will be children who do not appear in the week’s observations. Quiet children. Children who disappear into the middle of the room. Children who do not demand attention and so do not receive it. The unseen child is the equity problem.

Next Monday, put her name at the top of your notebook page. Observe her first. The act of naming her corrects the drift of your attention.
