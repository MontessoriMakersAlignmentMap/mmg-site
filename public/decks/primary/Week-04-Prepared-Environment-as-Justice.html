Residency · Week Four

The prepared environment
as a justice issue.

Every object in your classroom is speaking to children. Every object.

Montessori Makers Residency Week 04 · Primary
Opening

Every object is
saying something.

About who belongs. About what matters. About whose world this is. About whether this place was prepared for this particular child — or whether this child is supposed to fit into a place that was prepared for someone else.

02
Why this, why now

Theory meets
reality.

You are at the moment of building a classroom from the ground up, or inheriting one. You are deciding what goes on the shelf, what goes on the walls, what height the furniture is, what books are available, what the light is like.

These seem like design decisions. They are actually justice decisions.

Most Montessori classrooms are not prepared for every child in them. They are prepared for a particular kind of child — and everyone else has to adapt.

03
— PART ONE —

Not beautiful.
Intentional.

What Montessori meant by "prepared" — and what most guides hear instead.

A definition, carefully

Montessori, not as decor

Carefully designed
around the
children in this room.

Not around the preferences of adults. Not around the limitations of the budget. Not around the echoes of how Montessori classrooms have always looked.

An environment that communicates to children, through every available channel, that they belong here and that they are capable.

"Prepared" is not an aesthetic. It is a question: for whom?

05
Neurological, not optional

A child whose feet do not
touch the floor is dysregulated.

The proprioceptive feedback a child gets from having her feet on the ground matters for focus, for calm, for the ability to do careful work. Furniture sized for adults teaches a child something very specific: this space was not made for me.

This is a justice issue. Families with money can buy child-sized furniture. If you inherited adult tables, you figure it out — stools, cushions, used furniture, the community. You do not "live with it" because it is complicated.

06
The walls teach

Bare is
quiet.

A classroom with bare walls is neurologically quieter. Children with sensory sensitivities are calmer. Children with attention challenges can focus longer. But bare can also say no one put care into making this beautiful.

The balance: what is on the walls should be meaningful, changing with the work, at child eye-level.

A photograph of the children working is not the same as a picture from a magazine. A child-made book is not the same as a purchased chart. A beautiful artwork at child height is not the same as a commercial poster telling children what to do.

07
— PART TWO —

Representation
is not decoration.

It is the answer to: whose world is shown in this room?

Whose world is shown

Even if you are the warmest guide in the world — the walls are saying something different.

In most Montessori classrooms, the pictures, the books, the materials showing family structures and professions show primarily white families, primarily middle-class, primarily able-bodied, primarily heterosexual partnerships, primarily people who look a certain way.

A child who does not see her family structure, her race, a body like hers, a job like the job in her family — learns that her world is not visible here. Her world does not count.

09
The materials tell a story

Your world map.
Your color box.
Your "skin tone" markers.

They are teaching a story about what is normal. What is beautiful. What is real.

If your color box is rooted in a Eurocentric understanding of color mixing; if your map names countries in a way that reflects colonialism; if your "skin tone" markers are actually only pale pink — these are not small details.

They are the content of the environment. They are what children absorb.

10
Walk your own room

Get down on the floor.
See the room from a child's eye.

—

Whose face is on the wall?

Photographs of the actual children in this room, or generic commercial imagery? Are the families shown in your books the families sitting in your circle?

—

Whose home is in the Practical Life?

Dusting silver, or caring for a small apartment? Folding linens, or braiding hair? What does the shelf name as real work?

—

What is broken, and what do you do with it?

The doll with a missing arm. The chipped tray. The material in the locked cabinet. Every one of these is teaching.

11
A Montessori classroom, soft pastels, perfectly arranged shelves, new materials, walls with commercial posters. Morning light. Nothing specific about the children in it.
Scene one

Beautiful in a
generic way.

The shelves are perfectly arranged. The colors are soft pastels. The materials look like they cost a lot of money. But when you look closer, the children in the photographs are mostly white. The family structures are all nuclear. The skin tones in the art materials are limited.

A transgender child, a child from an immigrant family, a child whose parents are incarcerated — will walk in and experience it as not prepared for them. The beauty is being withheld.

12
Scene two

Beautiful for
these children.

Books by and about people of color. A Braille label beside the printed one because a blind child is in this room. Snack preparation materials for different ways of eating. Markers in many skin tones because these children will paint themselves.

The materials show professions the children's parents actually do. The photographs on the wall are of the children in this room working.

A Montessori classroom whose shelves, walls, and books specifically reflect the children in this community. Photographs of the actual children. Natural light.
13
Whose beauty

Beauty is not one thing.
It is multiple things.

— Order and —

warmth.

— Intentional and —

visibly lived in.

— Calm and —

vibrant.

A Scandinavian-minimalist beauty can read as cold to a child from a home with color and pattern. An austere order can overwhelm a child whose home is chaotic. The aesthetic you choose is communicating whose aesthetic belongs here.

14
Accessibility is for everyone

A ramp is not
only for the wheelchair.

A ramp is easier for everyone — the child pushing a cart, the guide moving materials, the child learning how bodies move through space. Sign language is beautiful, it is communication, it is part of the community. A Braille label beside the printed label means touching and reading go together — that there is more than one way to know something.

If you design only for the supposed "typical" child, you end up with an environment that does not actually work for anyone.

15
What you keep, what you fix

What you keep
communicates
your values.

A material can be old and worn and still worth using — if it is clearly being cared for, if the wear is a sign of love, if the guide maintains it with intention.

A material that is broken and ignored teaches that some things do not matter, that some work is not worth the effort to maintain.

When something breaks, the guide sits with the child who broke it. They get the tools. They fix it together. The message: we care for what is ours. We do not throw it away.

16
The equity provocation

An environment that is not
prepared for every child
is not a prepared environment.

It is a prepared environment for some children, and a place where other children have to make themselves smaller. That is not a neutral choice. Every guide makes this choice every day.

You cannot fix this by being nice. You cannot fix this by saying the right things. You have to change the environment.

17
Closing

Take this into the week

Picture one child whose face,
whose family, whose real life
is not anywhere in your room.

What would she absorb by being in this space, day after day — and what would you change so she knows this room was prepared for her?
