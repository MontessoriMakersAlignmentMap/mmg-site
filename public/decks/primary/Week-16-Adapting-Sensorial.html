02
Why this, why now

End of the
Sensorial unit.
Now it gets
practical.

Some children cannot see the color tablets. Some cannot grip the knobbed cylinders. Some process visual information differently. Some need movement. Some need shorter doses.

The sensorial thinking is what matters — discrimination, acuity, noticing difference. The pathway in is yours to build. This week is the concrete version.

03
— Part One —

Five profiles.
Five pathways.

The sensorial learning is intact in every one. The door in is what changes.

At a glance · what we are adapting for

The same material
has many front doors.

01

Visual impairment.

Significant loss, low vision, or color blindness. Touch and proprioception carry the discrimination when sight cannot.

02

Motor differences.

Cerebral palsy, low tone, tremor, dyspraxia. Separate the essential motor act from the incidental one.

03

Attention profiles.

Short bursts. Movement-integrated. Novelty-seeking. Dose and format change. Learning is the same.

04

Tactile sensitivities.

Defensiveness and seeking — often in the same child. Honor the preference. Never push touch.

05

Non-speaking and language-different.

Assessment happens through action, gesture, and AAC. The sensorial discrimination does not require speech.

05
Profile one · visual impairment

The work was never
about vision.
It was about discrimination.

— What becomes essential —

Touch and weight
carry the work.

— Baric tablets, weights, texture board become the first materials, not the last.

— Knobbed cylinders and pink tower are explored through proprioception — the hand knows size.

— Sensorial work extends into the room: fabrics, temperatures, weights of real objects.

— The sensory channel is different. The learning is the same. —
— For low vision and color blindness —

Increase the
contrast. Change
the axis.

— Larger tablets. High-contrast grading — black-to-white instead of subtle hues.

— Pair visual with tactile: she sees the form and feels it.

— For color blindness — sort by warmth or coolness, not by hue. Baric tablets, graded weights.

— You have not lowered the standard. You have widened the access. —
06
Profile two · motor differences

Is the motor act essential
to the learning — or just
incidental to the material?

— If the motor demand is incidental —

Remove it.
Keep the learning.

— Pink tower teaches size, not stacking. Order the blocks on a mat.

— Knobbed cylinders teach size in three dimensions — place them on a mat in order. Modify the knobs.

— Pointing counts. "Which comes next?" is the assessment.

— Same discrimination. Zero motor barrier. —
— If the motor act is the point —

Adapt the
demand. Keep the
act.

— Can't hold a pencil? Finger-trace the sandpaper letters.

— Can't lift the brown stair? Build the same grading in foam or plastic.

— Low tone may need the weighted work. Keep it. Modify the grip, not the material.

— The sensorial learning and the motor development both happen. —
07
Profile three · attention and engagement

Stop thinking of it
as a lesson period.
Think of it as a skill.

— Shorter doses —

Three minutes.
Then three more.

— Three minutes with color tablets, three with grading bells, three with texture. Same total. Different format.

— Over a week, all the material has been encountered. In bursts the nervous system can hold.

— The dose changes. The learning does not. —
— Movement and novelty —

Let the work
go through the room.

— Instead of matching colors at a mat, search the classroom for objects that match.

— Walk the line carrying a graded block. Skip while finding the medium-sized cylinder.

— The sensorial discrimination is real. You have added the movement her nervous system needed.

— Not filler. Fuel. —
08
Profile four · tactile sensitivities

Her nervous system
is not broken.
It is specific.

— Honor first —

Do not force
the touch.
Ever.

— Find the materials that are tolerable. Build from there.

— Swap textures — velvet or felt in place of sandpaper. Gloves for the brown stair.

— Extend into Practical Life: pouring, sorting, cleaning — discrimination through more tolerable input.

— The challenge stays. The distress goes. —
— If tolerance begins to widen —

Let it.
Do not accelerate it.

— You touch the sandpaper. She watches. She listens. Weeks pass.

— She touches the top of one letter. Weeks later, the whole letter.

— Never push. Follow the nervous system. The door opens on its schedule, not yours.

— Sensory defensiveness AND sensory seeking can live in the same child. —
09
Profile five · non-speaking and language-different children

The full Sensorial
curriculum — without
a single spoken word.

— Assessment through action —

She points.
She grades.
She shows.

— The sensorial discrimination happens with or without language. A child matches colors without ever speaking "red."

— Pair with visual supports — a picture of the color, a symbol. Input without required output.

— AAC, if she uses it, carries naming. The channel changes. The learning does not.

— The sensorial work does not require speech. —
— When the classroom language is not hers —

Demonstrate
more. Instruct
less.

— She watches you touch the textures. She watches you grade. Then she tries.

— Sensorial understanding develops through observation and experimentation — not only through words.

— Language follows demonstration. Name as she explores, without asking her to repeat.

— Observation is a curriculum. —

11
— Part Two —

When to adapt.
When not to skip.

Is the material truly inaccessible — or are you avoiding presenting it because you think it will be hard?

The one question · before you skip anything

Two paths look similar.
One is adaptation.
The other is the adult's limit.

— The honest adaptation —

Truly
inaccessible in this format.

You have tried. The motor demand, the sensory demand, or the cognitive load genuinely bars this child from the material as it stands.

— You adapt. The learning is essential; the format is not.

— The adult's fear —

You are
avoiding presenting it.

Because it feels hard. Because you are anxious she will struggle. Because you are not sure how to support her. This is your limit, not hers.

— You adapt your preparation. Then you present.

13
The guiding principle

If the sensorial learning
is essential — you find a
way to access it.

If the specific material is essential — you adapt the access. If the material is merely the usual pathway — you find another. You do not skip sensorial work because a child is different.

You ask: what is this child's pathway into sensorial thinking? And then you build it.

14
The equity provocation

If one child cannot access
the standard curriculum —
the curriculum was designed
without her in mind.

Every adaptation is an acknowledgment that your original design excluded someone. That is not a failure — it is an invitation. Adapt for this child. And then think about how to design better, so the next child does not need adaptation.

That is equity work. It begins with the room as you already prepared it.

15
A way to look — starting tomorrow

Three questions
for one child.

First — what is this child's best sensorial channel? Touch, weight, sound, movement, proprioception?

Second — is the motor demand of this material essential to the learning — or just the usual way the material is presented?

Third — what is one concrete adaptation I can make today that opens this material to this child?
