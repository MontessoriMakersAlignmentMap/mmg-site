Residency · Week Six

Grace & courtesy
as anti-bias curriculum.

There is no culturally neutral way to greet another human being.

Montessori Makers Residency Week 06 · Primary
Opening

The question is not whether
you are teaching culture.

The moment you teach a child that the correct way to say hello is eye contact, you have made a cultural choice. That interrupting is rude — a cultural choice. That please and thank you are the markers of kindness — a cultural choice.

The question is whose culture you are teaching, and whether you are doing it consciously.

02
Why this, why now

The nice stuff is actually
the heavy stuff.

Grace and Courtesy is often treated as nice: how to enter a room, how to ask for a turn, how to speak softly. Many guides touch it lightly and move on.

But this is where you either build an anti-bias curriculum — or quietly reinforce the dominant one. Where you either tell children that their way of being is right, or wrong. Where you either expand what counts as grace, or narrow it.

You have children in your room whose home culture is being judged every single day. You can stop that — or you can reinforce it by teaching only one way to be right.

03
— PART ONE —

Three "universal"
rules.

None of them are universal. All of them are taught.

What we call courtesy

Each of these was taught to a child somewhere as disrespect.

— Eye contact —

Respectful attention to some.

and, for others

Disrespect to an elder.

— Wait your turn —

Polite pacing to some.

and, for others

Cold. Disengaged. Unmoved.

— Personal space —

A respected boundary to some.

and, for others

Distance where closeness means love.

05
Eye contact

"Look at the person
who is speaking to you."

This is considered universally polite. In many cultures, direct eye contact with an authority figure — or with someone older than you — is considered disrespectful.

A child from those cultures is being taught implicitly that the way her family taught her to show respect is actually rude. That is not a small thing. That is a daily message about whose culture belongs in school.

06
Interrupting

Overlap is
engagement.

We teach that interrupting is rude. Raise your hand. Wait to be called on. We present this as universal courtesy.

But in many cultural communities, overlapping speech is how you show engagement and enthusiasm. It is how you demonstrate that you are listening and that you care about what is being said.

To silence a child who participates that way is to teach her that her voice is a problem.

07
Personal space

Closeness is how some
families show love.

Consent and boundaries matter. And — some children come from cultures where physical closeness is how you show care and connection. Where standing close is normal. Where arm-linking or hand-holding is how friends relate to each other.

When we teach the dominant culture's interpretation of appropriate touch, we are teaching some children that the way their family shows love is wrong.

08
— PART TWO —

Three jobs of a
Grace and Courtesy lesson.

Skills. Expansion. Practice — not judgment.

Job one — real skills

01 · Skills that travel

How to live together,
peacefully.

How to ask for what you need. How to respond when someone has done something that bothered you. How to enter a space without disrupting others.

These are real skills. They travel across cultures.

10
Job two — expand, don't narrow

Not the one right way.
Many ways of being respectful.

You are not teaching the one right way to speak or to move or to show respect. You are building a classroom where many ways of being respectful are recognized.

"In this classroom we have people from many cultures, so we are all learning what helps us understand each other." — then build those bridges together.

11
Job three — practice, not judgment

She does not get
told she is
wrong.

A child practices the movement of pushing in her chair quietly. She doesn't get told she did it wrong because she pushed too fast. She practices until her body learns.

Same with social skills. She practices different ways of joining a group. Different ways of asking for help. She discovers what works through experience, not through being told what is right.

12
Two scripts for the same moment

A child raises her hand
while another is still speaking.

— The correction —

"That's rude. We listen without interrupting."

The message, whether intended or not: your impulse is wrong. Your way of showing excitement doesn't fit here.

— The anti-bias move —

"I notice you're excited to share. In this classroom, we take turns so everyone gets heard. I'll make sure you get a turn."

Same skill. Different message about who she is. No shame.

13
A five-year-old boy in mixed-age circle, hand up before the teacher finishes the question, eyes bright with excitement, already starting to talk.
Scene · he already knows

Same skill.
Two different
messages.

After circle, the anti-bias teacher sits with him. Tells him she loves how much he wants to share. Practices waiting with him. Notices the next time he waits: "I noticed you raised your hand and waited. That took strength."

He is learning a skill that helps him be understood here — while his home way of being is still valued.

14
Scene · closeness and comfort

No one is wrong.
Everyone learns.

Two children stand close, shoulders touching, working together. A third child keeps stepping back.

"People in different families stand different distances when they are talking. That is normal. If someone steps back, that tells us they need a little more space right now — and that is okay. We both get to have what we need."

Three first-graders working together. Two stand close, one steps back slightly — a quiet negotiation of space between bodies that grew up with different norms.
15
The equity provocation

Every correction is a
judgment about a culture.

You might think you are teaching manners. Compliance. How to be a good student. But culture is embedded in all of it. And if the only ways you accept are dominant-culture ways, you are teaching some children that they must erase part of themselves to be successful here.

The more unconsciously you do this, the more you sort children. By third grade it has real consequences for how they see themselves as learners. That is not inevitable. That is a choice.

16
A note on every way of being social

Different starting point.
Same work.

Children with autism, ADHD, and other neurodivergent profiles may process eye contact, social distance, and turn-taking differently — without judgment or malice.

A child who does not make eye contact may show attention by listening carefully. A child who talks a lot might practice pausing after she speaks. Not because it is wrong — because it helps her be understood.

17
Closing

Take this into the week

If a child was corrected for how
her family taught her to show respect — whose culture
would you be defending?
