02
Why this, why now

Six months in.
Some children
are still not
reading.

Not lazy. Not unmotivated. Often the most determined children in the room — working harder with pink words than any child you have ever seen.

They do not need more Montessori. They need something different. Explicit, sequential instruction in phonological processing — in a way that does not become a separate program running alongside everything else.

03
— Part One —

Implicit. Explicit.
Both are
real reading
instruction.

Montessori language is implicitly phonetic. For most children, that works. For some, the brain needs the information made explicit.

Two approaches · neither is un-Montessori

Same underlying phonetics.
Different delivery to the brain.

— Implicit (Montessori core) —

The child extracts the pattern.

No rule is stated. The child handles the sandpaper letter and learns the sound. Works through pink words. Her brain builds the rule from enough repetitions.

Works beautifully for children whose systems are wired to predict pattern from repetition. They transfer it to reading without being told.

— The brain generalizes. —
— Explicit (structured literacy) —

The teacher names the pattern.

“The letter a makes the short a sound. When you put it with c and t, you get cat.” Not a rule to memorize — information her brain needs to organize the pattern.

For some children, implicit exposure alone does not consolidate. Their phonological processing systems require the information stated outright.

— The brain receives the map. —
05
A line to hold in the chest

Montessori is not about
doing things one specific way.

Montessori is about following the child and using the prepared environment to meet her where she is. The prepared environment includes pink words and sandpaper letters.

It also includes whatever other instruction this particular child needs in order to read.

Explicit phonological support is not a departure. It is responsive Montessori — in a classroom where the rest of her day still is.

— Part Two —

The signs
you are
watching for.

By month four or five, you have a picture of each child's trajectory. Three patterns tell you a child may need explicit support. You do not wait for formal assessment.

Three patterns · observed over weeks · not a single moment

The knowledge is there. The
transfer is not happening.

01

The gap between knowing and reading.

She can complete pink word sequences. Tell you the sounds. Blend in isolation. But when she looks at a book, there is a delay.

— The compartment is not connecting. —
02

The forgetting of sounds she learned.

Sounds she had weeks ago — gone today. She re-learns them. They fade again. The pattern is not consolidating from repetition.

— Exposure is not enough for her. —
03

The conscious sequencing that will not fade.

She still sounds out familiar words one letter at a time. Months in. The pattern recognition that should be automatic by now — isn’t.

— Automaticity is not arriving. —
08
The research is unambiguous

Early explicit
intervention changes
the trajectory.

Children who receive structured literacy support in kindergarten or first grade do not remain behind. They catch up — because their brain finally has the explicit map it needed to organize the implicit patterns.

You identify these children through consistent observation — not a formal assessment, not a diagnosis, not a frustration event. You observe early and you respond.

09
— Part Three —

What her
day
looks like.

Ten or fifteen minutes of explicit phonological work. The rest of her day is still Montessori. Not two programs. One classroom — responsive.

What structured literacy looks like inside a Montessori morning

Not a pull-out program.
A small piece of a whole morning.

Morning arrival
She chooses her first work. The classroom continues exactly as it does for every other child. No exception. No separation.
Ten minutes
Small group · three children · explicit phonological instruction. You sit with her and two others. Short a, direct teaching, blending, decodable practice matched to the explicit work. Twenty minutes, three days a week.
Back to the floor
She returns to the classroom. Sandpaper letters. Pink words. Moveable alphabet. The rich language environment continues. The small group is a layer — not a replacement.
Choice and reading
She chooses what to read. She reads books with meaning. She works across every strand. What shifts over weeks is that the explicit instruction gives her brain the map it needed — and the Montessori work starts to cohere.

“I am going to teach

A year in Montessori. Bright. Loves the classroom. Still not reading fluently. Pink words solid in isolation — inconsistent on the page.

12
The equity provocation · who has to be diagnosed to get this

In many schools, explicit
structured literacy is
available only to children
with a formal diagnosis.

Families with resources get evaluations. Families without those resources do not. The instruction that could change a child’s trajectory becomes a privilege — granted or withheld by what a family can afford.

In a Montessori classroom, the equity model is different. You observe all children. You provide the explicit support to any child whose neurology shows you she needs it — regardless of whether she has a diagnosis.

This is what equity looks like in reading instruction. Not a philosophical compromise. A justice issue.

13
A neurodivergence note · essential, not optional

Dyslexia.
Auditory processing
differences.
Executive function
challenges.

Children with these profiles may need explicit structured literacy instruction in order to develop fluent reading.

This is not optional. This is essential. Withholding it in the name of philosophical purity is withholding reading itself.
