Montessori Makers Residency Week 23 · Primary
Opening · a sentence to sit with

The decimal system
in Montessori is
not an introduction
to place-value notation.

It is an introduction to
the nature of quantity.

02
Why this, why now

You are about
to do the
first presentation
of the
decimal system.

It is not quick. It is not linear. It takes weeks. It looks inefficient if you measure mathematics by speed.

If you understand what you are actually teaching — the mathematical mind itself — then the time is not lost. It is the whole point.

03
— Part One —

Quantity
before
symbol.

This is where Montessori diverges from nearly every math curriculum in the world. Not preference. Neurological necessity.

Two orders of operation · the order matters

Most of the world teaches
symbol first. Montessori teaches quantity.

— Symbol first —

“This is three. It looks like 3.”

symbol → word → (maybe) quantity

The child memorizes the link between the symbol and the word. But has she constructed the concept of three?

Children taught this way often reverse numbers. Confuse them. Memorize facts without understanding. The concept was never built from the ground up.

— Quantity first —

“This is three.” — and she is holding three beads.

quantity → word → (weeks later) symbol

She counts out one bead. Another. Another. She understands through her body. Three exists in her as an actual amount — not a memorized mark.

The symbol, when it arrives, is written notation for something her hand already knows. It sticks. It transfers.

05
— Part Two —

One. Ten. Hundred.
Thousand.

The same relationship, repeating at every level. The child discovers a pattern — not a set of rules. The logic of our number system is the shape of the material.

The hierarchy · what her hand sees

Ten ones gathered becomes
one ten. Ten tens becomes one hundred.
The same relationship. Every level.

One · 1

A unit.

One bead. One amount. The beginning of counting.

— “this is one” —
Ten · 10

Ten ones in a row.

The bar. Same amount as ten loose beads. Her hand feels the equivalence.

— “ten ones, gathered” —
Hundred · 100

Ten bars side by side.

The square. Same as a hundred loose ones. The same pattern, one level up.

— “ten tens, gathered” —
Thousand · 1,000

Ten squares stacked.

The cube. A hundred, ten times. The same relationship, again.

07
A five-year-old is doing extraordinarily sophisticated thinking

Once she grasps the
pattern — she can
predict what
comes next.

“If ten tens makes a hundred — then ten hundreds must make something.” The child derives the logic herself.

But she does not experience it as sophisticated. She experiences it as obvious. Of course ten ones gathered together is called a ten. Her hand and her mind have shown her that this is simply how quantity organizes itself.

She is not being told the rule. She is discovering the structure of our number system through repeated handling of quantity.

08
— Part Three —

The body
knows before
the mind can name.

You will watch a child do the work correctly, without being able to explain it. She is not confused. She simply does not yet know through language what her hand has known for weeks.

A first-grader seated on a green mat on the floor. On the mat: a tray of loose golden beads, a ten-bar, and a golden-bead square. Her finger is resting on the ten-bar while her eyes flick to the loose beads beside it — comparing, measuring, checking her understanding against the material.
Scene · first week of mathematics · six years

Something is organizing
itself in her mind.

The guide shows her a bar. “Ten ones, gathered together. We call this a bar. It is the same amount as all these beads.” The child looks at the loose beads. She looks at the bar. She does not speak. Her hand is doing the work of comparison.

For the next week she returns. Gathers. Exchanges. Builds. Place value is not being told to her. It is being discovered — through careful, repeated work with material reality.

10
Mid-February · three weeks in · the symbol is introduced

The numeral 1 0.
The zero makes sense.

“This is how we write ten.” She already knows what ten is — she has been gathering it for weeks. She sees the numeral. She understands the zero as a placeholder — because her hand has already shown her that when she has one ten, the ones column is empty.

The symbol is never mysterious. It is always attached to something she understands deeply. Over the next weeks she will move between the beads and the numerals — but never away from what the beads taught her first.

11
What she is also building, underneath the math

She is learning
that numbers are
not mysterious.

She is learning that numbers are logical. They follow rules she can see. They are manipulable. She is learning that she can understand mathematics.

A child who built her understanding through sensorial experience becomes a child who trusts her own mathematical thinking. She does not panic when a problem is hard. She thinks: “I do not understand this yet. But I can figure it out the way I figured out the golden beads.”

12
The equity provocation · who loses when we rush

The slowness of the
golden beads is
exactly why it is
an equity tool.

Children with strong verbal support at home can sometimes make mathematics work by memorizing the symbol system. Children without that support cannot. They need to understand. They need to build the concept. They need time with the material.

The moment you rush the decimal system — compress it into a unit, cover it in a week — you are choosing which children have access to deep mathematical understanding and which are left memorizing.

13
A neurodivergence note · concrete before symbolic

Spatial
reasoning.
Processing
speed
variations.

Children with spatial-reasoning differences or processing-speed variations often thrive with the golden beads. The material is concrete. The repetition is adjustable. The pace can match the child.

The decimal system introduction should never be accelerated to match a timeline. It should be matched to the child.

14
Closing

Take this into the week

When you introduce the
decimal system to a child —
are you introducing a
